## Opening: The Gap

Not a narrative scene. A data-driven opening that presents the gap between current trajectories and what published roadmaps show is necessary.

**Content notes:**
- Open with the IEA Net Zero by 2050 Roadmap's key finding: the path to 1.5°C remains technically feasible but the window is closing rapidly
- Present side-by-side: "where we are" vs. "where we need to be" using IEA NZE milestones
- Frame as: "what the evidence shows is possible" versus "what is required" — avoid speculative optimism or doom framing
- Acknowledge the gap honestly: current policies lead to ~2.5–2.9°C by 2100 (UNEP Emissions Gap Report); the NZE pathway requires a fundamentally different pace of action
- Key tension: the technologies largely exist, the economics increasingly favor clean energy, but the speed and scale of deployment remain inadequate

**Key data points:**
- IEA NZE scenario: global CO2 emissions must fall ~35% by 2030 from 2022 levels
- Current trajectory vs. NZE trajectory divergence (IEA World Energy Outlook 2023)
- Global energy-related CO2 emissions reached ~37.4 GtCO2 in 2023 (IEA)
- Clean energy investment reached ~$1.8T in 2023 — needs to reach ~$4.5T/year by 2030 (IEA NZE)

## Section 1: Technologies Ready for Deployment Now

**Content notes:**
- Solar PV: now the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world; global weighted-average LCOE fell 89% between 2010 and 2022 (IRENA)
- Onshore wind: LCOE fell 69% over the same period; competitive with fossil fuels in most markets
- Electric vehicles: battery pack costs fell from ~$1,200/kWh in 2010 to ~$139/kWh in 2023 (BloombergNEF); approaching price parity with ICE vehicles
- Heat pumps: 3–5x more efficient than gas furnaces; sales surged 11% globally in 2022 (IEA)
- LED lighting, building insulation, industrial efficiency — proven, cost-effective, deployable now
- The common thread: these technologies don't need breakthroughs — they need policy support, investment, supply chain buildout, and workforce training

**Key data points:**
- Solar: ~1,200 GW installed globally by end of 2023; IEA NZE requires ~5,400 GW by 2030
- Wind: ~900 GW installed; NZE requires ~3,000 GW by 2030
- EVs: ~14 million sold in 2023 (~18% of global car sales); NZE target: 60% by 2030
- Heat pump sales: ~40 million units in 2022; need to roughly triple by 2030

**Profiles:**
- IEA and Fatih Birol — role in defining the NZE pathway and shifting the global energy discourse
- IRENA — tracking renewable energy cost declines and deployment data
- BloombergNEF — battery price tracking and EV market analysis

**Source references:**
- IRENA, *Renewable Power Generation Costs in 2022*
- BloombergNEF, *Lithium-Ion Battery Pack Prices* (annual survey)
- IEA, *Global EV Outlook 2024*

## Section 2: Technologies Needing Scale-Up

**Content notes:**
- Green hydrogen: currently ~$4–9/kg; needs to reach ~$1–2/kg to compete with grey hydrogen; electrolysis capacity must scale dramatically; IEA NZE: 70 Mt of low-emission hydrogen production by 2030
- Long-duration energy storage (LDES): beyond lithium-ion (4-hour duration); iron-air, compressed air, flow batteries, gravitational storage; needed for grid reliability at >80% renewable penetration
- Carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS): current global capacity ~45 MtCO2/year; IEA NZE: ~1 GtCO2/year by 2030; role in hard-to-abate sectors (cement, steel); controversy over use in fossil fuel extension
- Direct air capture (DAC): currently ~0.01 MtCO2/year; extremely early stage; costs ~$400–1,000/ton; needed at scale later in century for overshoot scenarios
- Enhanced geothermal: next-generation geothermal could access heat anywhere, not just volcanically active zones; Fervo Energy's results
- Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF): currently <1% of jet fuel; needs massive scale-up for hard-to-electrify aviation

**Key data points:**
- Green hydrogen: ~$4–9/kg production cost (2023); target: $1–2/kg by 2030
- LDES: ~1.5 GW deployed globally; IEA estimates 140+ GW needed by 2040
- CCUS: 45 MtCO2/year capacity (2023); NZE requires ~1 GtCO2/year by 2030
- DAC: 0.01 MtCO2/year captured (2023); 60+ MtCO2/year needed by 2030 in NZE
- Learning curves: every doubling of cumulative production reduces costs ~20–25% for most clean technologies

**Profiles:**
- Fervo Energy — next-generation geothermal demonstration
- Form Energy — iron-air battery development for 100-hour storage
- Climeworks — leading DAC company, Orca and Mammoth plants in Iceland
- Key electrolyzer manufacturers scaling green hydrogen

**Source references:**
- IEA, *Global Hydrogen Review 2023*
- IEA, *CCUS in Clean Energy Transitions* (2023)
- LDES Council, *Net-Zero Power: Long Duration Energy Storage for a Renewable Grid*
- Climeworks operational data and published cost estimates

## Section 3: The 2030 Milestones

**Content notes:**
- Organize around the IEA NZE scenario's key 2030 milestones and assess current progress toward each
- Triple renewable energy capacity globally: from ~3,600 GW (2023) to ~11,000 GW by 2030 — current annual additions ~500 GW; need ~1,000 GW/year
- Double the rate of energy efficiency improvement: current rate ~2%/year; NZE requires ~4%/year
- 60% EV sales share by 2030: currently ~18%; growth rapid but uneven (China leading, other markets lagging)
- Phase down unabated coal: no new unabated coal plants approved; existing fleet retirements must accelerate
- Reduce methane emissions from fossil fuel operations by 75%: Global Methane Pledge signatories; satellite monitoring now enables verification
- Clean energy investment: $4.5T/year by 2030 (from ~$1.8T in 2023)

## Section 5: What the Pathways Show

**Content notes:**
- IPCC SR1.5 and AR6 WGIII pathways: what 1.5°C requires vs. what 2°C requires
- The math: remaining carbon budget for 1.5°C (50% probability): ~250 GtCO2 from start of 2024 (IPCC AR6); at current emissions (~40 GtCO2/year including land use), budget exhausted in ~6 years
- 2°C budget: ~1,150 GtCO2 — more room but still requires rapid decarbonization
- Overshoot scenarios: most 1.5°C pathways now involve temporary overshoot followed by net-negative emissions later in century; implications and risks of overshoot
- The relationship between speed of near-term action and eventual temperature outcome: every year of delay narrows options and increases costs
- The cost comparison: IEA estimates the NZE transition requires $4.5T/year in clean energy investment by 2030, but also projects $12T in fuel cost savings over 2023–2050; McKinsey estimates the net economic opportunity
- Avoid both complacency ("technology will save us") and fatalism ("it's too late") — the evidence shows the outcome depends on choices made in this decade

**Key data points:**
- Remaining carbon budget for 1.5°C (50% probability): ~250 GtCO2 from Jan 2024 (IPCC AR6 WGI)
- Remaining carbon budget for 2°C (67% probability): ~1,150 GtCO2
- 1.5°C pathways: global CO2 must reach net zero by ~2050; global GHG net zero by ~2070
- 2°C pathways: global CO2 net zero by ~2070; more gradual decline
- Cost of inaction: Swiss Re estimates climate change could reduce global GDP by 10–23% by 2100 under higher warming scenarios
- IEA NZE: cumulative fuel savings of ~$12T between 2023 and 2050

**Profiles:**

- Carbon budget researchers (e.g., Pierre Friedlingstein, Global Carbon Project)
