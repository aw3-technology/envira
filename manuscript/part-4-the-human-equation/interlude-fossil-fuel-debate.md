# Interlude: The Debate — How Fast Can We Phase Out Fossil Fuels?

By mid-decade, a fault line had opened in the climate conversation — not between those who accepted the science and those who denied it, but between two camps that agreed on the destination and disagreed, profoundly, on the speed of travel. The question was not whether the world should move away from fossil fuels. The question was how fast. And the answer a person gave revealed not just a policy preference but a set of assumptions about economics, justice, political feasibility, and the tolerance of physical systems for continued loading.

Two voices. Both informed by evidence. Both operating in good faith. Irreconcilable on strategy.

---

**Bill McKibben**, author, journalist, and co-founder of the climate advocacy organization 350.org, has spent four decades arguing that the fossil fuel era must end — and that the principal obstacle to ending it is not technological but political. His position, articulated across dozens of books, essays, and public campaigns, rests on arithmetic that he considers non-negotiable.

The carbon budget for a fifty percent chance of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels was, as of 2023, approximately 250 gigatons of CO2 — roughly six years of current emissions. The budget is a physical constraint, not a policy preference; it derives from the relationship between cumulative emissions and equilibrium temperature, a relationship established by decades of climate science and codified in the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report. Every year of continued fossil fuel combustion at current rates consumes a larger fraction of what remains. There is no scenario, McKibben has argued repeatedly, in which the expansion of fossil fuel production is compatible with the targets that nearly every government on Earth endorsed in Paris in 2015.

The International Energy Agency's own Net Zero by 2050 roadmap, published in May 2021, stated the point with unusual institutional clarity: under a net-zero pathway, no new oil and gas fields are needed beyond those already approved for development. Every new fossil fuel project approved after that date is, in the IEA's framing, a bet against the climate goals the world has set for itself.

McKibben's case does not rely solely on the carbon budget. It rests equally on the economic transformation that has already occurred. Solar electricity is now the cheapest source of new power generation in most of the world. Battery storage costs have fallen by more than 90 percent in a decade. The energy transition is not a sacrifice requiring heroic political will — it is an economic upgrade being delayed by the political power of incumbent industries. The documented record of fossil fuel companies funding disinformation, lobbying against climate legislation, and using the language of gradualism to forestall action is, McKibben argues, the single most important context for any discussion of transition pace. When industry representatives call for a "managed transition," they are, in his view, using reasonable-sounding language to extend the timeline of extraction.

And then there is justice. The communities most harmed by climate change — low-income populations, the Global South, communities of color in industrialized nations — are those with the least historical responsibility for the emissions that caused it. Another decade of incrementalism, McKibben has written, is another decade of asking the most vulnerable to bear the cost of the most powerful's delay.

---

**Fatih Birol**, Executive Director of the International Energy Agency since 2015, occupies a position that requires him to hold two truths simultaneously: the energy transition must accelerate dramatically, and the world still runs on fossil fuels. His perspective, expressed through the IEA's annual World Energy Outlook and in interviews and public statements, begins from a fact that McKibben's framing does not dispute but that Birol places at the center of his analysis.

As of 2023, fossil fuels supplied roughly 80 percent of the world's primary energy. Oil, natural gas, and coal heat homes, fuel transport, generate electricity, and serve as feedstocks for the chemicals and materials that constitute modern life. The replacement infrastructure — solar panels, wind turbines, batteries, electrified transport, heat pumps, hydrogen electrolyzers — is growing at record rates. Global investment in clean energy exceeded $1.7 trillion in 2023. But the installed base of fossil fuel infrastructure is vast, long-lived, and deeply integrated into economic systems that three billion people in the developing world are still building.

Birol's caution is not a defense of the status quo. The IEA under his leadership has been more aggressive in its climate scenarios than at any previous point in the agency's history. But Birol draws a distinction between the pace of transition that physics demands and the pace that energy systems can physically and politically sustain. The 2022 European energy crisis, triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, demonstrated what happens when energy supply contracts faster than alternatives can fill the gap. Natural gas prices in Europe spiked by a factor of ten. Electricity bills doubled and tripled. Public support for climate policy, as measured by Eurobarometer surveys, softened in several EU member states during the worst of the price shock — not because Europeans had stopped caring about climate change, but because they had also not stopped caring about heating their homes.

Developing economies add a further dimension. India, Southeast Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa are home to hundreds of millions of people who lack reliable electricity access. Their governments face a choice between the cheapest available energy source — often natural gas, sometimes coal — and cleaner alternatives that may cost more or require infrastructure that does not yet exist. Telling nations at the beginning of their industrialization that they cannot use the energy sources that wealthy nations used to build their prosperity is not a proposition that any international negotiation has found a way to make viable without massive financial transfers from rich to poor countries — transfers that have been repeatedly promised and repeatedly under-delivered.

The risk Birol emphasizes is not that the transition will happen too fast but that it will happen in a disorderly fashion — that premature supply cuts without adequate demand reduction will not eliminate fossil fuels but simply shift production to less regulated producers, increase energy prices, erode public support for climate policy, and ultimately slow the very transition they were meant to accelerate. The answer, in the IEA's framing, is to invest massively and rapidly in clean energy alternatives while managing the decline of fossil fuels through orderly policy — not to pretend that the 80 percent can become zero overnight.

---

The debate between these positions is genuine, and this book does not pretend to resolve it with a sentence. But the evidence does tilt the scales.

The carbon budget arithmetic is not a matter of perspective. It is a physical constraint derived from the relationship between cumulative emissions and warming, and it is unforgiving of delay. Every year that global emissions remain near 40 gigatons of CO2 narrows the remaining margin and increases the severity and cost of the eventual adjustment. McKibben is right that the math does not bend to political convenience.

Birol is right that the transition must be managed — that energy systems are physical infrastructure with long lifetimes, that political backlash from energy price shocks is real and has consequences, and that the needs of developing economies cannot be wished away by advocates in wealthy nations. The 2022 European experience was not an argument for slowing the transition; it was an argument for building the replacement faster, so that the vulnerability to fossil fuel supply shocks is eliminated rather than managed.

The synthesis, to the extent one exists, is that the evidence supports the fastest feasible transition — with "feasible" defined not by the preferences of incumbent industries but by the physical and financial constraints of deploying clean energy at scale. Those constraints are loosening rapidly as costs fall and deployment accelerates. The task is to ensure that the political will matches the pace that the technology now permits and the physics demands.

Whatever the pace, the next decade is decisive. Part V maps the obstacles that remain, the milestones that must be hit, and the actions available to every reader.
