## Opening Scene

**Event:** Residents of Reserve, Louisiana — a predominantly Black community in St. John the Baptist Parish, part of the 85-mile industrial corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans known as "Cancer Alley" — deploy PurpleAir and other low-cost air quality sensors to document chloroprene emissions from the Denka Performance Elastomer (formerly DuPont) neoprene plant.

**Content Notes:** The EPA classified chloroprene as a "likely human carcinogen" in 2010. The Denka plant is the only chloroprene production facility in the United States. EPA's own National Air Toxics Assessment (NATA) found that Census tracts near the plant face cancer risk levels 50 times the national average. But for years, official state monitoring was sparse, and Louisiana's Department of Environmental Quality contested federal data. Community leader Robert Taylor and the Concerned Citizens of St. John organized their own monitoring using low-cost sensors and partnered with university researchers. Their citizen science data helped build the evidentiary case that led to intensified EPA scrutiny and ongoing legal action.

**Scene Details:** Describe the physical landscape — the plant's proximity to an elementary school (Fifth Ward Elementary, less than a quarter mile away, which was eventually relocated). The smell. The health data — elevated cancer rates documented by the Louisiana Tumor Registry. The sensors mounted on residents' porches and fence posts. The tension between a community that depends on the plant for jobs and simultaneously suffers from its emissions.

**Alternative Opening:** If the Cancer Alley scene proves difficult to develop with sufficient specificity, consider: The Shipibo-Konibo people of the Peruvian Amazon using satellite imagery and GPS mapping (through partnerships with organizations like Rainforest Foundation US or Global Forest Watch) to document illegal gold mining and logging encroaching on their titled territory. Or: The West Oakland Environmental Indicators Project, where Black community members used mobile air monitoring mounted on Google Street View cars to map block-by-block pollution variation.

**Research Needed:** Contact Concerned Citizens of St. John (Robert Taylor) or the Tulane Environmental Law Clinic. Cross-reference with ProPublica's "Sacrifice Zone" investigation (2019) and The Guardian's Cancer Alley reporting. Academic sources: studies by Dr. Kimberly Terrell (Tulane) on chloroprene exposure.

## Section 1: The Disproportionate Burden

**Header:** The Disproportionate Burden

**Content Notes:**
- Open with the structural reality: environmental harm in the United States is not randomly distributed. It maps onto race and income with troubling precision.
- **EPA EJScreen:** The EPA's Environmental Justice Screening and Mapping Tool combines environmental and demographic indicators to identify overburdened communities. Walk the reader through what the tool shows — how to read it, what patterns emerge.
- **The foundational data:** Cite the landmark Tesai et al. (2019) study in *PLOS ONE* (often cited as Colmer et al. or referenced via Mikati et al. 2018 in *AJPH*): communities of color in the U.S. are exposed to 56% more NO2 (nitrogen dioxide) pollution than white communities. For PM2.5, disparities persist across income levels — race is a stronger predictor of pollution exposure than poverty.
- **The concept of "sacrifice zones":** Communities written off as collateral damage for industrial production. Cancer Alley. South Side Chicago. The Navajo Nation (uranium mining legacy). South Bronx. Port Arthur, Texas. Richmond, California.
- **Climate vulnerability layered on pollution exposure:** The communities already overburdened by industrial pollution are often the most vulnerable to climate impacts — heat islands (fewer trees, more asphalt), flood risk (lower-lying land, less infrastructure investment), and inadequate cooling and disaster response.
- **Historical roots:** Briefly trace the lineage — from Robert Bullard's *Dumping in Dixie* (1990) to the First National People of Color Environmental Leadership Summit (1991) to Executive Order 12898 (1994, Clinton) to Justice40 (2021, Biden).
- **Current federal framework:** The Justice40 Initiative commits that 40% of benefits from federal climate and clean energy investments flow to disadvantaged communities. How is it being implemented? What are the critiques?

**Key Data Points:**
- Mikati et al. (2018), *American Journal of Public Health*: PM2.5 disparities by race and income
- Tesai/Colmer et al. (2019): People of color exposed to 56% more NO2 than white communities (note: verify exact citation — this statistic has been attributed to multiple studies; the 2019 figure may come from Colmer et al. working paper or Jbaily et al. 2022 in *Nature*)
- EPA NATA: Cancer risk near Denka plant 50x national average
- American Lung Association: People of color are 3x more likely to live in the most polluted areas
- Justice40: 40% of benefits from federal climate investments to disadvantaged communities
- FEMA: 1 in 3 Americans lived in a county hit by a weather disaster in 2021

## Section 2: Technology as Equalizer

**Header:** Technology as Equalizer

**Content Notes:**
- The democratization thesis: For decades, environmental monitoring was the exclusive domain of government agencies and large corporations with expensive reference-grade equipment. Low-cost sensor technology and open-source data platforms have fundamentally changed who can measure pollution and where.
- **PurpleAir:** Founded by Adrian Dybwad in 2015. The PurpleAir sensor (approximately $200-$250) measures PM2.5 in real time and uploads data to a public map. Over 50,000 sensors deployed globally as of 2023. During wildfire season, PurpleAir data often provides more granular, real-time information than the EPA's AirNow network. But sensors require calibration and correction factors — discuss the EPA-PurpleAir correction factor developed to improve data accuracy.
- **Community air monitoring networks:** South Coast AQMD's Community Air Monitoring Program. The Imperial County community monitoring network along the U.S.-Mexico border. West Oakland Environmental Indicators Project's mobile monitoring.
- **Satellite data for land defense:** Global Forest Watch (World Resources Institute) provides near-real-time deforestation alerts using Landsat, Sentinel, and VIIRS satellite data. Indigenous communities in the Amazon, Congo Basin, and Southeast Asia use these alerts to detect illegal logging and mining within days, enabling rapid response. The Shipibo-Konibo mapping project. The Uru-Eu-Wau-Wau people in Brazil, profiled in the documentary *The Territory* (2022), using drones and GPS to document illegal encroachment.
- **Water monitoring:** Community-driven water quality testing after Flint, Michigan. Low-cost turbidity sensors and nitrate test strips. The rise of community science water monitoring networks.
- **Limitations and caveats:** Low-cost sensors are not reference-grade. They can produce misleading data without proper calibration. There is a risk of "data without power" — collecting information that does not lead to regulatory action because communities lack political leverage.

**Key Data Points:**
- PurpleAir: 50,000+ sensors globally (company data, 2023)
- PurpleAir sensor cost: ~$200-$250 per unit
- Global Forest Watch: Monitoring 70+ countries, over 100 million deforestation alerts generated
- EPA AirNow network: ~2,000 monitoring stations across the U.S. (compared to 50,000+ PurpleAir sensors)
- Cost of reference-grade PM2.5 monitor: $15,000-$50,000+ vs. $200 for PurpleAir

**Profiles:**
- **PurpleAir / Adrian Dybwad** — The founder's story; how a personal project became a global monitoring network.
- **Robert Taylor / Concerned Citizens of St. John** — Community leader in Reserve, Louisiana. Longtime advocate against Denka/DuPont emissions.
- **West Oakland Environmental Indicators Project (WOEIP)** — Ms. Margaret Gordon, co-founder. Pioneered community-based air monitoring in West Oakland, a community surrounded by port, freeway, and industrial pollution.

## Section 3: Indigenous Knowledge Integration

**Header:** Indigenous Knowledge Integration

**Content Notes:**
- The premise: Indigenous peoples manage or hold tenure over approximately 25% of the world's land surface, encompassing about 80% of remaining biodiversity (World Bank, 2023). Their traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) represents thousands of years of close observation and adaptive management. Modern technology and traditional knowledge are not opposites — they can be powerful complements.
- **Amazon example:** The RAISG (Amazonian Network of Georeferenced Socio-Environmental Information) combines satellite mapping with Indigenous territorial knowledge. Communities use GPS to demarcate territory, drones to monitor for incursions, and smartphone apps to report illegal activity. The Coordinator of Indigenous Organizations of the Amazon Basin (COICA) coordinates across nine countries.
- **Australian example:** Indigenous ranger programs in northern Australia combine Aboriginal fire management practices (cultural burning / "cool burning") with satellite monitoring and GIS mapping. The work of Victor Steffensen and the Mulong people. Research showing that Indigenous fire management reduces wildfire severity and emissions. These programs are now supported by government funding and carbon credit markets (savanna burning methodology under the Emissions Reduction Fund).
- **Arctic example:** Inuit Circumpolar Council's use of remote sensing combined with traditional knowledge of sea ice, animal migration, and weather patterns. The SIKU app and platform, which allows Inuit communities to combine satellite ice data with traditional ice knowledge for safe travel and hunting.
- **The "two-eyed seeing" framework:** Mi'kmaw Elder Albert Marshall's concept of integrating Indigenous and Western scientific knowledge systems, each contributing its strengths. This is not about instrumentalizing Indigenous knowledge — it is about genuine epistemic partnership.
- **Critical perspective:** The tension between open data and Indigenous data sovereignty. CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) as a counterpart to FAIR data principles. Indigenous communities must control their own data and knowledge.

**Key Data Points:**
- World Bank: Indigenous peoples manage ~25% of world's land surface, encompassing ~80% of biodiversity
- Australian Indigenous ranger programs: 900+ rangers across 100+ groups (NIAA data)
- Australian savanna burning: Indigenous fire management has abated an estimated 5+ million tonnes of CO2 equivalent since program inception
- RAISG: Mapping covers 8.5 million km2 of Amazonia
- SIKU platform: Used by Inuit communities across Canadian Arctic and Greenland

## Section 4: The Digital Divide

**Header:** The Digital Divide

**Content Notes:**
- The section's argument: Climate technology, for all its promise, risks reproducing existing inequalities if access is not deliberately equalized. The benefits of climate tech flow disproportionately to wealthy nations, educated populations, and connected communities.
- **Global South access gaps:** Many of the communities most vulnerable to climate change have the least access to digital tools. Lack of broadband, lack of smartphones, lack of technical literacy. Climate information systems (early warning, crop planning, weather data) often fail to reach the people who need them most. Example: smallholder farmers in sub-Saharan Africa who lack the connectivity to access satellite-derived weather forecasts.
- **The energy cost of data centers:** The environmental monitoring and AI systems described throughout this book require enormous computational infrastructure. IEA estimates data centers consumed about 460 TWh of electricity in 2022, roughly 2% of global electricity demand, and this is projected to potentially double by 2026. The irony: the systems we build to monitor and mitigate climate change have their own significant carbon footprint.
- **E-waste:** The global e-waste problem — 62 million tonnes generated in 2022 (UN Global E-waste Monitor 2024). Developing nations, particularly Ghana (Agbogbloshie), Nigeria, India, and China, bear the disproportionate burden of processing wealthy nations' electronic waste, often in unsafe conditions. The sensors, servers, batteries, and devices of the green economy will eventually become e-waste.
- **Algorithmic bias in climate tools:** Climate risk models used by insurers and lenders can systematically undervalue properties and creditworthiness in communities of color — essentially redlining by algorithm. Flood risk models may undercount risk in historically underinvested communities because they lack the data infrastructure for accurate modeling.
- **Solutions and bridges:** Initiatives to close the gap — organizations like Digital Green (agricultural climate adaptation via video), Satelligence (affordable satellite monitoring for smallholders), and the role of mobile-first platforms in reaching disconnected communities.

**Key Data Points:**
- IEA (2024): Data centers consumed ~460 TWh in 2022 (~2% of global electricity), projected to potentially double by 2026
- UN Global E-waste Monitor (2024): 62 million tonnes of e-waste generated in 2022; only 22% formally recycled
- ITU (2023): ~2.6 billion people remain offline globally
- WMO (2023): Early warning systems cover only ~50% of the world's population; coverage in Africa and small island developing states remains critically low

## Section 5: Energy Democracy

**Header:** Energy Democracy

**Content Notes:**
- The concept: Energy democracy means communities having meaningful ownership of and decision-making power over their energy systems. It is the antidote to both fossil fuel extractivism and green colonialism (e.g., large renewable projects that benefit distant investors while disrupting local land and livelihoods).
- **Community solar:** The model — a shared solar installation whose energy credits are distributed among subscribers, typically with a portion reserved for low-income households. The U.S. community solar market has grown significantly; SEIA data shows community solar capacity reached 6+ GW by 2023. The model allows renters, people in apartments, and those with unsuitable roofs to access solar energy.
- **Case study: Co-op Power, Western Massachusetts.** A multi-class, multiracial energy cooperative that develops community-owned solar projects, biodiesel production, and weatherization services. Members invest and share in the benefits. Model for community wealth building through energy.
- **Case study: The Hopi Foundation's NativeSUN program.** Solar installation and training on the Hopi reservation, where many homes still lack grid electricity. The program combines energy access with economic development and cultural values.
- **Microgrids for resilience:** Communities investing in microgrids that can disconnect from the main grid during outages. Borrego Springs, California microgrid (SDG&E pilot). Brooklyn Microgrid (peer-to-peer energy trading using blockchain). The Islandia project in Puerto Rico after Hurricane Maria — community-owned solar and battery systems that provided power when the central grid failed for months.
- **The political economy:** Energy democracy challenges the centralized utility model. Utilities have pushed back against community solar, net metering, and distributed generation. Document the policy battles — e.g., Florida's utility-backed opposition to rooftop solar, Nevada's net metering fight.
- **Global examples:** Energy cooperatives in Germany (Energiegenossenschaften — over 800 energy cooperatives). Kenya's community-owned mini-grids. Bangladesh's solar home system revolution (IDCOL program installed 6 million+ solar home systems).

**Key Data Points:**
- SEIA: U.S. community solar capacity reached 6+ GW by 2023
- Germany: 800+ energy cooperatives (Energiegenossenschaften)
- Bangladesh IDCOL: 6 million+ solar home systems installed
- Puerto Rico post-Maria: Some communities waited 11 months for grid restoration; community solar/battery systems provided power in days
- DOE: Community solar could serve 49% of U.S. households (those unable to install rooftop solar)

**Profiles:**
- **Peggy Shepard** — Co-founder and Executive Director, WE ACT for Environmental Justice (West Harlem, New York). One of the pioneers of the environmental justice movement. Has served on the White House Environmental Justice Advisory Council (WHEJAC). Her work bridges community organizing, policy advocacy, and climate equity.
- **Indigenous Environmental Network (IEN)** — Founded in 1990. Tom Goldtooth (now Kichisaga Misko Mukwa), former executive director. IEN has been central to Indigenous resistance to fossil fuel infrastructure (Dakota Access Pipeline, Line 3, Keystone XL) and advocates for Indigenous-led climate solutions.
- **Robert Taylor / Concerned Citizens of St. John** — (see Opening Scene).
- **Margaret Gordon / WOEIP** — (see Section 2).

**Writing Notes:**
- Tone: This is the most politically and emotionally charged chapter in the book. The tone must be respectful, precise, and grounded in data while honoring the lived experience of communities on the front lines. Avoid both detached academic distance and performative outrage. Let the facts and the people speak.
- Structure: The chapter moves from problem (Sections 1-2) through partial solutions (Sections 2-3) to the structural tensions (Section 4) and then to transformative alternatives (Section 5). The opening narrative should thread through the chapter — return to Cancer Alley or the featured community at key points to keep the human stakes visible.
- Tension: The central tension is between technology's emancipatory potential (sensors, satellites, open data) and its capacity to reproduce inequality (digital divide, algorithmic bias, e-waste). Do not resolve this tension artificially. Hold it.
- Voice: Center the voices of affected communities. Do not write about frontline communities from a distance — their words, their framing, their priorities should shape the narrative. This is not a chapter about what technology does *for* these communities; it is about what these communities are doing *with* technology, and what they demand from those who build it.
- Sensitivity: Be attentive to the difference between Indigenous knowledge systems and Western science. Do not frame TEK as merely a "supplement" to Western methods. The "two-eyed seeing" framework offers a more respectful model. Be mindful of intellectual property and data sovereignty concerns.
