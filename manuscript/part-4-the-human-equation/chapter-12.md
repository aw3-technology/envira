# Chapter 12: The Frontlines
## Environmental Justice and Climate Equity

**Part IV: The Human Equation: People, Power, and the Social Circuitry**

---

In Reserve, Louisiana, a small, predominantly Black community in St. John the Baptist Parish, tucked into the eighty-five-mile industrial corridor between Baton Rouge and New Orleans that locals and reporters have long called Cancer Alley, the air smells faintly of chloroprene on warm days. The compound, used to manufacture neoprene, was classified by the Environmental Protection Agency as a "likely human carcinogen" in 2010. The Denka Performance Elastomer plant, which acquired the facility from DuPont in 2015, is the only chloroprene production site in the United States. The EPA's National Air Toxics Assessment found that census tracts surrounding the plant face cancer risk levels fifty times the national average.¹

For years, Louisiana's Department of Environmental Quality contested the federal data. Official state monitoring was sparse, a handful of reference-grade stations positioned to capture regional averages rather than the hyperlocal exposure that mattered to residents living downwind. So the community did what communities across America have increasingly learned to do: they built their own nervous system.

Robert Taylor, a longtime resident and leader of the Concerned Citizens of St. John, organized neighbors to mount PurpleAir sensors on their porches, fence posts, and garage walls. At roughly $200 per unit, compared to $15,000 to $50,000 for a reference-grade monitor, the sensors could not match government equipment for precision.² But they could do something government equipment was not doing: measure the air in the places people actually lived, at the times they actually breathed it. Partnered with researchers from Tulane University, including environmental health scientist Kimberly Terrell, the community's citizen science data helped build the evidentiary case for intensified EPA scrutiny and ongoing legal action.³

Fifth Ward Elementary School sat less than a quarter mile from the plant. It was eventually relocated. The fact that it had been there at all, that an elementary school could operate in the shadow of the nation's sole chloroprene producer for decades without triggering regulatory intervention, is not an anomaly in American environmental governance. It is the pattern.

## The Disproportionate Burden

Environmental harm in the United States is not randomly distributed. It maps onto race and income with a precision that researchers have documented for more than three decades.

In 1990, sociologist Robert Bullard published *Dumping in Dixie*, the foundational text of the environmental justice movement, documenting how hazardous waste facilities, polluting industries, and toxic exposures were systematically concentrated in communities of color and low-income neighborhoods across the American South.⁴ The following year, delegates to the First National People of Color Environmental Leadership Summit adopted seventeen principles of environmental justice that reframed pollution not as an externality to be managed but as a civil rights issue to be confronted. In 1994, President Clinton signed Executive Order 12898, directing federal agencies to identify and address disproportionate environmental and health effects on minority and low-income populations.

Three decades later, the data has only grown sharper. A 2018 study by Mikati and colleagues in the *American Journal of Public Health* confirmed that disparities in PM2.5 exposure persist across income levels, with race serving as a stronger predictor of pollution exposure than poverty.⁵ Jbaily and colleagues, writing in *Nature* in 2022, found that the most polluted census tracts in the United States are disproportionately nonwhite and low-income, and that these disparities have narrowed only modestly over two decades despite overall air quality improvements.⁶ Tessum and colleagues, in a 2019 study in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, found that Black and Hispanic Americans are exposed to more air pollution than they generate through their consumption patterns: they bear a disproportionate burden of pollution caused by the consumption of others.⁷

The geography of this burden is legible from orbit. Cancer Alley. The South Side of Chicago, ringed by former steel mills and active waste facilities. The Navajo Nation, scarred by the legacy of Cold War uranium mining. Port Arthur, Texas, where oil refineries and petrochemical plants encircle a majority-Black and Latino community. Richmond, California, where the Chevron refinery has operated since 1902 and accidental releases have sent thousands to hospitals.

Climate vulnerability layers onto this existing burden with cruel efficiency. The communities already overburdened by industrial pollution tend to be the most vulnerable to climate impacts. Research on urban heat islands has documented temperature differences of 10 to 15 degrees Fahrenheit between wealthy, tree-lined neighborhoods and low-income neighborhoods with more asphalt, less green space, and fewer resources for air conditioning, a disparity that traces directly to twentieth-century redlining policies that determined which neighborhoods received investment and which did not. Flood risk concentrates in lower-lying areas with less infrastructure investment. Heat wave mortality falls disproportionately on elderly residents of color in neighborhoods with inadequate cooling. Disaster response reaches some communities faster than others, a pattern that Hurricane Katrina in 2005 made visible to the nation and that subsequent disasters have confirmed repeatedly. A 2021 analysis found that one in three Americans lived in a county struck by a weather disaster that year.⁸ The odds were not evenly distributed.

The international dimension of climate injustice is equally stark. The fifty least developed countries, which account for roughly 4 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions, experience climate damages, including crop failures, infrastructure destruction, health costs, and displacement, that consume a disproportionate share of their GDP. The Loss and Damage fund agreed at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh and operationalized at COP28 in Dubai represented a landmark acknowledgment that wealthy nations bear responsibility for the climate damages suffered by countries that contributed least to the problem. But the initial pledges, roughly $700 million, were a fraction of the estimated $400 billion per year in climate-related losses that developing countries already face.

In 2021, the Biden administration launched the Justice40 Initiative, committing that 40 percent of the benefits from federal climate and clean energy investments would flow to disadvantaged communities. The administration developed the Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool, CEJST, to identify qualifying communities using environmental, health, and socioeconomic indicators.⁹ The initiative represented the most significant federal commitment to environmental justice in American history. Its implementation has been uneven: some agencies have moved aggressively to direct investment toward overburdened communities, while others have struggled to translate the commitment into measurable benefit flows. Critics have noted that the tool does not explicitly include race as an indicator, potentially missing the communities the policy was designed to serve.

## Technology as Equalizer

For decades, environmental monitoring was the exclusive domain of government agencies and large corporations with expensive reference-grade equipment. The democratization of sensor technology has begun to change that, though the change is neither complete nor uncomplicated.

PurpleAir, founded by Adrian Dybwad in 2015, has become the most visible symbol of citizen environmental monitoring. The sensor network had grown to more than 50,000 units globally by 2023, providing real-time PM2.5 readings uploaded to a public map that anyone with an internet connection can access.² During wildfire seasons, PurpleAir data often provides more granular, more current information than the EPA's AirNow network, which relies on roughly 2,000 monitoring stations across the entire United States. The contrast is stark: fifty thousand citizen sensors versus two thousand government monitors.

But the comparison requires nuance. PurpleAir sensors are not reference-grade instruments. They require correction factors (the EPA developed a PurpleAir correction algorithm to improve data accuracy) and they can produce misleading readings under certain humidity and temperature conditions. What they lack in precision, however, they compensate for in coverage and accessibility. When Margaret Gordon, co-founder of the West Oakland Environmental Indicators Project, helped pioneer community-based air monitoring in a neighborhood surrounded by port operations, freeway traffic, and industrial facilities, the point was not to replace government monitoring but to reveal what government monitoring was missing.¹⁰ Block-by-block variation in pollution exposure, the difference between living on the windward side of a freeway versus the leeward side, was invisible to regional monitoring networks. Community sensors made it visible.

The same principle extends beyond air quality. Global Forest Watch, operated by the World Resources Institute, provides near-real-time deforestation alerts using Landsat, Sentinel, and VIIRS satellite data. Indigenous communities in the Amazon, Congo Basin, and Southeast Asia use these alerts to detect illegal logging and mining within days, enabling rapid response.¹¹ The Uru-Eu-Wau-Wau people in the Brazilian state of Rondônia, profiled in the 2022 documentary *The Territory*, used drones and GPS mapping to document illegal encroachment on their territory — the planetary nervous system repurposed as a tool of territorial defense.¹²

In Flint, Michigan, where the 2014 water crisis exposed both lead contamination and the failure of government monitoring to detect it, community-driven water quality testing became an act of survival and an assertion of democratic accountability. Residents partnered with Virginia Tech researcher Marc Edwards, whose independent testing contradicted official assurances from the Michigan Department of Environmental Quality. The community's data, gathered sample by sample, house by house, became the evidence that forced a state and federal emergency declaration.

The lesson from Flint, Cancer Alley, West Oakland, and the Amazon converges on a single insight: when communities have their own environmental data, they can challenge corporate and government narratives. When they do not, they are invisible to the system.

But there is a harder lesson embedded in these stories. Data alone does not produce justice. Reserve, Louisiana's residents have had air quality data for years; the Denka plant continues to operate. Flint's water contamination was documented and publicized across the world; the infrastructure repairs took years and are still, in some measures, incomplete. West Oakland's pollution maps are precise enough to distinguish one block from the next; the port and the freeways remain. The gap between knowing and acting, the same gap that structures the entire argument of this book, is widest in communities with the least political power. The nervous system detects the signal. Whether the organism responds depends on who is listening.

## Climate Migration and the Geography of Displacement

The environmental justice framework was built around stationary communities, people rooted in place, fighting pollution where they live. Climate change is adding a dimension that the framework was not designed to address: displacement.

The World Bank's Groundswell report projected that by 2050, internal climate migration could reach 216 million people across six regions: sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Latin America, East Asia and the Pacific, North Africa, and Eastern Europe and Central Asia.³² These are not people fleeing a single disaster. They are people whose livelihoods have been gradually eroded by shifting rainfall patterns, declining crop yields, rising seas, and intensifying heat, until remaining in place is no longer viable.

In the United States, the pattern is already visible. The residents of Isle de Jean Charles, Louisiana, a narrow strip of land in Terrebonne Parish that has lost 98 percent of its area since 1955 to subsidence, sea level rise, and canal dredging for the oil industry, became the nation's first officially recognized climate refugees when the federal government awarded $48 million for their relocation in 2016.³³ The community is largely Indigenous, members of the Biloxi-Chitimacha-Choctaw tribe, and their displacement reprises a pattern as old as the republic: Indigenous peoples bearing the cost of extraction economies they did not choose and did not benefit from.

Globally, the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre reported that weather-related events triggered 26.4 million internal displacements in 2023 alone.³⁴ The people who move are disproportionately poor, rural, and from the Global South, populations whose cumulative carbon emissions are a fraction of those produced by the wealthy nations whose consumption drives the warming that displaces them. This is the climate justice equation in its starkest form: those who contributed least to the crisis bear its heaviest costs.

## Indigenous Knowledge and Data Sovereignty

Indigenous peoples manage or hold tenure over approximately 25 percent of the world's land surface, encompassing roughly 80 percent of remaining biodiversity.¹³ Their traditional ecological knowledge represents millennia of close observation and adaptive management. The question facing the planetary nervous system is not whether to incorporate this knowledge, but how, and on whose terms.

In the Amazon, the RAISG network, the Amazonian Network of Georeferenced Socio-Environmental Information, combines satellite mapping with Indigenous territorial knowledge across 8.5 million square kilometers, coordinated by the Coordinator of Indigenous Organizations of the Amazon Basin across nine countries.¹⁴ Communities use GPS to demarcate territory, drones to monitor for incursions, and smartphone applications to report illegal activity. The technology is Western; the knowledge of what to look for, and why it matters, is Indigenous.

In northern Australia, Indigenous ranger programs combine Aboriginal fire management practices, cultural burning, or "cool burning," which uses carefully timed low-intensity fires to reduce fuel loads and prevent catastrophic wildfire, with satellite monitoring and GIS mapping. More than 900 rangers work across more than 100 groups, and research has demonstrated that Indigenous fire management reduces both wildfire severity and greenhouse gas emissions. These programs are now supported by government funding and carbon credit markets under the Emissions Reduction Fund's savanna burning methodology, with an estimated five million tonnes of CO2 equivalent abated since the program's inception.¹⁵

In the Canadian Arctic, the SIKU platform allows Inuit communities to combine satellite ice data with traditional knowledge of sea ice conditions, animal migration patterns, and weather for safe travel and hunting. The platform was developed with and for Inuit communities, not imposed upon them.¹⁶

Mi'kmaw Elder Albert Marshall articulated the principle that should govern these collaborations: "two-eyed seeing," the practice of learning to see from one eye with the strengths of Indigenous knowledge and from the other with the strengths of Western science, and using both together for the benefit of all.¹⁷ This is not about instrumentalizing Indigenous knowledge as a "supplement" to Western methods. It is about genuine epistemic partnership, and it requires that Indigenous communities control their own data and knowledge.

The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) were developed as a counterpart to the FAIR data principles that govern open science.¹⁸ They assert that data collected from or about Indigenous communities belongs to those communities, not to the researchers, corporations, or governments that collect it. In the age of the planetary nervous system, data sovereignty is sovereignty.

## The Global South and the Access Question

The communities most vulnerable to climate change often have the least access to the technologies described in this book. This is not an accident of geography. It is a consequence of investment patterns that have systematically undervalued the Global South.

And yet some of the most innovative models for climate-resilient energy access have emerged precisely from the places that wealthy-nation infrastructure planning has overlooked. In Kenya, M-KOPA has provided solar home systems to more than three million households using a pay-as-you-go model built on mobile money: the solar panel and battery arrive with a SIM card, and families pay in small daily installments that are typically less than what they previously spent on kerosene.²⁴ D.light, founded in 2007, has reached more than 200 million people across sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia with affordable solar lanterns and home power systems.²⁵ In Bangladesh, the Infrastructure Development Company Limited installed more than six million solar home systems through a program that became one of the largest off-grid energy initiatives in history, reaching communities that the central grid was decades away from serving.²⁶

These are not charity projects. They are market-creating innovations that recognized what conventional energy planning missed: that the roughly 770 million people who still lack electricity access represent not a development burden but an opportunity to build clean energy systems from scratch, bypassing the fossil fuel infrastructure that wealthier nations are now struggling to dismantle.

Harish Hande, the founder of SELCO Foundation in India and a 2011 Magsaysay Award laureate, has spent decades demonstrating that decentralized renewable energy can be designed specifically for the poorest communities, that the technology works when the financing, distribution, and maintenance are designed around how those communities actually live rather than how energy planners imagine they should.²⁷

The models scale. But they scale only when capital flows toward them, and the investment gap in climate adaptation and clean energy access for developing countries remains one of the most consequential failures of the international response to climate change.

## Energy Democracy

Energy democracy, the principle that communities should have meaningful ownership of and decision-making power over their energy systems, represents the antidote to both fossil fuel extractivism and what critics have called green colonialism: large renewable projects that benefit distant investors while disrupting local land and livelihoods.

In the United States, community solar has emerged as the most accessible expression of this principle. The model, a shared solar installation whose energy credits are distributed among subscribers, typically with a portion reserved for low-income households, allows renters, apartment dwellers, and those with unsuitable roofs to access solar energy. According to the Solar Energy Industries Association, U.S. community solar capacity reached more than 6 gigawatts by 2023, and the Department of Energy has estimated that community solar could serve 49 percent of American households.²⁸

In Germany, more than 800 energy cooperatives, *Energiegenossenschaften*, allow citizens to collectively invest in and operate renewable energy projects. The German model demonstrates that distributed ownership is not merely aspirational; at scale, it reshapes the political economy of energy by giving communities a financial stake in the transition.²⁹

In Puerto Rico, the failure of centralized infrastructure during Hurricane Maria, where some communities waited eleven months for grid restoration, catalyzed a movement toward community-owned solar and battery systems. Organizations like the Casa Pueblo community center in Adjuntas became models for energy self-sufficiency, providing power to surrounding neighborhoods while the central grid remained inoperable.³⁰

Peggy Shepard, co-founder and executive director of WE ACT for Environmental Justice in West Harlem, New York, and a member of the White House Environmental Justice Advisory Council, has spent more than three decades demonstrating that environmental justice and energy democracy are inseparable. Her organization's work, from fighting bus depot pollution to advocating for equitable distribution of clean energy investments, exemplifies the principle that the communities most affected by environmental harm must be centered in the design of environmental solutions, not merely consulted after the designs are complete.³¹

The political economy of energy democracy is not simple. Utilities have pushed back against community solar, net metering, and distributed generation. In Florida, utility-backed campaigns have fought rooftop solar; in Nevada, net metering battles have reshaped state energy policy. The transition from centralized fossil fuel systems to distributed renewable systems does not automatically redistribute power. It does so only when communities organize to claim it.

## Nervous System Connection

The nervous system metaphor raises a justice question that runs deeper than technology access: whose nervous system is it? Who builds it, who owns the data, and who benefits from the insights it produces?

Community sensor networks in fence-line communities near refineries and chemical plants represent the nervous system democratized: residents generating their own environmental data, often revealing pollution levels far higher than official monitors showed. Citizen science platforms like iNaturalist and eBird enable millions of non-experts to contribute to the planetary sensing network, though participation skews heavily toward educated, affluent populations in wealthy countries. Satellite monitoring of Amazonian deforestation has empowered Indigenous communities to document illegal logging in near-real-time. The same satellite technology, in different hands, could be used to monitor and control communities rather than protect them.

The design choices matter. The EPA's EJScreen tool and the federal CEJST platform represent attempts to ensure that the nervous system's data flows toward the communities that need it most. But the platforms are only as useful as the political will to act on what they reveal. Data without power is surveillance. Data with power is justice.

## Tensions and Headwinds

The promise of technology-enabled environmental justice confronts structural obstacles that no sensor can overcome.

The digital divide operates as a climate divide. Communities most vulnerable to climate change are least likely to have broadband access, digital literacy, or the resources to engage with climate technology platforms. The World Meteorological Organization reported in 2023 that early warning systems cover only about 50 percent of the world's population, with coverage in Africa and small island developing states remaining critically low.¹⁹ The approximately 2.6 billion people who remain offline globally, according to the International Telecommunications Union, are disproportionately the people most exposed to climate risk.²⁰

Data colonialism presents a subtler threat. Wealthy nations and corporations collect environmental data from developing countries, including satellite imagery, sensor readings, and biodiversity surveys, often without consent, benefit-sharing, or local capacity building. The question of who owns the data from a satellite monitoring the Congo Basin has no simple answer, but the default arrangement, in which the data flows to institutions in the Global North, reproduces colonial patterns in digital form.

The environmental monitoring and AI systems described throughout this book carry their own environmental costs. The International Energy Agency estimated that data centers consumed approximately 460 terawatt-hours of electricity in 2022, roughly 2 percent of global demand, with projections suggesting this could double by 2026.²¹ The 62 million tonnes of electronic waste generated globally in 2022, of which only 22 percent was formally recycled, disproportionately burden communities in Ghana, Nigeria, India, and China.²²

Perhaps most fundamentally, the clean energy transition itself does not automatically produce justice. Solar farms, wind installations, and lithium mines displace communities. The cobalt that powers electric vehicle batteries is mined under conditions in the Democratic Republic of Congo that environmental justice advocates have compared to the extractive regimes the green transition is supposed to replace. The transition from fossil fuels to renewables requires not just different energy sources but different power structures, and building those structures is political work, not technological work.

The adaptation finance gap measures the distance between recognition and response. Developing countries need an estimated $160 to $340 billion per year for climate adaptation by 2030. Current flows are approximately $21 billion.²³ The nervous system can reveal the need with unprecedented precision. It cannot fund the response.

## The Work Ahead

Robert Taylor's PurpleAir sensor is still on his porch. The data it collects flows to a public map where anyone, a researcher at Tulane, a journalist, an EPA analyst in Washington, a neighbor across the street, can see it. The sensor cost two hundred dollars. It does not have the precision of a federal reference monitor. But it is there, in a community where the state's monitors were not, generating a continuous record of what the air contains.

The environmental justice movement has always understood that the crisis is not primarily informational. People in Cancer Alley did not need a PurpleAir sensor to know the air was making them sick. They knew from the diagnoses, the funerals, the neighbors who moved away or could not. What the sensor provided was evidence in a form that the systems of power recognize: quantified, time-stamped, uploadable, comparable to regulatory thresholds. The nervous system does not create the injustice or solve it. It makes the injustice legible in the language of institutions. What those institutions do with the information is a political question, not a technological one.

The synthesis between the technology optimist and the justice advocate is not that one side is right and the other wrong. Both describe real dynamics operating simultaneously. Technology has genuinely democratized environmental monitoring in ways that would have been unimaginable a generation ago. That democratization has genuinely failed to reach many of the communities that need it most. Both statements are true, and the gap between them is where the work lies.

A sensor without political power is a curiosity. A sensor coupled with legal standing, organized community advocacy, and responsive regulatory institutions is a tool of liberation. The planetary nervous system described in Part II of this book is the most powerful environmental monitoring infrastructure in human history. Whether it serves justice depends on choices being made now: in the design of data policies, the distribution of sensor networks, the governance of AI systems, and the political movements that demand accountability from the institutions that control them.

The environmental justice movement, from Cancer Alley to the Arctic to the small island states negotiating for their survival at global climate summits, insists on a truth that data and technology alone cannot supply: that the measure of a just transition is not the total tons of carbon removed or the total watts of clean energy installed but whether the people who have borne the heaviest costs are the first to benefit from the solutions. That principle is easy to state and extraordinarily difficult to implement. Its implementation is the work of a generation — and the question of whether the broader culture is shifting fast enough to support it is the subject of the next chapter.
