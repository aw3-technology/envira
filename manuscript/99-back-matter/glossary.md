# Glossary

Key terms used throughout this book, defined for quick reference. Terms are listed alphabetically. Cross-references to other glossary entries are indicated with **bold**.

---

**Adaptation** — Adjustments in ecological, social, or economic systems in response to actual or expected climate change and its effects. Distinct from **mitigation**, which addresses the causes. Examples: building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops, relocating communities from flood zones.

**Albedo** — The fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface. Ice and snow have high albedo (reflect most sunlight); ocean and dark surfaces have low albedo (absorb most sunlight). Changes in albedo due to ice loss create a positive **feedback loop** that amplifies warming.

**AMOC** (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation) — A system of ocean currents that transports warm water northward in the Atlantic and returns cold water southward at depth. A potential **tipping point**: significant weakening or collapse would disrupt weather patterns, sea levels, and ecosystems across the Northern Hemisphere. Sometimes informally referred to as part of the "Gulf Stream system."

**Anthropocene** — A proposed geological epoch dating from the start of significant human impact on Earth's geology and ecosystems. Not yet formally adopted by the International Commission on Stratigraphy, but widely used to describe the current era of human-driven planetary change.

**Biochar** — A stable form of carbon produced by heating biomass in the absence of oxygen (pyrolysis). When added to soil, biochar can sequester carbon for hundreds to thousands of years while improving soil health, water retention, and nutrient availability.

**Biodiversity** — The variety of life at all levels: genetic diversity within species, species diversity within ecosystems, and ecosystem diversity across landscapes. Measured through indices such as species richness, the Shannon diversity index, and the **Living Planet Index**.

**Blended Finance** — The strategic use of public or philanthropic capital to mobilize private investment in sustainable development. Typically involves concessional (below-market-rate) capital absorbing risk to make projects attractive for commercial investors.

**Carbon Budget** — The maximum amount of cumulative **CO2** emissions that would keep global warming below a specific temperature threshold with a given probability. The remaining budget for **1.5°C** (50% probability) is approximately 250 GtCO2 from January 2024.

**Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS)** — Technologies that capture CO2 from industrial sources or power plants, then either use it in products (utilization) or inject it underground for permanent storage. Distinct from **DAC**, which captures CO2 from ambient air.

**Carbon Credit** — A tradable certificate representing the reduction or removal of one metric ton of CO2 equivalent. Used in both compliance markets (regulatory) and voluntary markets. Quality and integrity vary widely; verification is a key challenge.

**Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR)** — The process of removing CO2 from the atmosphere and durably storing it. Includes nature-based approaches (reforestation, soil carbon) and engineered approaches (**DAC**, **enhanced weathering**, **BECCS**). Distinct from emissions reduction.

**Carbon Intensity** — The amount of CO2 emitted per unit of economic output (e.g., tCO2 per dollar of GDP) or per unit of energy produced (e.g., gCO2 per kWh). Carbon intensity can decline even as total emissions rise, if the economy or energy production grows faster.

**Carbon Neutrality** — See **Net Zero**.

**Carbon Pricing** — A policy mechanism that puts a price on CO2 emissions, either through a carbon tax (setting the price directly) or an emissions trading system (cap and trade, where the market sets the price). Covers approximately 23% of global emissions as of 2023.

**Circular Economy** — An economic model that aims to eliminate waste and maximize resource use by designing products for durability, reuse, repair, remanufacturing, and recycling. Contrasts with the linear "take-make-waste" model.

**Climate Justice** — A framework recognizing that climate change disproportionately affects communities that have contributed least to the problem — particularly low-income communities, communities of color, Indigenous peoples, and the Global South. Encompasses both distributive justice (fair sharing of burdens and benefits) and procedural justice (meaningful participation in decision-making).

**Climate Sensitivity** — The amount of warming expected from a doubling of atmospheric CO2 concentration. The IPCC's best estimate for equilibrium climate sensitivity is 3°C (likely range: 2.5–4°C). A key parameter in climate models and projections.

**CMIP** (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project) — An international framework for comparing climate model outputs. CMIP6 (Phase 6) provides the model ensemble underlying IPCC AR6 assessments. Over 100 models from 50+ research groups participate.

**COP** (Conference of the Parties) — The annual meeting of countries that have signed the **UNFCCC**. The primary forum for international climate negotiations. COP21 (Paris, 2015) produced the Paris Agreement. COP28 (Dubai, 2023) completed the first **Global Stocktake**.

**DAC** (Direct Air Capture) — Technology that captures CO2 directly from ambient air using chemical sorbents or solvents. Currently captures approximately 0.01 MtCO2/year at costs of $400–1,000/ton. Needed at much larger scale in scenarios that limit warming to 1.5°C.

**Decarbonization** — The process of reducing carbon dioxide emissions from energy systems, industry, transportation, and other sectors. Can occur through efficiency improvements, fuel switching, electrification, and **carbon capture**.

**Digital Twin** — A virtual replica of a physical system (city, ecosystem, energy grid, building) that is continuously updated with real-world data. Used for simulation, optimization, and scenario planning.

**Ecosystem Services** — The benefits humans derive from ecosystems, including provisioning services (food, water, timber), regulating services (climate regulation, flood control, water purification), cultural services (recreation, spiritual value), and supporting services (nutrient cycling, soil formation).

**Electrification** — The process of replacing fossil fuel-powered systems with electrically powered alternatives. Key examples: heat pumps replacing gas furnaces, EVs replacing gasoline cars, induction stoves replacing gas stoves. Effectiveness depends on the carbon intensity of the electricity grid.

**Enhanced Weathering** — Spreading crushed silicate minerals (such as basalt) on land surfaces to accelerate natural chemical weathering processes that absorb CO2. A form of **CDR** with potential agricultural co-benefits.

**ESG** (Environmental, Social, and Governance) — A framework for evaluating corporate sustainability and ethical practices. ESG ratings and labels vary widely across providers and are subject to criticism for inconsistency and potential **greenwashing**.

**Feedback Loop** — A process where the output of a system influences its own input. Positive feedback loops amplify change (e.g., ice melting reduces **albedo**, causing more warming, causing more melting). Negative feedback loops dampen change (e.g., increased CO2 stimulates plant growth, which absorbs some CO2).

**GHG** (Greenhouse Gas) — Gases that trap heat in the atmosphere by absorbing and re-emitting infrared radiation. The main GHGs: carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases (HFCs, PFCs, SF6, NF3). Each has different warming potency and atmospheric lifetime.

**Global Stocktake** — A process under the Paris Agreement (occurring every 5 years, first completed at COP28 in 2023) to assess collective global progress toward the Agreement's goals. Intended to inform and increase ambition in the next round of **NDCs**.

**Greenwashing** — The practice of making misleading claims about the environmental benefits of a product, service, technology, or corporate practice. Can range from outright falsehood to selective emphasis on minor positive actions that distract from significant negative impacts.

**Heat Pump** — A device that moves heat from one place to another using electricity, providing both heating and cooling. Air-source heat pumps are 3–5x more efficient than gas furnaces because they move existing heat rather than generating it through combustion.

**IPCC** (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) — The United Nations body responsible for assessing the science related to climate change. Produces comprehensive Assessment Reports (AR1 through AR6) and Special Reports synthesizing published research. Does not conduct original research.

**Just Transition** — The principle that the shift to a low-carbon economy must be fair and inclusive, providing support for workers and communities that depend on fossil fuel industries. Includes retraining, economic diversification, social safety nets, and meaningful participation in transition planning.

**LCOE** (Levelized Cost of Energy) — The total cost of building and operating an energy-generating asset over its lifetime, divided by the total energy produced. Allows comparison across technologies. Solar and onshore wind now have the lowest LCOE of any new electricity source in most markets.

**Mitigation** — Actions to reduce or prevent greenhouse gas emissions. Distinct from **adaptation**, which responds to climate impacts. Examples: renewable energy deployment, energy efficiency, electrification, carbon capture, land-use change.

**NDC** (Nationally Determined Contribution) — A country's self-defined climate action plan under the Paris Agreement, outlining emissions reduction targets and climate policies. Updated every 5 years with the expectation of increasing ambition. Current NDCs collectively fall far short of limiting warming to 1.5°C or 2°C.

**Net Zero** — A state in which greenhouse gas emissions produced are balanced by an equal amount of emissions removed from the atmosphere, resulting in no net addition of GHGs. The Paris Agreement calls for global net-zero CO2 emissions by mid-century. Does not mean zero gross emissions.

**Paris Agreement** — International treaty adopted at COP21 in 2015, committing signatory nations to limit global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit to 1.5°C. Mechanism: countries submit **NDCs** and participate in the **Global Stocktake** review process.

**Perovskite** — A class of materials with a specific crystal structure showing promise for next-generation solar cells. Perovskite solar cells have achieved >26% efficiency in the lab and can be manufactured at potentially lower cost than silicon. Stability and scalability remain challenges.

**Planetary Boundaries** — A framework identifying nine Earth system processes with proposed quantitative boundaries within which humanity can safely operate. Developed by Rockstrom et al. (2009) and updated by Steffen et al. (2015). As of 2023, six of nine boundaries have been transgressed.

**Renewable Energy Certificate (REC)** — A tradable certificate proving that 1 MWh of electricity was generated from a renewable source. Used by companies and utilities to claim renewable energy use. Does not necessarily mean the buyer consumed renewable electricity directly.

**Scope 1 / 2 / 3 Emissions** — A classification system for greenhouse gas emissions defined by the **GHG Protocol**. Scope 1: direct emissions from owned sources. Scope 2: indirect emissions from purchased electricity/heat. Scope 3: all other indirect emissions in the value chain (typically 70–90% of total footprint).

**Social Cost of Carbon (SCC)** — An estimate of the economic damages caused by emitting one additional metric ton of CO2. Used in cost-benefit analyses of climate policies. Estimates range widely ($50–$200+/tCO2) depending on discount rate, damage function, and other assumptions.

**Stranded Assets** — Assets that have suffered unanticipated or premature write-downs, devaluations, or conversion to liabilities. In climate context: fossil fuel reserves, infrastructure, and related assets that may lose value as the world transitions to clean energy.

**Tipping Point** — A threshold in a climate or ecological system beyond which change becomes self-reinforcing and potentially irreversible, even if the original forcing is removed. Examples include ice sheet collapse, permafrost thaw, Amazon dieback, and **AMOC** disruption. Some may interact in cascading "tipping cascades."

**UNFCCC** (United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change) — The international environmental treaty adopted in 1992 establishing the framework for international climate negotiations. The Paris Agreement operates under the UNFCCC. Its **COP** meetings are the primary forum for global climate diplomacy.

## Glossary Notes

**Scope:** This glossary covers terms used frequently in this book and in broader climate and environmental discourse. It is not exhaustive. For more comprehensive climate terminology, see the IPCC AR6 Glossary (available at ipcc.ch).

**Currency:** Definitions reflect scientific understanding and common usage as of [publication date]. Some terms (particularly around climate policy and technology) evolve rapidly.
