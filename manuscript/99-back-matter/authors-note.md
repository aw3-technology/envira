# Author's Note

This book was commissioned by AW3 Technology, a company that works at the intersection of technology development and environmental awareness. AW3's engagement with climate data, environmental monitoring, and cross-industry conversations about the convergence of technology and environmentalism convinced the company that this story, the construction of a planetary nervous system and the question of whether humanity would act on what it revealed, needed a comprehensive, independent telling.

The relationship was one of support, not editorial control. AW3 funded the research and writing. The reporting, analysis, and conclusions are entirely independent. No chapter, finding, or recommendation was shaped by commercial interest. The book's stance, honest about both the promise and limitations of technology, critical of greenwashing and corporate overreach, grounded in peer-reviewed evidence and institutional data, reflects independent editorial judgment throughout.

This transparency about funding is itself part of the book's commitment to honesty. Readers deserve to know who supported the work, and to judge its integrity on the evidence presented. The same standard of disclosure that this book demands of corporations, governments, and research institutions applies to its own production.

I am grateful to AW3 for the intellectual freedom to follow the evidence without constraint, including into territory that is uncomfortable for any company operating in the technology sector. The book's treatment of data center energy consumption, electronic waste, algorithmic bias, and the environmental costs of digital infrastructure reflects that freedom.

The four people whose stories thread through these pages, Katharine Hayhoe, Will Marshall, Brandon Dennison, and Rikki Held, gave generously of their time and their trust. Their willingness to let their work and their lives be examined in detail, including the uncertainties and contradictions, made this a better book.

Any errors that remain are mine.
