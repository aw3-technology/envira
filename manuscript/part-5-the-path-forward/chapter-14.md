# Chapter 14: The Reckoning
## Fossil Fuels, Finance, and the Forces Arrayed Against Change

**Part V: The Path Forward: Strategy, Action, and the Critical Decade**

---

Seven and a half trillion dollars. That is the International Monetary Fund's estimate of global fossil fuel subsidies in 2024, including both direct budgetary support and the unpriced costs of air pollution, climate damage, and other externalities, equivalent to 5.8 percent of global GDP.^1 In the same period, global clean energy investment reached approximately $2.2 trillion in 2025, a record figure that was still less than half the $4.5 trillion per year needed to reach net-zero emissions by 2050.^2 The arithmetic is stark: the world spends more than three times as much propping up the fuels driving the crisis as it invests in the technologies that could end it.

These two numbers, the $7.4 trillion in subsidies and the $2.3 trillion annual shortfall in clean energy investment, define the structural landscape this chapter maps. The technologies exist. The economics have shifted. The monitoring infrastructure described in Part II can now track emissions from individual facilities in near-real time. And yet the gap between what we can do and what we are doing continues to widen. That gap is not a failure of knowledge or capability. It is a product of power: entrenched financial interests, misaligned incentive structures, and political architectures that reward inaction over transformation.

The reader has already encountered these forces. In Part III, every solution chapter carried a "Tensions and Headwinds" section where the friction surfaced in context: grid bottlenecks choking renewable deployment, the moral hazard lurking behind carbon removal, the cost premiums for green steel and sustainable aviation fuel. In Part IV, the obstacles wore human faces: workers stranded in dying industries, communities excluded from the benefits of the transition, a culture still saturated with fossil fuel money. This chapter does not introduce new obstacles. It names the systemic patterns that connect them: the structural forces that make the distance between ambition and action so persistent, and so dangerous.

## What They Knew and When They Knew It

The fossil fuel industry's knowledge of climate change is not recent, not ambiguous, and not disputed. What has been disputed, fiercely, expensively, and for decades, is what should follow from that knowledge.

In 1977, James Black, a senior scientist at Exxon, informed the company's Management Committee that carbon dioxide from fossil fuel combustion was warming the planet. His 1978 internal presentation warned that humanity had a narrow window before difficult decisions about energy strategy would become unavoidable.^3 Exxon's response was not to change course but to build one of the most sophisticated corporate climate research programs in existence. Throughout the late 1970s and early 1980s, Exxon scientists published peer-reviewed papers, constructed climate models, and equipped the company's Esso Atlantic tanker with instruments to measure oceanic CO2 absorption. A 2023 study by Geoffrey Supran, Stefan Rahmstorf, and Naomi Oreskes, published in *Science*, analyzed Exxon's internal climate projections from this period and found them remarkably accurate: between 63 and 83 percent of the company's modeled projections correctly forecast the warming trajectory that has since been observed.^4

Exxon was not alone in understanding the science early. The American Petroleum Institute had commissioned the Robinson Report as early as 1968, which warned of potentially significant temperature changes from rising CO2 by the year 2000.^5 API's CO2 and Climate Task Force operated through the 1980s, producing internal assessments of climate risk. Shell's 1988 confidential report, "The Greenhouse Effect," acknowledged the threat and projected impacts broadly consistent with what has since been observed. A 1991 Shell educational film, *Climate of Concern*, described the risks of climate change with a candor that would vanish from the company's public communications within a few years.^6

The transition from internal acknowledgment to public obstruction came in the late 1980s and 1990s. The Global Climate Coalition, an industry lobbying group founded in 1989 and active until 2002, fought the Kyoto Protocol and publicly questioned climate science even as its own scientific advisors privately conceded that the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's conclusions were well-established.^7 The playbook was borrowed directly from the tobacco industry's decades-long disinformation strategy, as Naomi Oreskes and Erik Conway documented in their 2010 book *Merchants of Doubt*. In some cases, the same individuals and institutions migrated from defending tobacco to defending fossil fuels.^8

The investigative journalism that brought this corporate record to light deserves specific recognition. Inside Climate News, which won the 2013 Pulitzer Prize for National Reporting for its series on the Kalamazoo River oil spill, published the landmark 2015 investigation "Exxon: The Road Not Taken," by Neela Banerjee, Lisa Song, and David Hasemyer. Built on hundreds of internal Exxon documents obtained through litigation and archival research, the series was a 2016 Pulitzer finalist for Public Service.^9 Separately, sociologist Robert Brulle, at Drexel University and later Brown University, traced more than $900 million in dark money flowing to climate contrarian organizations between 2003 and 2018.^10 The disinformation was not spontaneous. It was industrialized.

The scale of ongoing political spending reinforces the point. According to OpenSecrets, the oil, gas, and coal industries spent $124.4 million on federal lobbying in 2022 alone. Cumulative fossil fuel lobbying expenditures exceeded $2 billion between 2000 and 2023.^11 Those figures do not include dark money, political action committee contributions, or the vast network of think tanks, the Heartland Institute, the Competitive Enterprise Institute, the American Legislative Exchange Council, that received industry funding to amplify doubt and delay. The $124 million spent on lobbying in a single year should be understood not as an expense but as an investment; one that has delivered extraordinary returns measured in delayed regulation, preserved subsidies, and deferred accountability.

## The Subsidy Machine

The $7 trillion figure from the IMF demands closer examination, because it reveals the architecture of fossil fuel entrenchment more clearly than any other single data point.

The IMF's 2025 working paper disaggregated the total into two components. Explicit subsidies, direct government spending on below-market fuel pricing, tax breaks for exploration and production, and other budgetary transfers, accounted for roughly $725 billion.^1 The remaining $6.7 trillion represented implicit subsidies: the unpriced costs of air pollution, climate damage, traffic congestion, and other externalities that fossil fuel consumption imposes on society but that producers and consumers do not pay for. Every ton of coal burned without a price on its particulate emissions shifts the health costs onto public hospitals and household budgets. Every barrel of oil consumed without a carbon price shifts the damage costs onto future generations and climate-vulnerable communities.

The subsidy is not a neutral feature of the economic landscape. It is an active distortion. When a solar installation in sub-Saharan Africa must compete against kerosene whose true costs are hidden by subsidies, the subsidy does not merely preserve the status quo; it actively suppresses the alternative. When a wind farm in Texas competes against natural gas whose methane leakage and downstream carbon damage carry no price, the playing field tilts toward the incumbent. The $7 trillion is not money left on the table. It is money used, year after year, to maintain the competitive position of fuels whose full costs, if honestly accounted, would make them uncompetitive in most applications where alternatives exist.

Brandon Dennison, the founder of Coalfield Development in southern West Virginia whom the reader met in Chapter 11, understands this dynamic from the ground level. In Wayne County and across the coalfields of Appalachia, Dennison has spent years building alternative economic pathways for communities where coal employment has collapsed: solar installation training, sustainable agriculture, heritage tourism, social enterprise. But his work proceeds against a policy backdrop that still directs billions in federal and state support toward fossil fuel extraction through percentage depletion allowances, intangible drilling cost deductions, and royalty structures that undervalue publicly owned mineral resources. Every dollar of subsidy that flows to sustain the old economy is a dollar that does not flow to build the new one. The workers Dennison trains compete for investment in a system that has not stopped subsidizing the industries that left their communities behind.

The G7 and G20 have pledged repeatedly to phase out inefficient fossil fuel subsidies. The first such pledge came in 2009, at the G20 summit in Pittsburgh. More than fifteen years later, global fossil fuel subsidies are higher than they were when the pledge was made.^12 The persistence is not accidental. Fuel subsidies are popular with consumers, particularly in developing nations where energy costs consume a large share of household income. Governments that attempt to remove them face public backlash, as Nigeria, Ecuador, Iran, and others have learned through fuel-price protests that have, in some cases, toppled administrations. The politics of subsidy reform are treacherous precisely because the subsidies, however economically inefficient, deliver tangible short-term benefits to identifiable constituencies, while the costs they impose are diffuse, long-term, and borne disproportionately by people who are not yet born.

## The Investment Gap

The International Energy Agency estimates that achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 requires approximately $4.5 trillion per year in clean energy investment by 2030.^2 As of 2025, actual clean energy investment flows stood at roughly $2.2 trillion annually, a record figure, and yet still less than half of what the IEA pathway demands. The $2.3 trillion annual shortfall is not a static number. It compounds. Every year that clean energy deployment falls short, the cumulative emissions budget shrinks, adaptation costs rise, and the eventual price of the transition increases. Delayed investment is not deferred investment. It is more expensive investment, or, past certain tipping points, investment that can no longer undo what has already been set in motion.

The gap is widest where the need is most urgent. The Songwe-Stern Commission, co-chaired by Vera Songwe and Nicholas Stern, estimated that developing countries other than China would need approximately $2.4 trillion per year in climate-related investment by 2030, a figure that dwarfs current flows to these economies.^13 Sub-Saharan Africa, which accounts for a small fraction of historical emissions but faces some of the most severe climate impacts, receives a vanishingly small share of global clean energy investment.

Several structural forces sustain this disparity. The first is the cost of capital. Many developing countries carry sovereign credit ratings that make project finance prohibitively expensive, even when the underlying renewable energy economics are favorable. The cost of capital for a solar project in sub-Saharan Africa can be two to four times higher than for an identical project in Europe, not because the solar resource is inferior, but because financial markets price in political risk, currency volatility, and weak institutional frameworks. A solar panel generates the same electrons in Nairobi as in Munich. The financing that makes it possible does not.

The second is institutional design. The multilateral development banks, the World Bank, the regional development banks, were built for a different era and a different pace of investment. Their capital structures, risk appetites, and approval processes were not engineered for the speed and scale the climate crisis demands. Reforms are underway. The Bridgetown Initiative championed by Barbados Prime Minister Mia Mottley called for restructuring international financial institutions to better serve climate-vulnerable nations, and the World Bank's Evolution Roadmap launched in 2023 represents a genuine attempt to expand the institution's lending capacity, but institutional momentum is a formidable force, and the transformation required exceeds what incremental reform can deliver.

The third is risk absorption. Many of the most critical investments, including grid infrastructure, adaptation measures, and nature-based solutions, have risk-return profiles that private capital will not accept without public de-risking. Blended finance structures, first-loss guarantees, and concessional lending can bridge the gap, but the public capital needed to catalyze private flows remains far below the required scale.

The climate finance architecture, the Green Climate Fund, the Loss and Damage fund agreed at COP28 in Dubai, bilateral pledges, remains dramatically underfunded. Wealthy nations pledged $100 billion per year in climate finance for developing countries; they did not consistently meet even that modest target until 2022, years late.^14 The inadequacy of the response is not disputed by anyone who has examined the numbers. It is merely tolerated.

## The Political Economy of Inaction

Subsidies, investment shortfalls, and corporate disinformation are not independent phenomena. They are outputs of a political economy that systematically rewards short-term extraction over long-term sustainability.

Carbon pricing, the mechanism most economists favor for internalizing the cost of emissions, covers roughly 24 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions as of 2024.^15 The coverage is uneven and the prices, in most jurisdictions, are far too low to drive transformative change. The European Union's Emissions Trading System, the most mature carbon market, saw prices rise above 100 euros per ton in early 2023, high enough to begin shifting investment decisions in the power sector. But the vast majority of the world's emissions carry no price at all. Where carbon taxes exist, they are frequently undermined by exemptions, rebates, or parallel fossil fuel subsidies that neutralize the price signal.

Regulatory capture compounds the problem. The revolving door between the fossil fuel industry and the government agencies charged with overseeing it is well-documented across multiple jurisdictions. In the United States, senior officials from the American Petroleum Institute and major oil companies have cycled through the Department of Energy, the Environmental Protection Agency, and Congressional committees with jurisdiction over energy policy. The result is not always overt corruption. More commonly, it is a structural alignment of assumptions: the unstated belief that energy policy must prioritize existing producers, that regulation should avoid disruption, that the transition can proceed at whatever pace the incumbents find comfortable. This is the most insidious form of obstruction, because it operates within the ordinary functioning of institutions, shaping which options are considered realistic and which are dismissed before the conversation begins. On February 12, 2026, the EPA formally repealed the 2009 endangerment finding that had classified greenhouse gases as a threat to public health and welfare under the Clean Air Act, the legal foundation for federal climate regulation in the United States. The repeal dismantled four decades of regulatory architecture and signaled that captured institutions can not only delay action but reverse it.

The Paris Agreement, adopted in December 2015, established the aspirational framework: limit global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius (3.6 degrees Fahrenheit) above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit the increase to 1.5 degrees Celsius (2.7 degrees Fahrenheit). The mechanism for achieving this, nationally determined contributions, or NDCs, was deliberately designed to respect national sovereignty. Each country sets its own targets, on its own timeline, with no binding enforcement. The result is a structural gap between ambition and implementation. As of the latest UNEP Emissions Gap Report, the aggregate effect of all current NDCs, even if fully implemented, would leave the world on track for approximately 2.5 to 2.9 degrees Celsius of warming by 2100.^16 Full implementation itself is uncertain: many countries are not on track to meet their existing pledges.

The quarterly earnings cycle adds another layer of distortion. Publicly traded energy companies operate under relentless pressure to deliver short-term returns. A CEO who redirects capital from profitable oil and gas operations toward renewable energy investments with longer payback periods faces immediate punishment from equity markets. When BP announced in February 2023 that it would scale back its emissions reduction targets, reducing its planned 2030 oil and gas production cut from 40 percent to 25 percent — its stock price rose.^17 The market rewarded retreat from climate ambition. When Orsted, the Danish company that completed a genuine transformation from fossil fuels to offshore wind, faced headwinds from rising interest rates and supply chain costs in 2023 and 2024, its stock price fell sharply.^18 The market punished commitment. The signal these episodes transmit to every boardroom in the energy sector is unambiguous: financial markets, as currently structured, discount the future at rates that are incompatible with climate stability.

The discourse of delay has evolved alongside these structural forces. Outright climate denial, once the industry's primary strategy, has given way to subtler forms of obstruction. Researchers William Lamb and colleagues, in a 2020 study published in *Global Sustainability*, identified a taxonomy of delay arguments: redirecting responsibility ("consumers should change their behavior"), promoting non-transformative solutions ("natural gas as a bridge fuel"), emphasizing the downsides of action ("renewables kill jobs"), and surrendering to inevitability ("it's too late anyway").^19 These arguments do not reject the science. They accept the science and argue against doing anything meaningful in response. They are, in many ways, more dangerous than outright denial, because they operate within the bounds of reasonable-sounding discourse while producing the same result: inaction.

Subsidies, the revolving door, the NDC gap, short-termism in capital markets, and the evolving rhetoric of delay are not separate problems. They are expressions of the same underlying dynamic: political and economic systems that respond to concentrated interests more readily than to diffuse ones. The fossil fuel industry possesses concentrated financial power, concentrated political access, and concentrated organizational capacity. The beneficiaries of climate action, future generations, the global poor, ecosystems, possess none of these. This asymmetry is the engine of policy failure.

## Stranded Assets and the Financial Reckoning

The divestment movement offered one form of financial challenge. Bill McKibben's 2012 *Rolling Stone* article introduced the concept of "unburnable carbon" to a mass audience, translating the carbon budget into a financial argument: the fossil fuel reserves already on company balance sheets exceeded the remaining carbon budget by a factor of five, meaning most of those assets could never be safely burned.^20 By 2024, over 1,600 institutions representing more than $40 trillion in total assets under management had made some form of fossil fuel divestment commitment.^21 Harvard University's September 2021 announcement that its $53.2 billion endowment would divest from fossil fuels marked a symbolic turning point.^22

Mark Carney, then Governor of the Bank of England, gave shape to the financial establishment's engagement with these risks in his September 2015 speech at Lloyd's of London, "Breaking the Tragedy of the Horizon." The core argument was temporal: the catastrophic effects of climate change would manifest beyond the standard planning horizons of firms, regulators, and central bankers, creating a systematic underpricing of risk. By the time the market fully recognized the danger, it would be too late for an orderly adjustment.^23 The speech moved the stranded assets concept from activist rhetoric into the language of central banking and financial regulation. When the IEA concluded in its 2021 Net Zero by 2050 roadmap that no new oil and gas fields were needed beyond projects already committed, the finding implied that billions of dollars in planned exploration and development spending could become worthless.^24

Yet divestment and risk disclosure alone have not broken the structural power of the fossil fuel industry. The Engine No. 1 proxy fight at ExxonMobil in May 2021, in which a small activist hedge fund successfully placed three climate-focused directors on the board, with support from BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street, demonstrated that shareholder activism could succeed even at the largest fossil fuel companies.^25 But such victories remain exceptions. Carbon markets, designed to put a price on emissions, have in some cases become instruments of delay rather than transformation. A joint investigation published by *The Guardian*, *Die Zeit*, and SourceMaterial in January 2023 found that over 90 percent of Verra's rainforest offset credits, Verra being the world's largest carbon credit certifier, did not represent genuine emission reductions.^26 Companies purchasing these credits could claim progress toward net-zero targets without actually reducing their emissions. The offset market, intended to channel capital toward conservation, had in practice become a mechanism for deferring the structural changes that decarbonization requires. This is the dynamic that Chapter 7 identified as moral hazard in the context of carbon removal: the risk that the existence of a market or technological fix becomes an alibi for avoiding the harder work of systemic change.

The documented record matters because it establishes a pattern that extends beyond any single company or any single market failure: an industry that understood the science, funded the doubt, and invested decades of effort in ensuring that political and economic systems remained aligned with continued extraction. The courts have now begun to adjudicate that record. What happened in a courtroom in Montana suggested that the era of consequence-free obstruction may be drawing to a close.

## The Courtroom: Held v. Montana

Rikki Held was sixteen years old when she became a plaintiff. The daughter of a cattle ranching family near Broadus, a town of fewer than 300 people in the southeastern corner of Montana, she had grown up on land her family had worked for generations: wide, rolling rangeland along the Powder River, where cottonwoods line the creek bottoms and pronghorn move across grassland that stretches to the horizon. It was not an abstract relationship with the environment. It was the daily, physical reality of raising cattle, fixing fence, and depending on weather patterns that determined whether the ranch would have a good year or a bad one.

By the time Held reached her teens, the bad years were coming more frequently. Summers brought wildfire smoke thick enough to keep the family indoors, the sun reduced to a dull orange disk behind a haze that irritated lungs and coated surfaces with fine ash. Drought years thinned the herd because the grass did not come in the way it once had. Streams that her family had relied on for livestock water ran lower than anyone could remember. The Yellowstone River, which drains the watershed near the family's ranch, experienced record-low flows. Spring runoff patterns shifted as snowpack declined in the mountains upstream. These were not statistics to Held. They were the conditions of her daily life, observed across years, discussed at the kitchen table, measured against the memories of parents and grandparents who knew what the land had been.

In 2020, Held and fifteen other young Montanans, ranging in age from five to twenty-two, filed suit against the State of Montana, represented by Our Children's Trust, a nonprofit legal organization that had been pursuing youth climate litigation in multiple states. The central argument was that the state's policy of promoting fossil fuel development without considering climate impacts violated the right to a "clean and healthful environment" guaranteed by Article II, Section 3, and Article IX, Section 1, of Montana's 1972 constitution. Montana is one of only a handful of states whose constitutions include explicit environmental rights provisions, a legacy of the 1972 constitutional convention, where delegates, many of them concerned about the mining industry's impact on Montana's landscape, wrote environmental protections into the state's foundational law.

The case, *Held v. State of Montana*, went to trial in June 2023 in the First Judicial District Court in Helena, before Judge Kathy Seeley. It was the first constitutional climate trial in United States history. Over the course of the proceedings, the young plaintiffs testified about the specific harms they had experienced: asthma attacks triggered by wildfire smoke, anxiety about the future of landscapes they loved, disruptions to outdoor activities and livelihoods that depended on stable environmental conditions. Expert witnesses presented scientific testimony linking Montana's fossil fuel emissions to measurable climate change and its local consequences.

Montana's defense was revealing in what it chose not to contest. The state's attorneys did not seriously challenge the underlying science. Instead, they argued that Montana's greenhouse gas emissions, roughly 166 million tons per year, were too small to matter in the context of global climate change. The argument was a familiar one, echoed by polluters and governments worldwide: our share is negligible, so we bear no responsibility. It was the logic of collective inaction distilled to its essence, the same logic that, if accepted, would excuse every emitter on Earth, since no single source constitutes a majority of global emissions.

On August 14, 2023, Judge Seeley issued a 103-page ruling that rejected Montana's reasoning on every significant count. The court found that Montana's greenhouse gas emissions are "a substantial factor" in climate change, that the young plaintiffs had standing because they were already experiencing concrete harms, and that a provision of the Montana Environmental Policy Act that specifically barred state agencies from considering climate impacts when permitting fossil fuel projects was unconstitutional.^27

The ruling did not order Montana to reduce emissions directly. What it did was dismantle the legal architecture that had allowed the state to approve fossil fuel development while systematically excluding climate consequences from the decision-making process. The state appealed, moving the case to the Montana Supreme Court. But the precedent had been established: a court had examined the evidence, weighed the science, and concluded that constitutional rights extend to a stable climate.

*Held v. Montana* was not an isolated event. In the Netherlands, the Hague District Court ruled in May 2021 in *Milieudefensie v. Royal Dutch Shell* that Shell was required to reduce its global CO2 emissions by 45 percent by 2030 relative to 2019 levels, the first time a court imposed a binding emissions reduction obligation on a private corporation based on human rights law.^28 In April 2024, the European Court of Human Rights ruled in *KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland* that Switzerland's inadequate climate policies violated the European Convention on Human Rights, the first climate judgment by an international human rights tribunal.^29 The Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment at the London School of Economics counted more than 2,300 climate-related cases filed across more than 50 countries as of 2023.^30 On February 24, 2026, the United States Supreme Court agreed to hear *City of Boulder v. Suncor Energy* and a consolidated challenge by ExxonMobil, a decision that will determine whether municipalities can sue fossil fuel companies for climate damages in state courts or whether federal law preempts such claims. The case could shape the legal landscape for climate liability for a generation. The courtroom had become a venue for the accountability that legislative bodies had failed to deliver.

## Transparency as Counterforce

Will Marshall, the Planet Labs founder the reader met in Chapter 3, built the satellite infrastructure that can now image the entire land surface of Earth every day. In that earlier chapter, the focus was on the monitoring revolution itself: the technological achievement of deploying hundreds of shoebox-sized satellites in low Earth orbit, creating a daily scan of the planet at three-to-five-meter resolution. What matters in this chapter is what that capability means for accountability.

Satellite imagery has become a tool for verifying, or debunking, corporate environmental commitments. Organizations including Global Forest Watch, Chain Reaction Research, and Mighty Earth have used Planet Labs data and other satellite sources to track deforestation in real time, revealing that supply chains linked to major consumer brands continue to source commodities from recently cleared forests despite public zero-deforestation pledges.^31 The gap between corporate promise and operational reality, once obscurable behind the opacity of global supply chains, has become measurable from orbit.

Climate TRACE, the coalition described in Part II, has built on these foundations to create a facility-level global emissions inventory, using satellite data, artificial intelligence, and publicly available information to track greenhouse gas emissions from individual power plants, steel mills, oil and gas fields, and other major sources worldwide. The database encompasses tens of thousands of the world's largest emitting facilities, providing a level of granularity that country-level reporting never achieved. When a nation submits its emissions inventory to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, the reported figures can now be cross-referenced against independent satellite-derived estimates. The discrepancies, in some cases, are substantial.

This is the accountability function of the planetary nervous system described throughout this book. The same infrastructure that detects deforestation, tracks methane plumes, and monitors ice sheet dynamics also makes it increasingly difficult for governments and corporations to misrepresent their emissions. Transparency does not, by itself, compel action. A satellite image of illegal deforestation does not stop the chainsaw. An independently verified emissions figure does not force a power plant to close. But transparency changes the information environment in which political and legal decisions are made. Rikki Held's case drew on climate science that was, in part, observationally verified through the satellite and ground-based monitoring systems described in Part II. The offset investigations that exposed the failures of the voluntary carbon market relied on remote sensing to compare claimed forest protection with actual forest cover. The journalism that uncovered the fossil fuel industry's documented record of climate knowledge was amplified by a monitoring infrastructure that made the consequences of that knowledge visible in real time.

The nervous system does not solve the political problem. But it removes the excuse of ignorance.

## The System and What Must Change

The obstacles catalogued in this chapter are formidable. They are also, for the first time in the history of the climate crisis, no longer invisible. The investigative reporting of Inside Climate News and others brought the documented record of corporate knowledge and public deception into view. The satellite monitoring infrastructure built by Planet Labs and its peers has made corporate claims verifiable from orbit. The courtrooms where Rikki Held and the plaintiffs of *Milieudefensie v. Shell* brought their cases have begun to impose accountability where legislatures would not. The financial system itself is shifting, however unevenly: the concept of stranded assets has migrated from activist rhetoric to central bank risk assessment, and the divestment movement has grown from a handful of university endowments to institutions representing $40.5 trillion.

None of this erases the power of the obstacles. The $7.4 trillion in annual fossil fuel subsidies will not evaporate because a judge in Montana ruled that young people have constitutional climate rights. The quarterly earnings cycle will not pause because researchers at Harvard demonstrated that Exxon's own scientists predicted the warming we now observe. The investment gap will not close because a commission published a report quantifying it. The revolving door between industry and government will not stop turning because a taxonomy of delay has been published in an academic journal.

But naming these forces matters, because the structural nature of the obstacles determines the structural nature of the response required. Individual action, however necessary, cannot overcome a $7.4 trillion annual subsidy regime. Corporate commitments, however sincere, cannot survive a financial system that punishes long-term investment and rewards retreat from climate ambition. Technology deployment, however rapid, cannot outrun a political economy designed to protect incumbents. The obstacles are not separate from the system. They are the system, and the system is working exactly as its most powerful participants have designed it to work.

Short-termism, subsidies, the revolving door, financialized carbon markets, the discourse of delay: these are not failures of individual actors. They are features of a system that optimizes for quarterly returns and electoral cycles in a world that requires generational commitments. The fossil fuel industry did not create this system alone. But it has invested enormous resources, the $124 million in annual lobbying, the $900 million in dark money, the strategic cultivation of doubt across decades, in ensuring that the system remains intact. The expenditures are rational from the perspective of companies whose balance sheets depend on continued extraction.

What follows, then, is not a list of things to hope for. It is an assessment of what must change, in policy, in markets, in institutions, and in the exercise of civic power, to close the gap between what we can do and what we are doing. The obstacles are named. The next chapter asks what the critical decade from 2025 to 2035 demands.
