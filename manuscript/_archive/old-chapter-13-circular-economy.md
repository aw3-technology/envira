## Opening Scene

**Event:** The EU's "Right to Repair" directive and the broader circular economy legislative package
**Time:** 2024, Brussels

The scene opens with the European Parliament's adoption of the Right to Repair directive — a landmark regulation requiring manufacturers to repair consumer products (washing machines, vacuum cleaners, smartphones) for a reasonable price even after the warranty expires, and banning practices that prevent independent repair. This directive is one piece of a broader circular economy legislative package that also includes the Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (requiring products to be designed for durability, repairability, and recyclability) and revised waste and packaging regulations.

Describe the significance: the linear economy — extract, make, use, dispose — is not just environmentally destructive, it's economically wasteful. The Ellen MacArthur Foundation estimates that only 7.2% of the global economy is circular (materials recycled or reused). The remaining 92.8% is either dissipated, degraded, or locked in long-lived stocks (buildings, infrastructure). The waste sector accounts for ~3.2% of global GHG emissions (~1.6 GtCO2e/year), primarily methane from landfills. But the full impact of the linear economy is much larger: extraction, processing, and manufacturing of materials account for roughly 45% of global CO2 emissions when you include the energy and industrial processes involved.

Frame the chapter: the circular economy isn't just about recycling — it's a systemic redesign of how materials flow through the economy. Reduce, reuse, repair, remanufacture, recycle — in that order of priority. The technologies and policies to enable this transformation are advancing rapidly, from AI-powered waste sorting to chemical recycling of plastics to product-as-a-service business models.

**Narrative tone:** Systems-level thinking. Connect individual waste streams to the macro picture of material flows and emissions.

## Section 1: The Plastics Crisis
### 400 Million Tons Per Year and Growing

**Content Notes:**

- **Scale:** Global plastic production: ~400 million metric tons per year and projected to double by 2040 and triple by 2060 under current trends (OECD). Only ~9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled. ~12% has been incinerated. The remaining ~79% has accumulated in landfills or the environment.
- **Climate impact:** Plastics production and incineration generate ~1.8 GtCO2e/year (~3.4% of global emissions). Nearly all plastic is made from fossil fuel feedstocks (ethylene, propylene from oil and gas). The petrochemical industry sees plastics as a key growth market as transportation fuel demand declines — making plastics a major battleground for fossil fuel industry transition.
- **Ocean plastic:** An estimated 8-10 million metric tons of plastic enter the oceans annually. By 2050, under current trends, there could be more plastic than fish (by weight) in the ocean (Ellen MacArthur Foundation).
- **Solutions landscape:**
  - **Mechanical recycling:** Mature technology, but limited: only works well for clean, single-polymer streams (primarily PET and HDPE). Contamination, mixed materials, and degradation with each cycle limit effective recycling rates. Most "recycled" plastic is actually downcycled into lower-quality products.
  - **Chemical (advanced) recycling:** Technologies that break plastic down to its molecular components for reprocessing into virgin-quality material. Pyrolysis, gasification, depolymerization. Companies: Plastic Energy, PureCycle Technologies, Eastman Chemical. Promise: handle contaminated and mixed plastic waste that mechanical recycling can't. Reality: most chemical recycling facilities have faced significant technical and economic challenges. Energy intensity is high. Honest reporting of both promise and limitations is essential.
  - **Biodegradable and compostable alternatives:** PLA (polylactic acid), PHA (polyhydroxyalkanoates), and other bio-based polymers. Challenges: most "biodegradable" plastics only degrade in industrial composting facilities (60°C+, controlled conditions), not in the ocean or landfills. Confusion and greenwashing are rampant. Some applications make sense (food service, agricultural mulch); others are questionable.
  - **Reduction and reuse systems:** Refill stations, returnable packaging (Loop by TerraCycle), deposit-return schemes for bottles. Reduction is the highest-leverage intervention but faces consumer behavior barriers.
- **UN Global Plastics Treaty:** Negotiations for a legally binding international instrument on plastic pollution, expected to conclude in 2024-2025. Would set global rules on plastic production, design, waste management, and potentially cap virgin plastic production.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global plastic production: ~400 Mt/year, projected to double by 2040 (source: OECD Global Plastics Outlook)
- Only ~9% of all plastic ever recycled (source: Science Advances, Geyer et al. 2017)
- Plastic emissions: ~1.8 GtCO2e/year (source: OECD)
- Ocean plastic pollution: ~8-10 Mt/year entering oceans (source: Science, Jambeck et al. 2015)
- UN Global Plastics Treaty: negotiations ongoing, expected completion 2024-2025 (source: UNEP)

**Profile: PureCycle Technologies**
- PureCycle licenses technology from Procter & Gamble to purify and recycle polypropylene (PP) — the second most common plastic — back to virgin-quality resin.
- First commercial facility in Ironton, Ohio, began operations in 2023.
- The challenge: demonstrating that advanced recycling can work reliably at commercial scale and competitive cost. Early operations have faced production ramp-up difficulties — report honestly.
- Narrative angle: the promise and difficulty of closing the loop on one of the world's most ubiquitous materials.

## Section 2: E-Waste and Critical Minerals
### The Hidden Cost of the Digital Age

**Content Notes:**

- **Scale:** Global e-waste generation: ~62 million metric tons in 2022 (UN Global E-waste Monitor) — the fastest-growing waste stream in the world, growing at ~2.5 Mt/year. Only ~22% is formally collected and recycled. The rest is landfilled, incinerated, or exported to developing countries where informal recycling under hazardous conditions is common.
- **The critical minerals connection:** E-waste contains valuable metals essential for the energy transition: lithium, cobalt, nickel, rare earth elements, copper, gold, silver. A ton of circuit boards contains 40-800 times more gold than a ton of gold ore. "Urban mining" — recovering materials from e-waste — is increasingly economically attractive and essential for reducing dependence on primary mining.
- **Battery recycling:** As millions of EV batteries reach end-of-life (starting in the late 2020s), battery recycling becomes critical. Companies: Redwood Materials (JB Straubel, Tesla co-founder), Li-Cycle, Cirba Solutions. Redwood Materials can recover 95%+ of critical minerals (nickel, cobalt, lithium, copper) from spent batteries and manufacture them back into battery materials. This creates a closed loop that reduces mining demand and supply chain risk.
- **Legislation:** EU Battery Regulation (2023): mandates minimum recycled content in new batteries (16% cobalt, 6% lithium, 6% nickel by 2031, increasing to 26%, 12%, 15% by 2036). Also requires collection targets and recycling efficiency rates.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global e-waste: ~62 Mt in 2022, growing 2.5 Mt/year (source: UN Global E-waste Monitor 2024)
- Only ~22% formally collected and recycled (source: UN)
- Redwood Materials: recovers 95%+ of critical minerals from batteries (source: Redwood Materials)
- EU Battery Regulation: minimum recycled content mandates (source: European Commission)

**Profile: Redwood Materials (JB Straubel, CEO)**
- JB Straubel, co-founder and former CTO of Tesla, founded Redwood Materials in 2017 to solve the battery recycling challenge before it becomes a crisis.
- Operating one of the largest battery recycling facilities in the U.S. (Nevada), with a second major facility under construction in South Carolina.
- The thesis: creating a domestic closed-loop battery supply chain reduces dependence on foreign mining, lowers costs, and dramatically reduces the environmental footprint of EV batteries.
- Narrative angle: the Tesla co-founder who realized that making electric cars sustainable requires making their batteries circular.

## Section 3: Food Waste
### One-Third of All Food, Lost or Wasted

**Content Notes:**

- **Scale:** Approximately one-third of all food produced globally (~1.3 billion tons/year) is lost or wasted (FAO). Food waste in landfills decomposes anaerobically, producing methane — making it a significant contributor to the waste sector's GHG emissions. The IPCC estimates that food waste accounts for 8-10% of global GHG emissions when you include the entire lifecycle (production, transport, storage, disposal).
- **Solutions:**
  - **Reduction:** Better supply chain management, cold chain infrastructure (particularly in developing countries where up to 40% of food is lost post-harvest), demand forecasting, ugly produce programs (Imperfect Foods, Misfits Market), consumer education.
  - **Redistribution:** Food banks, surplus food platforms (Too Good To Go, OLIO) that connect surplus food with consumers.
  - **Composting and anaerobic digestion:** Diverting food waste from landfills to composting (aerobic, produces soil amendment) or anaerobic digestion (produces biogas — renewable methane — and digestate fertilizer). San Francisco's mandatory composting program. Denmark's anaerobic digestion infrastructure.
  - **Insect farming:** Companies like InnovaFeed and Protix use black soldier fly larvae to convert food waste into animal feed protein and organic fertilizer — a biological closed loop.
- **Policy:** France's food waste hierarchy law (2016): mandates that supermarkets donate unsold food to charities rather than discarding it. EU's Farm to Fork Strategy includes a target to halve per capita food waste at retail and consumer levels by 2030.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global food loss/waste: ~1.3 billion tons/year, one-third of production (source: FAO)
- Food waste GHG impact: 8-10% of global emissions (lifecycle) (source: IPCC, Project Drawdown)
- Too Good To Go: saved 300+ million meals as of 2024 (source: company data)
- Project Drawdown ranks reducing food waste as the #1 climate solution by emissions impact (source: Drawdown)

## Section 4: The Systemic Shift
### From Linear to Circular — Design, Policy, and Business Models

**Content Notes:**

- **Circularity metrics:** The Circularity Gap Report (Circle Economy) finds the global economy is only 7.2% circular — and this number has been declining as material extraction outpaces recycling.
- **Design for circularity:** The biggest lever is at the design stage. Products designed for disassembly, repair, and material recovery are far easier to circularize. Modular design (Fairphone), material passports (documenting all materials in a product for end-of-life recovery), standardized components.
- **Product-as-a-service:** Shifting from ownership to service models — Philips leasing lighting as a service (paying per lux, not per lightbulb), Michelin leasing tires by the kilometer. These models incentivize manufacturers to design for durability and take-back because they retain ownership.
- **Digital tools:** AI-powered waste sorting (AMP Robotics uses computer vision and robotic arms to sort recyclables 2x faster and more accurately than humans). Blockchain for material traceability. Digital product passports (the EU's digital product passport requirement under the Ecodesign Regulation).
- **Extended producer responsibility (EPR):** Policies that make producers financially responsible for the end-of-life management of their products. EPR systems for packaging are now standard in the EU and spreading globally.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global circularity rate: 7.2% (and declining) (source: Circle Economy, Circularity Gap Report 2024)
- AMP Robotics: AI-powered sorting, 2x speed, fewer contamination errors (source: AMP Robotics)
- Fairphone: modular smartphone designed for 5+ year lifespan and easy repair (source: Fairphone)
- EU Digital Product Passport: required under Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (source: European Commission)

## Chapter Summary and Transition

Close by connecting the circular economy to the climate math: materials account for a massive share of emissions when you trace the full lifecycle from extraction to disposal. Every ton of material kept in circulation is a ton not mined, smelted, shipped, and landfilled. The circular economy is not an alternative to decarbonization — it is a necessary complement. The technologies to enable it (AI sorting, chemical recycling, digital passports) are maturing. The policies to mandate it (EPR, right to repair, recycled content mandates) are spreading. The remaining challenge is speed: bending a 200-year-old linear industrial model toward circularity in a single generation.

Transition to Chapter 14: While the circular economy addresses what we make and discard, a more radical set of interventions is being debated: deliberate manipulation of the climate system itself. Geoengineering and solar radiation management represent the most controversial frontier of the climate response.
