## Opening Scene

**Event:** MethaneSAT's first detection sweep, March 2024
**Time:** Spring 2024, Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics and Environmental Defense Fund

The scene opens with the launch and first operational data from MethaneSAT, a satellite purpose-built to detect and quantify methane emissions globally with unprecedented precision. Funded by the Environmental Defense Fund and developed in partnership with Harvard, MethaneSAT represents a new era: the ability to see methane leaks from space, anywhere on Earth, with enough resolution to identify individual facilities and hold emitters accountable.

Describe the significance: for decades, methane was the overlooked greenhouse gas. CO2 dominates the long-term warming picture, but methane is responsible for roughly 30% of global warming since pre-industrial times. Over a 20-year period, methane traps approximately 80 times more heat than CO2. Yet unlike CO2, which persists in the atmosphere for centuries, methane breaks down in about 12 years. This short atmospheric lifetime creates a unique opportunity: rapid methane cuts could slow the rate of warming within a decade — buying critical time while the longer, harder work of CO2 reduction proceeds.

Use MethaneSAT as the entry point to explore the methane problem, the technology revolution in detection, the policy momentum (Global Methane Pledge), and the realistic pathways to deep methane cuts.

**Narrative tone:** Urgent and action-oriented. Methane is the fastest lever we have on warming, and the tools to pull it are arriving now.

## Section 1: The Methane Problem
### 30% of Warming, Hidden in Plain Sight

**Content Notes:**

- **Scale of the problem:** Global methane emissions are approximately 580 million metric tons per year. Roughly 60% is anthropogenic (human-caused), with the remainder from natural sources (wetlands, termites, ocean sediments).
- **Three major anthropogenic sources:**
  1. **Fossil fuels (~35% of anthropogenic methane):** Oil and gas production, processing, transmission, and distribution. Coal mining. Leaks, venting, and flaring throughout the supply chain. The IEA estimates that the oil and gas industry alone emits ~120 Mt of methane annually — and that 75% of these emissions could be eliminated with existing technology, with ~40% at zero net cost (because captured methane can be sold).
  2. **Agriculture (~40% of anthropogenic methane):** Enteric fermentation (livestock digestion — primarily cattle), rice paddies, and manure management. Approximately 1 billion cattle globally produce methane through their digestive process. Rice paddies, when flooded, create anaerobic conditions that generate methane.
  3. **Waste (~20% of anthropogenic methane):** Landfills and wastewater treatment. Organic waste decomposing in landfills without oxygen produces methane. Many landfills vent methane directly or flare it inefficiently.
- **The 80x multiplier:** Methane's Global Warming Potential (GWP) over 20 years is approximately 80 times that of CO2. Over 100 years, it's ~28-30 times. The choice of timeframe matters enormously for policy. Given the urgency of the next two decades, the 20-year GWP is increasingly cited.
- **Methane's acceleration:** Atmospheric methane concentrations have been rising sharply since 2007 after a brief plateau (2000-2007). Current levels: ~1,925 ppb (parts per billion), up from ~722 ppb pre-industrial. The cause of the post-2007 acceleration is debated — likely a combination of expanding tropical wetlands (climate feedback), increased fossil fuel production, and growing livestock numbers.

**Key Data Points:**
- Methane responsible for ~0.5°C of the ~1.1°C warming since pre-industrial (source: IPCC AR6, UNEP Global Methane Assessment 2021)
- Global anthropogenic methane emissions: ~380 Mt/year (source: Global Methane Budget 2020, Global Carbon Project)
- Atmospheric methane: ~1,925 ppb, 2.5x pre-industrial levels (source: NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory)
- Oil & gas methane: ~120 Mt/year; 75% abatable with existing technology (source: IEA Methane Tracker 2024)
- Methane GWP-20: ~80x CO2; GWP-100: ~28-30x CO2 (source: IPCC AR6)

## Section 2: The Detection Revolution
### Satellites, Sensors, and the End of Invisible Leaks

**Content Notes:**

- **The transparency transformation:** Until recently, methane emissions were largely estimated from bottom-up inventories (counting equipment, applying emission factors). These inventories systematically underestimate actual emissions — often by a factor of 1.5-3x. Top-down measurements from aircraft and satellites have revealed that actual methane emissions from oil and gas operations are far higher than industry-reported figures.
- **Satellite detection constellation:**
  - **MethaneSAT (EDF):** Launched 2024. Designed to measure methane emissions at facility scale globally, with a 200km swath and sensitivity to detect emissions as low as 2-3 tons/hour. Free, publicly available data. A game-changer for transparency and accountability.
  - **TROPOMI (ESA/Copernicus Sentinel-5P):** Operational since 2018. Measures atmospheric methane columns globally. Lower resolution than MethaneSAT but provides daily global coverage. Has identified numerous "ultra-emitter" events worldwide.
  - **GHGSat (private):** Commercial satellite constellation providing high-resolution methane imaging. Has identified thousands of individual methane sources. Sells data to companies and governments.
  - **Carbon Mapper (multi-stakeholder):** Launching dedicated satellites to detect and quantify methane and CO2 point sources globally.
- **The "super-emitter" revelation:** Satellite and aerial surveys have identified thousands of "super-emitter" events — individual facilities or equipment releasing enormous quantities of methane. A relatively small number of super-emitters account for a disproportionate share of total emissions. Many are easily fixable (e.g., stuck valves, open hatches, unlit flares).
- **Ground-level detection:** Continuous monitoring systems at well pads and processing plants (e.g., Project Canary, Qube Technologies). Drone-based detection for pipeline surveys. Handheld optical gas imaging cameras for inspection.

**Key Data Points:**
- MethaneSAT: 200km swath, sensitivity to 2-3 t/hr emissions, global coverage (source: MethaneSAT/EDF)
- TROPOMI: identified 1,500+ ultra-emitter events in 2022-2023 (source: Science, TROPOMI data analyses)
- Permian Basin (Texas/New Mexico) methane emissions: 2-3x higher than EPA estimates (source: multiple aerial/satellite studies published in Science, Nature)
- Top 50 super-emitter events detected by satellite in 2023 released >4 Mt methane (source: UNEP IMEO)

**Profile: Mark Brownstein, SVP, Environmental Defense Fund**
- Led EDF's methane strategy for over a decade, from early aerial surveys over U.S. oil fields to the conception and deployment of MethaneSAT.
- EDF's approach: use hard data and satellite transparency to make methane emissions visible, creating pressure on companies and governments alike.
- Narrative angle: the power of radical transparency — when you can see the problem from space, it becomes much harder to deny or ignore.

## Section 3: Policy Momentum
### The Global Methane Pledge and Regulatory Action

**Content Notes:**

- **Global Methane Pledge:** Launched at COP26 (Glasgow, November 2021) by the U.S. and EU, with over 150 countries now signed on. Commits signatories to collectively reduce global methane emissions by at least 30% from 2020 levels by 2030. If achieved, this could avoid up to 0.2°C of warming by the 2040s — a significant contribution.
  - Assessment: significant political momentum but implementation varies widely. Some countries have enacted strong regulations; others have done little beyond signing.
- **U.S. EPA methane rules:** In 2024, the EPA finalized new regulations requiring oil and gas facilities to monitor for and repair methane leaks, eliminate routine flaring, and phase out pneumatic controllers that vent methane. The "Super Emitter Response Program" allows approved third parties (including satellite operators) to report large leaks, triggering mandatory company response.
- **EU Methane Regulation:** The EU adopted comprehensive methane regulations in 2024 covering oil, gas, and coal sectors — including mandatory leak detection and repair (LDAR), limits on venting and flaring, and — critically — requirements applying to methane emissions from imported fossil fuels. This is significant because most of the EU's methane exposure comes from imported gas and oil.
- **IEA's abatement pathway:** The IEA estimates that global methane emissions from fossil fuel operations could be reduced by 75% using existing, proven technologies. Roughly 40% of these reductions would come at zero net cost because the captured methane can be sold. The main measures: leak detection and repair, replacing high-bleed pneumatic devices, capturing gas at well completions, eliminating routine venting and flaring.
- **Challenges:** Agricultural methane is harder — no equivalent of "plug the leak." Enteric fermentation solutions (feed additives like Bovaer/3-NOP, seaweed supplements) show promise in trials but face scaling challenges. Rice paddy management (alternate wetting and drying) can reduce emissions 30-50% but requires changes to centuries-old practices.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global Methane Pledge: 150+ countries, target 30% reduction from 2020 levels by 2030 (source: Global Methane Pledge Secretariat)
- 30% methane reduction by 2030 could avoid ~0.2°C warming by 2040s (source: UNEP Global Methane Assessment)
- EPA methane rules: cover ~1 million existing oil and gas sources in the U.S. (source: EPA)
- EU methane regulation: first-ever EU-wide rules on energy sector methane, including imports (source: European Council)
- IEA: 75% of fossil fuel methane abatable with existing tech; 40% at zero net cost (source: IEA Methane Tracker 2024)

## Section 4: The Realistic Pathway
### What Rapid Methane Cuts Could Achieve

**Content Notes:**

- **The time-value argument:** Because methane breaks down in ~12 years, cutting methane emissions delivers warming benefits within a decade. CO2 cuts, while essential, take much longer to affect temperatures because CO2 persists for centuries. Methane reduction is the fastest lever humanity has to slow the rate of warming in the near term.
- **Quantifying the prize:** UNEP's Global Methane Assessment (2021) found that available methane mitigation measures could reduce anthropogenic methane emissions by approximately 45% by 2030. This would avoid 0.3°C of warming by the 2040s and prevent approximately 255,000 premature deaths annually from ground-level ozone (methane is a precursor to tropospheric ozone, a harmful air pollutant).
- **Sector-by-sector pathways:**
  - **Fossil fuels:** The most tractable. Leak detection and repair, end routine flaring, replace pneumatic controllers, electrify compressors. 75% reduction technically possible with existing technology.
  - **Waste:** Landfill gas capture and utilization, organic waste diversion (composting, anaerobic digestion), improved wastewater treatment. Significant reductions achievable.
  - **Agriculture:** The hardest sector. Feed additives (3-NOP/Bovaer can reduce enteric methane 20-35%), selective breeding for lower-emission cattle, improved manure management (covered lagoons with biogas capture), alternate wetting and drying for rice. Progress is slower but real.
- **Methane removal:** Emerging research into atmospheric methane removal — catalytic or photocatalytic oxidation of methane in ambient air. Very early stage but could eventually complement emission reductions. Iron salt aerosols and zeolite-based catalysts are being studied.
- **The honest assessment:** Methane alone won't solve climate change. CO2 is still the dominant long-term driver. But methane reduction is the single most impactful near-term action available — a way to buy time while the harder structural changes in energy, industry, and agriculture take hold.

**Key Data Points:**
- 45% reduction in anthropogenic methane achievable by 2030 (source: UNEP Global Methane Assessment 2021)
- 0.3°C avoided warming by 2040s from available methane measures (source: UNEP)
- 255,000 premature deaths/year avoidable from reduced ground-level ozone (source: UNEP)
- Methane atmospheric lifetime: ~12 years (source: IPCC AR6)
- Bovaer (3-NOP) feed additive: reduces enteric methane 20-35% in cattle trials (source: DSM, peer-reviewed studies)

**Profile: Durwood Zaelke, President, Institute for Governance & Sustainable Development**
- A leading advocate for fast-action climate strategies, particularly methane and short-lived climate pollutants.
- Co-architect of the intellectual framework behind the Global Methane Pledge — the argument that cutting methane is the fastest way to slow warming.
- Narrative angle: the strategic case for speed — why cutting methane now matters as much as cutting CO2 over the long term.
