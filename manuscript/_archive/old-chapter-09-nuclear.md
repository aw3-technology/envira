## Opening Scene

**Event:** The restart of Unit 3 at Michigan's Palisades Nuclear Generating Station
**Time:** 2025, Lake Michigan shoreline, Covert Township, Michigan

The scene opens with the unprecedented decision to restart the Palisades nuclear plant — the first time in U.S. history that a closed nuclear reactor has been brought back online. Palisades was shut down in May 2022 by its owner, Entergy, citing unfavorable economics. Within months, the Inflation Reduction Act's nuclear production tax credits and a $1.52 billion conditional loan from the Department of Energy's Loan Programs Office changed the calculus entirely. Holtec International, which had purchased the plant for decommissioning, reversed course and began the complex process of restarting the 800-megawatt pressurized water reactor.

Describe the significance: Palisades is a symbol of nuclear power's turbulent position in the climate fight. Nuclear generates roughly 10% of global electricity and approximately 25% of the world's low-carbon electricity — more than wind and solar combined in many countries. Yet the industry has spent decades in decline in the West: aging plants closing, new builds running catastrophically over budget (Vogtle 3 & 4 in Georgia: $35 billion, over a decade late), and public anxiety never fully dissipated since Fukushima. Now the tide may be turning. Climate urgency, energy security concerns, AI data center demand, and new reactor designs are converging to create what the industry calls a "nuclear renaissance."

Frame the chapter's central question: can nuclear power — both existing fission plants and next-generation designs — scale fast enough to play the role that most credible net-zero pathways say it must?

**Narrative tone:** Clear-eyed and balanced. Nuclear is neither the silver bullet nor the boogeyman. It is a proven, scalable low-carbon energy source with real challenges that deserve honest treatment.

## Section 1: The Existing Fleet
### 440 Reactors and the Case for Life Extension

**Content Notes:**

- **Global nuclear fleet:** As of 2024, approximately 440 commercial nuclear reactors operate in 32 countries, providing roughly 370 GW of capacity and generating ~2,700 TWh/year — about 10% of global electricity. France leads in nuclear share (~65% of electricity), followed by Slovakia, Ukraine, Hungary, and others.
- **The carbon math:** Nuclear power has a lifecycle carbon intensity of approximately 5-12 gCO2/kWh — comparable to wind and lower than solar when manufacturing is included. The existing fleet avoids approximately 1.5 GtCO2/year in emissions compared to gas generation. Losing these plants would create an enormous hole in decarbonization efforts.
- **The closure crisis:** In the U.S., nuclear generation peaked in 2012 at about 770 TWh/year. Multiple plants have closed due to competition from cheap natural gas, market designs that don't value dispatchable low-carbon generation, and high operating costs for aging plants. Every nuclear plant that closes is typically replaced by natural gas, not renewables — increasing emissions.
- **Life extension and policy support:**
  - The Inflation Reduction Act's nuclear production tax credit ($15/MWh for existing plants meeting wage requirements) has dramatically improved the economics of operating nuclear plants. Several plants that were scheduled for closure have reversed course.
  - License extensions: the NRC has approved 20-year license renewals for most U.S. reactors, extending operating licenses from 40 to 60 years. Some operators are now seeking "subsequent license renewal" to 80 years.
  - France's "Grand Carene" program: investing billions to extend the life of its 56-reactor fleet, the backbone of one of the lowest-carbon electricity grids among major economies.
- **Japan's restart:** After Fukushima (2011), Japan shut down all 54 of its nuclear reactors. As of 2024, 12 have restarted under new safety standards, with more in the pipeline. Japan's government now explicitly supports nuclear as essential to its 2050 carbon neutrality goal.

**Key Data Points:**
- Global nuclear fleet: ~440 reactors, ~370 GW, ~2,700 TWh/year, ~10% of global electricity (source: IAEA PRIS)
- Nuclear lifecycle emissions: 5-12 gCO2/kWh (source: IPCC AR6, UNECE 2022)
- Existing fleet avoids ~1.5 GtCO2/year vs. gas replacement (source: IEA)
- U.S. IRA nuclear PTC: $15/MWh for qualifying existing plants (source: IRA text)
- Palisades restart: 800 MW, $1.52 billion DOE conditional loan (source: DOE Loan Programs Office)

**Profile: Palisades Nuclear Plant / Holtec International**
- Holtec International, led by CEO Kris Singh, purchased Palisades for decommissioning.
- The IRA's nuclear production tax credits and DOE loan support changed the economics, making restart viable.
- Narrative angle: the policy power of well-designed incentives — how a single piece of legislation reversed a closure decision and preserved 800 MW of carbon-free generation.

## Section 2: New Builds and the Cost Problem
### Why Nuclear Got So Expensive — And What's Being Done About It

**Content Notes:**

- **The Western cost crisis:** Nuclear power's biggest challenge in the West is not technology or safety — it's cost and construction time. Recent Western new builds have been catastrophically over budget:
  - **Vogtle 3 & 4 (Georgia, U.S.):** The only new nuclear units built in the U.S. in a generation. Original estimate: ~$14 billion, 2016 completion. Actual: ~$35 billion, Unit 3 operational 2023, Unit 4 operational 2024. The contractor (Westinghouse) went bankrupt during construction.
  - **Hinkley Point C (UK):** Two EPR reactors under construction in Somerset. Original estimate: ~$23 billion (2013). Current estimate: $38+ billion, with completion pushed to 2029-2031.
  - **Olkiluoto 3 (Finland):** Single EPR reactor. Construction began 2005, commercial operation 2023 — 18 years. Final cost: ~$12 billion, roughly 3x the original budget.
  - **Flamanville 3 (France):** Single EPR. Began 2007, fuel loading 2024. Cost: ~$14 billion, 4x original estimate.
- **Why the overruns?** First-of-a-kind (FOAK) penalty, loss of construction expertise and supply chain after decades without new builds, regulatory ratcheting, bespoke designs, project management failures, and a construction model that requires enormous upfront capital deployed over a decade.
- **The contrast: South Korea and China:** South Korea's APR-1400 reactors (Barakah in the UAE: four units, ~$24 billion, on schedule) and China's nuclear build program (150+ reactors planned, with multiple designs being built on schedule and near budget) demonstrate that nuclear can be built efficiently with standardized designs, maintained supply chains, and experienced workforces.
- **The cost paradox:** Nuclear's problem is not the cost of generating electricity (operating costs for existing plants are very competitive at ~$30/MWh). It's the cost and risk of construction — the massive upfront capital and the risk of multi-billion-dollar overruns that make financing difficult.

**Key Data Points:**
- Vogtle 3 & 4: ~$35 billion final cost, $14 billion original estimate (source: Georgia PSC filings)
- Hinkley Point C: $38+ billion current estimate, 2029-2031 completion (source: EDF)
- Barakah (UAE, Korean-built APR-1400): 4 units, ~$24 billion, largely on schedule (source: ENEC)
- China: 55+ operating reactors, 20+ under construction, targeting 150 GW by 2035 (source: World Nuclear Association)
- Nuclear operating cost: ~$30/MWh for existing plants (source: Lazard LCOE analysis)

## Section 3: Small Modular Reactors
### Can Factory-Built Reactors Change the Equation?

**Content Notes:**

- **The SMR concept:** Small Modular Reactors (typically defined as <300 MW, compared to ~1,000-1,600 MW for conventional reactors) aim to solve nuclear's cost problem through modular, factory-fabricated construction. Instead of building massive, bespoke plants on-site, SMR components would be manufactured in factories and assembled on-site — reducing construction time, cost, and risk.
- **Leading SMR designs:**
  - **NuScale Power (U.S.):** The first (and so far only) SMR design to receive NRC design certification (January 2023). NuScale's VOYGR design uses natural circulation (no pumps needed for cooling), with modules of 77 MW each, up to 12 modules per plant. However, the flagship Carbon Free Power Project (CFPP) at Idaho National Laboratory was cancelled in November 2023 after cost estimates rose from $5.3 billion to $9.3 billion (for 462 MW) — translating to over $20,000/kW, far exceeding conventional nuclear and most other generation sources. This is a significant setback for the SMR industry.
  - **X-energy (U.S.):** Developing the Xe-100, a high-temperature gas-cooled reactor (HTGR) using TRISO fuel — tiny fuel particles coated in layers of graphite and ceramic that can withstand extremely high temperatures, making meltdown physically impossible. Targeting 80 MW per module. Has a DOE Advanced Reactor Demonstration Program award. Partnership with Dow Chemical to provide process heat and electricity at an industrial site.
  - **Rolls-Royce SMR (UK):** Developing a 470 MW pressurized water reactor designed for factory manufacture. UK government has provided substantial funding. Aiming for first power in the early 2030s.
  - **Kairos Power (U.S.):** Developing a fluoride salt-cooled reactor. Received NRC construction permit for its Hermes test reactor in Oak Ridge, Tennessee — the first non-light-water reactor construction permit issued by the NRC in over 50 years.
- **The honest assessment:** SMRs are theoretically compelling but commercially unproven. No SMR has yet been built at the projected cost. The NuScale CFPP cancellation demonstrated that the "modular = cheap" thesis has not yet been validated. The core question remains: can factory production actually deliver the cost reductions promised? Russia's floating SMR (Akademik Lomonosov) and China's HTR-PM are operational examples, but at costs that are not yet competitive.

**Key Data Points:**
- NuScale VOYGR: 77 MW modules, NRC design certification January 2023 (source: NRC)
- CFPP cancellation: cost rose from $5.3B to $9.3B for 462 MW (source: UAMPS, NuScale filings)
- X-energy Xe-100: 80 MW HTGR, TRISO fuel, DOE ARDP award (source: DOE, X-energy)
- Kairos Hermes: first non-LWR construction permit in 50+ years (source: NRC)
- China HTR-PM: 210 MW demonstration HTGR, operational 2023 (source: China National Nuclear Corporation)

**Profile: Kairos Power (Mike Laufer, CEO)**
- Mike Laufer co-founded Kairos Power to develop a simpler, cheaper nuclear reactor using molten fluoride salt coolant — a technology with roots in Oak Ridge National Laboratory experiments in the 1960s.
- Received the first NRC construction permit for a non-light-water reactor since the 1960s, a milestone for advanced nuclear.
- Approach: rapid iteration, using non-nuclear prototypes to test systems before going nuclear — borrowing from software development methodology.
- Narrative angle: a startup mentality applied to nuclear, trying to break the cycle of cost overruns through radical simplification.

## Section 4: Nuclear and Net-Zero Pathways
### The Role Most Climate Models Require

**Content Notes:**

- **What the models say:** Most credible net-zero-by-2050 pathways (IEA, IPCC, national plans) include a significant role for nuclear power — typically requiring a doubling or tripling of global nuclear capacity. The IEA's Net Zero by 2050 scenario projects global nuclear capacity growing from ~370 GW today to ~800 GW by 2050. The IPCC's median pathway shows nuclear roughly doubling by mid-century.
- **Why nuclear in net-zero pathways?**
  - **Firm, dispatchable power:** Solar and wind are intermittent. Batteries handle short-duration variability (hours). But multi-day, multi-week low-renewable periods ("dunkelflaute") require firm, dispatchable generation. Nuclear provides 24/7 baseload power regardless of weather.
  - **Energy density:** Nuclear has the highest energy density of any source. A single 1 GW plant occupies ~1 square mile and produces as much electricity as ~3 million solar panels or ~400+ large wind turbines, making it critical where land is constrained.
  - **Grid stability:** Nuclear provides inertia and frequency regulation services that support grid stability — increasingly important as variable renewables grow.
  - **Industrial heat:** Advanced reactors (particularly HTGRs) can provide high-temperature process heat for industries that are extremely difficult to electrify — hydrogen production, chemical processing, desalination.
- **The complementarity argument:** Nuclear and renewables are not competitors but complements. Renewables provide the cheapest electrons when the sun shines and wind blows. Nuclear provides the reliable, weather-independent baseload. Storage bridges the gap. The optimal grid likely includes all three.
- **AI and data center demand:** The explosion of AI computing is driving enormous growth in electricity demand. Major tech companies (Microsoft, Google, Amazon) have signed nuclear power agreements or invested in nuclear development specifically to power data centers, recognizing that nuclear provides the 24/7 carbon-free power that data centers require and that renewables alone struggle to deliver at the necessary reliability.
  - Microsoft's agreement with Constellation Energy to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 (the undamaged reactor).
  - Google's agreement to purchase power from Kairos Power SMRs.
  - Amazon's investment in X-energy and acquisition of a nuclear-powered data center campus.

**Key Data Points:**
- IEA NZE 2050: nuclear capacity ~370 GW (2024) to ~800 GW (2050) (source: IEA NZE Roadmap)
- IPCC median pathway: nuclear roughly doubling by 2050 (source: IPCC AR6 WGIII)
- Nuclear capacity factor: ~92% in the U.S. — highest of any energy source (source: EIA)
- Microsoft-Constellation TMI restart: 835 MW, expected mid-2028 (source: Constellation Energy)
- Global data center electricity demand: projected to double by 2030 (source: IEA Electricity 2024)

## Section 5: Safety, Waste, and Public Perception
### The Barriers That Won't Go Away

**Content Notes:**

- **Safety record:** Nuclear power has the lowest death rate per TWh of any energy source, including wind and solar (source: Our World in Data, Markandya & Wilkinson). The three major accidents — Three Mile Island (1979), Chernobyl (1986), Fukushima (2011) — loom large in public consciousness but represent outlier events in a technology that has generated >17,000 reactor-years of commercial operation.
  - Chernobyl was a design type (RBMK) that would never be licensed in the West. Fukushima's lessons have driven significant safety upgrades globally. Modern designs incorporate passive safety systems that don't require operator action or external power.
- **Waste:** The nuclear waste challenge is real but often overstated in scale. The entire volume of spent nuclear fuel produced by all U.S. reactors over 60+ years of operation would fit on a single football field stacked about 10 yards high. Finland's Onkalo deep geological repository (the world's first) is under construction and expected to begin accepting waste in the mid-2020s — demonstrating that geological disposal is technically feasible. Sweden has also approved a deep repository.
  - Advanced fuel cycles (reprocessing, fast reactors) could dramatically reduce waste volume and radiotoxicity. France already reprocesses spent fuel, recovering 96% of usable material.
- **Public perception:** Nuclear remains one of the most polarizing energy technologies. Support has been increasing in recent years, driven by climate urgency and energy security concerns. Surveys in the U.S., UK, and EU show growing support, particularly among younger demographics who prioritize climate action.
- **Proliferation:** The link between civilian nuclear power and weapons proliferation is a legitimate concern, particularly for enrichment and reprocessing technologies. International safeguards (IAEA) and the Non-Proliferation Treaty provide guardrails, but the expansion of nuclear power to new countries requires careful attention to non-proliferation frameworks.

**Key Data Points:**
- Nuclear deaths per TWh: 0.03 (compared to coal: 24.6, gas: 2.8, wind: 0.04, solar: 0.05) (source: Our World in Data/Markandya & Wilkinson)
- 17,000+ reactor-years of commercial operation globally (source: IAEA)
- Finland Onkalo: world's first deep geological repository, accepting waste mid-2020s (source: Posiva)
- Total U.S. spent fuel: ~90,000 metric tons (60+ years of operation) (source: DOE)
- U.S. nuclear favorability: ~55-60% support, up from ~45% a decade ago (source: Gallup, Bisconti Research)

## Section 6: The Fusion Horizon
### The Long-Term Promise

**Content Notes:**

- Briefly reference the fusion content covered in Chapter 8 (NIF ignition, Commonwealth Fusion Systems, ITER) and place fusion in the nuclear context: fusion is the long-term dream — virtually limitless energy with no long-lived waste and no meltdown risk. But commercial fusion electricity is likely a decade or more away.
- **Key framing:** Fission is here now, proven, and necessary for near-term decarbonization. Fusion is the horizon technology for the second half of the century. The nuclear imperative is not to choose between them but to deploy fission aggressively while developing fusion for the future.
- **Private fusion investment:** Over $6 billion in private capital has flowed into fusion companies as of 2024, a dramatic acceleration driven by advances in high-temperature superconducting magnets and the urgency of climate change.

**Key Data Points:**
- Private fusion investment: >$6 billion cumulative as of 2024 (source: Fusion Industry Association)
- CFS SPARC tokamak: targeting net energy in late 2020s (source: CFS)
- ITER: first plasma early 2030s (source: ITER Organization)
