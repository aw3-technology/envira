## Opening Scene

**The White House, August 16, 2022 — Signing of the Inflation Reduction Act**

President Biden signed the Inflation Reduction Act in the State Dining Room with Senator Joe Manchin and Senator Chuck Schumer flanking him — a pairing that encapsulated the political improbability of the legislation. The IRA represented the largest climate investment in United States history: roughly $369 billion in energy and climate provisions, embedded within a broader fiscal package. It had survived eighteen months of negotiation that nearly killed it multiple times. Manchin, the conservative West Virginia Democrat who chaired the Senate Energy Committee and whose personal fortune derived from coal brokerage, had walked away from negotiations in July 2022, seemingly killing the bill. The deal that brought him back was brokered in secret over two weeks.

The IRA was not the comprehensive climate bill that advocates had originally envisioned. The Clean Electricity Performance Program, which would have mandated utility-sector decarbonization, had been stripped out at Manchin's insistence. Permitting reform for fossil fuel projects was part of the political bargain. But the bill's core mechanism — a system of tax credits, grants, and loan guarantees designed to make clean energy cheaper than fossil alternatives — represented a fundamental bet: that economic incentives could achieve what mandates could not.

*Content note: Open with the scene in the State Dining Room — the specific political dynamics, the strange-bedfellows quality of the moment. Then zoom out to the global policy landscape: 2022-2023 saw an unusual concentration of major climate policy milestones (IRA, EU Green Deal implementation, COP15 biodiversity framework, COP27 Loss and Damage fund). Frame the chapter's central question: unprecedented policy momentum, but is it enough, and is it being implemented fast enough?*

**Alternate opening:** COP15 in Montreal, December 19, 2022, when nearly 200 nations adopted the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, including the "30x30" target to protect 30% of land and sea by 2030. Chinese COP presidency, Canadian host — the complex multilateral dynamics.

## Section 1: What's Working: The Inflation Reduction Act

**Header: The Incentive Architecture**

### Content Notes

- The IRA's core climate mechanism: technology-neutral clean energy tax credits (Production Tax Credit and Investment Tax Credit), extended for 10 years to provide long-term investment certainty. This was a deliberate design choice — previous US clean energy tax credits had been short-term and subject to expiration cliffs, creating boom-bust cycles.
- Key provisions to detail:
  - Clean electricity tax credits (Section 45Y/48E): technology-neutral, available for any zero-emission electricity generation
  - Electric vehicle tax credits (Section 30D): up to $7,500 for new EVs, with domestic content requirements for battery minerals and manufacturing
  - Energy efficiency and electrification: Heat pump and efficiency rebates (Sections 25C, 25D), whole-home electrification (HOMES program)
  - Industrial decarbonization: 45Q tax credit for carbon capture enhanced to $85/ton for geologic storage, $180/ton for direct air capture
  - Environmental justice provisions: Energy Communities bonus credits, Low-Income Communities bonus, Justice40 initiative directing 40% of benefits to disadvantaged communities
  - Hydrogen production tax credit (Section 45V): up to $3/kg for clean hydrogen
  - Manufacturing incentives: Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit (Section 45X) for domestic production of solar cells, battery components, critical minerals processing
- Early results and deployment data (to be verified at time of writing):
  - Private sector investment announcements: Clean energy manufacturing investment announcements exceeded $270 billion in the 18 months following IRA passage (source: Clean Investment Monitor, Rhodium Group / MIT CEEPR)
  - Factory announcements concentrated in Republican-represented districts — a political dynamic that complicates repeal
  - Solar and wind deployment acceleration: EIA data on capacity additions
  - EV market share trajectory: from ~6% of new car sales in 2022 to higher figures
- Limitations and implementation challenges:
  - Permitting and interconnection bottlenecks: average wait time for grid interconnection exceeded 5 years (Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory)
  - Treasury Department rulemaking complexity: delays in finalizing guidance for hydrogen, EV, and other credits
  - Domestic content requirements creating supply chain friction
  - Political vulnerability: provisions can be modified or repealed without 60-vote threshold since they were passed through reconciliation

### Key Data Points

- **~$369 billion**: CBO estimated climate and energy spending in the IRA (source: Congressional Budget Office, August 2022; Goldman Sachs and others later estimated actual spending could reach $1.2 trillion due to uncapped tax credits)
- **$270+ billion**: Clean energy manufacturing investment announcements post-IRA (source: Clean Investment Monitor, Rhodium Group / MIT CEEPR, through early 2024)
- **10 years**: Duration of clean energy tax credit extensions — the longest commitment in US clean energy policy history
- **$85/ton**: Enhanced 45Q credit for geologic carbon storage; $180/ton for direct air capture
- **5+ years**: Average grid interconnection queue wait time (source: Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, 2023)

### Profiles

- **John Podesta** — Senior Advisor to the President for Clean Energy Innovation and Implementation (appointed 2023, succeeding Gina McCarthy as the administration's top climate official). Tasked with overseeing IRA implementation. Previously founded the Center for American Progress and served as White House Chief of Staff under Clinton.
- **Senator Joe Manchin** — The pivotal and contradictory figure. His coal brokerage business (Enersystems), his insistence on fossil fuel provisions, and yet his ultimate role in passing the most significant US climate legislation.
- **Jesse Jenkins** — Princeton ZERO Lab. His team's modeling of IRA emissions impacts (projecting US emissions reductions of ~40% below 2005 levels by 2030 under full implementation) became a key reference for evaluating the law's potential.

## Section 2: What's Working: The EU Green Deal

**Header: Regulation at Continental Scale**

### Content Notes

- The EU Green Deal as the most comprehensive regulatory framework for climate and environment attempted by any major economy. Announced by European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen in December 2019, with legislative implementation accelerating in 2021-2023.
- **European Climate Law** (June 2021): Enshrined the EU's commitment to climate neutrality by 2050 and a binding target of at least 55% net greenhouse gas reduction by 2030 (relative to 1990), into law.
- **Fit for 55 package**: A suite of interconnected legislative proposals to align EU climate, energy, transport, and taxation policy with the 55% target:
  - Revised EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS): Expanded to cover maritime shipping, strengthened for aviation, reduced free allowances for industry. Creation of a new ETS2 for buildings and road transport (starting 2027).
  - Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM): Phased in starting October 2023 (reporting phase), with full implementation from 2026. Applies to imports of carbon-intensive goods (cement, iron/steel, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen). Designed to prevent carbon leakage — where production shifts to jurisdictions with weaker climate policy.
  - Renewable Energy Directive (revised): Target of 42.5% renewables in final energy consumption by 2030 (up from previous 32%).
  - Energy Efficiency Directive (revised): Binding 11.7% reduction in final energy consumption by 2030.
  - End of internal combustion engine car sales by 2035 (with narrow exception for e-fuels).
- **EU Taxonomy**: Classification system defining which economic activities qualify as "environmentally sustainable" — intended to direct capital flows. Controversial inclusion of nuclear and natural gas under specific conditions.
- **Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)**: Mandatory sustainability reporting for ~50,000 companies operating in the EU, including non-EU companies with significant EU revenues. Much broader than previous disclosure requirements.
- **Nature Restoration Law** (June 2024): Binding targets to restore degraded ecosystems, covering at least 20% of EU land and sea areas by 2030.
- Limitations: Implementation varies dramatically across 27 member states. Political backlash — farmer protests in 2023-2024 across multiple EU countries, partly driven by Green Deal-related agricultural regulations. The European People's Party (center-right) campaigned on softening Green Deal provisions ahead of June 2024 European Parliament elections.

### Key Data Points

- **55%**: EU binding emissions reduction target for 2030 (vs. 1990 baseline) — enshrined in European Climate Law
- **EUR 100/ton**: EU ETS carbon price peaked above EUR 100 in February 2023 (source: ICE Endex, European Energy Exchange)
- **2035**: EU ban on new internal combustion engine car sales takes effect
- **~50,000 companies**: Subject to mandatory sustainability reporting under CSRD
- **CBAM**: First cross-border carbon tariff mechanism implemented by a major economy; reporting phase began October 2023

### Profiles

- **Frans Timmermans** — Executive Vice-President of the European Commission for the European Green Deal (2019-2023). The political architect who shepherded the Fit for 55 package through complex multi-stakeholder negotiations. Resigned in 2023 to lead the Dutch Labour/Green alliance in national elections.
- **Ursula von der Leyen** — European Commission President. Framed the Green Deal as both climate policy and industrial strategy — a "growth strategy" rather than a cost burden. Re-elected for a second term in 2024 with a slightly modified green agenda.
- **Fatih Birol** — IEA Executive Director. While not an EU figure, the IEA under Birol's leadership provided much of the analytical foundation for ambitious climate policy (the 2021 Net Zero Roadmap, annual World Energy Outlook projections showing renewable energy cost trajectories).

## Section 3: What's Failing: The Paris Gap

**Header: The Arithmetic of Ambition vs. Action**

### Content Notes

- The Paris Agreement's goal (Article 2): Hold global average temperature increase to "well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels" and pursue efforts to limit to 1.5C. The gap between this aspiration and the trajectory implied by current policies and pledges.
- **UNEP Emissions Gap Report** (annual, key edition: 2023): Current unconditional Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) would lead to approximately 2.5-2.9C of warming by 2100. Even conditional NDCs (dependent on international finance and support) would lead to approximately 2.1-2.4C. The emissions gap: to limit warming to 1.5C, global emissions need to fall 42% by 2030 relative to 2019 levels. Under current policies, emissions are projected to fall only ~2%.
- **The implementation gap within NDCs**: Many countries' actual policies do not align with their own stated pledges. Climate Action Tracker (by Climate Analytics and NewClimate Institute) rates country policies and finds most are "insufficient" or "highly insufficient."
- **Major emitters' trajectories**:
  - China: Pledged to peak emissions before 2030 and reach carbon neutrality by 2060. Coal power capacity additions continued in 2022-2023 (over 100 GW of new coal approved in 2022 alone, per Global Energy Monitor). Simultaneously the world's largest deployer of renewables.
  - India: Pledged net zero by 2070. Rapidly expanding both coal and renewable capacity. Updated NDC targets but categorized as "insufficient" by CAT.
  - United States: IRA significantly improved trajectory but still projected to fall short of Biden's 50-52% reduction target by 2030. Political uncertainty given potential for policy reversal.
  - Russia: Minimal climate ambition; conflict and sanctions further deprioritized climate action.
- The 1.5C threshold: IPCC AR6 found that 1.5C of warming would likely be reached in the early 2030s under all modeled scenarios. The question has shifted from "can we stay below 1.5C?" to "how far past 1.5C will we go, and can we eventually return to lower temperatures through drawdown?"
- The Global Stocktake: The first formal assessment of collective progress under the Paris Agreement was completed at COP28 in Dubai (December 2023). The outcome text acknowledged that the world is "not on track" to meet Paris goals and called for "transitioning away from fossil fuels" — the first time fossil fuels were explicitly mentioned in a COP decision text.

### Key Data Points

- **2.5-2.9 degrees C**: Projected warming under current unconditional NDCs (source: UNEP Emissions Gap Report 2023)
- **42%**: Required emissions reduction by 2030 (from 2019 levels) to align with 1.5C pathway (source: UNEP 2023)
- **~2%**: Projected emissions reduction by 2030 under current policies (source: UNEP 2023)
- **100+ GW**: New coal power capacity approved in China in 2022 (source: Global Energy Monitor / Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air)
- **COP28**: First COP decision text to mention "transitioning away from fossil fuels" (December 2023, UAE Consensus)

### Profiles

- **Climate Action Tracker** — Joint project of Climate Analytics and NewClimate Institute. Their country-level policy assessments and temperature projections have become the standard reference for evaluating whether national policies align with Paris targets.
- **Christiana Figueres** — Former UNFCCC Executive Secretary who oversaw the Paris Agreement negotiations. Her perspective on the gap between Paris ambition and implementation.
- **Sultan Al Jaber** — COP28 President and CEO of Abu Dhabi National Oil Company (ADNOC). His dual role embodied the tension at the heart of climate governance. The controversy over his appointment and the eventual "UAE Consensus" outcome.

## Section 4: The Carbon Pricing Landscape

**Header: Putting a Price on Pollution**

### Content Notes

- The economic theory: carbon pricing internalizes the externality of greenhouse gas emissions, making polluters pay for climate damage and creating incentives for emissions reductions. Two main mechanisms: carbon taxes (price certainty, quantity uncertain) and emissions trading systems / cap-and-trade (quantity certainty, price uncertain).
- **The EU ETS as model**: Launched in 2005, the world's first and largest carbon market. Covers ~40% of EU emissions. After years of low prices due to oversupply (EUR 5-8/ton through 2017), reform via the Market Stability Reserve tightened supply and prices rose to EUR 80-100+ by 2023. Demonstrates both the potential and the difficulty of carbon pricing — it took nearly two decades of reform to achieve meaningful price signals.
- **Global landscape**: World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard data: 73 carbon pricing initiatives in operation or scheduled globally as of 2024, covering approximately 23% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Key systems:
  - EU ETS (broadest, highest prices)
  - China's national ETS (launched 2021 — world's largest by covered emissions, but initially covering only the power sector with modest prices of ~$10/ton)
  - California cap-and-trade (linked with Quebec)
  - UK ETS (post-Brexit replacement for EU ETS participation)
  - South Korea ETS
  - Carbon taxes: Canada (federal backstop rising to CAD 170/ton by 2030), Sweden (highest rate, ~$130/ton), and others
- **Why carbon pricing alone is insufficient**: distributional concerns (regressive impact on low-income households without revenue recycling), political vulnerability (Australia repealed its carbon tax in 2014; Canada faced fierce political backlash), coverage gaps (most systems exempt agriculture, international shipping, and other sectors), and price levels too low to drive the required rate of transformation in most jurisdictions.
- **The border adjustment frontier**: EU CBAM as a potential model for other jurisdictions. The UK announced its own CBAM. Implications for trade law and WTO compatibility.
- **Revenue use**: Carbon pricing generates significant revenue. EU ETS revenues exceeded EUR 40 billion in 2022. How revenue is used — direct transfers to households, clean energy investment, deficit reduction — determines both the equity and political viability of carbon pricing.

### Key Data Points

- **73**: Carbon pricing initiatives globally (source: World Bank Carbon Pricing Dashboard, 2024)
- **23%**: Share of global GHG emissions covered by carbon pricing (source: World Bank, 2024)
- **EUR 100+/ton**: Peak EU ETS price (February 2023)
- **CAD 170/ton**: Canada's planned federal carbon price by 2030 (source: Government of Canada)
- **EUR 40+ billion**: EU ETS auction revenues in 2022 (source: European Commission)
- **~$10/ton**: China national ETS price range (2021-2023, source: Shanghai Environment and Energy Exchange)

### Profiles

- **William Nordhaus** — Yale economist, 2018 Nobel Prize in Economics for integrating climate change into long-run macroeconomic analysis. His work on the social cost of carbon laid the intellectual foundations for carbon pricing, though his models have been criticized for underestimating climate damages.
- **Mark Carney / GFANZ context** — His role in connecting carbon pricing to broader financial system reform.
- **World Bank Carbon Pricing Leadership Coalition** — The institutional infrastructure supporting carbon pricing expansion globally.

## Section 5: International Cooperation Challenges

**Header: The Hardest Collective Action Problem**

### Content Notes

- Climate change as the defining collective action problem: global commons, diffuse costs and benefits, intergenerational timescales, deep inequities between those who caused the problem and those most affected.
- **Loss and Damage fund**: Established at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh (November 2022) after decades of resistance from developed countries. The concept: vulnerable countries that have contributed least to emissions are suffering the most severe climate impacts and deserve financial support beyond adaptation funding. At COP28, initial pledges totaled ~$700 million — widely regarded as inadequate given estimated needs in the hundreds of billions annually.
- **The Global Stocktake** (COP28, December 2023): The Paris Agreement's built-in accountability mechanism. First completed cycle. The technical assessment confirmed the gap between pledges and required action. The political outcome: the "UAE Consensus" called for "transitioning away from fossil fuels in energy systems, in a just, orderly and equitable manner" — landmark language, but non-binding.
- **Accountability challenges**: The Paris Agreement is built on voluntary NDCs with no enforcement mechanism. Countries can revise NDCs downward (as the US effectively did when Trump withdrew from the agreement in 2017-2020). The "ratchet mechanism" (each NDC should be more ambitious than the last) depends on political will.
- **Climate finance**: Developed countries committed in 2009 to mobilize $100 billion/year by 2020 for developing countries. This target was not met on time (achieved in 2022, per OECD, two years late). The inadequacy of this figure: developing countries need trillions, not billions. The New Collective Quantified Goal (NCQG), the successor to the $100 billion target, was under negotiation for COP29 (Baku, November 2024).
- **Geopolitical complications**: US-China climate cooperation — the Sunnylands agreement (November 2023) restarted bilateral climate dialogue but remained fragile. Russia-Ukraine conflict's effects on energy security calculations. The rise of climate-skeptic politics in multiple countries.
- **The role of non-state actors**: When national governments falter, cities, states/provinces, and corporations have stepped in. The "We Are Still In" movement after US Paris withdrawal. C40 Cities network. The question of whether bottom-up action can compensate for inadequate national ambition.

### Key Data Points

- **$700 million**: Initial pledges to Loss and Damage fund at COP28 (source: UNFCCC, December 2023)
- **$100 billion/year**: Developed country climate finance commitment (made 2009, achieved 2022 — two years late) (source: OECD, 2023)
- **2022**: First year the $100 billion target was reportedly met (source: OECD climate finance tracking)
- **"Transitioning away from fossil fuels"**: First explicit mention of fossil fuels in a COP decision text (COP28, December 2023)
- **195 parties**: Signatories to the Paris Agreement

### Profiles

- **Simon Stiell** — UNFCCC Executive Secretary (appointed 2022). From Grenada — a small island developing state on the front lines of climate impacts. His leadership of COP28-29 processes.
- **Mia Mottley** — Prime Minister of Barbados. Her Bridgetown Initiative (2022) proposed fundamental reform of the international financial architecture to address climate and development financing, including reform of multilateral development banks and new financing mechanisms like SDR rechanneling and natural disaster clauses. Widely regarded as one of the most compelling voices for climate justice from the Global South.
- **John Kerry / later successors** — US Special Presidential Envoy for Climate (2021-2024). His role in re-engaging US climate diplomacy after the Trump withdrawal, the US-China bilateral process, and the limitations of diplomatic engagement without binding commitments.
