## Opening Scene

**IEA World Energy Investment Report — June 2023**

When the International Energy Agency published its 2023 World Energy Investment report, it contained a data point that would have been unthinkable a decade earlier: for the first time, global investment in clean energy was set to surpass investment in fossil fuels by a decisive margin. The IEA projected $1.8 trillion flowing into clean energy technologies in 2023 — solar, wind, electric vehicles, nuclear, heat pumps, grid infrastructure, batteries, and clean fuels — versus approximately $1 trillion into coal, oil, and gas supply. For every dollar going into fossil fuels, $1.70 was going into clean energy.

The crossover was driven by multiple converging forces: the cost declines that had made solar and wind the cheapest new electricity sources in most of the world, policy incentives from the IRA and EU Green Deal, energy security concerns accelerated by the Russia-Ukraine conflict, and industrial strategy as major economies competed to capture clean energy manufacturing. Solar investment alone was expected to surpass upstream oil investment for the first time in 2023.

But the headline number concealed a deeply uneven geography. The vast majority of clean energy investment was concentrated in China, the United States, and Europe. Emerging and developing economies outside China accounted for only about 15% of global clean energy investment despite being home to the majority of the world's population and facing the most severe climate impacts. The investment challenge was not just about total volume — it was about where the capital was flowing.

*Content note: Open with the IEA data release, then immediately complicate the narrative. The $1.8 trillion figure is historically significant but still falls far short of what is needed. Use this tension — progress is real but insufficient — to frame the entire chapter. Include Fatih Birol's quote context from the report launch.*

**Alternate opening:** Bill Gates' Breakthrough Energy Ventures announcing its second fund (BEV II, over $1 billion) in January 2022, or the broader climate tech investment peak of 2021-2022, followed by the correction of 2023 — illustrating the boom-and-bust dynamics of climate capital.

## Section 1: The Funding Gap

**Header: Four and a Half Trillion Dollars a Year**

### Content Notes

- **The IEA net-zero investment framework**: The IEA's Net Zero by 2050 roadmap (May 2021) and subsequent World Energy Outlook updates estimate that reaching net-zero emissions by 2050 requires annual clean energy investment to reach approximately $4.5 trillion by 2030 — roughly 2.5 times the 2023 level of $1.8 trillion.
- **Climate Policy Initiative's Global Landscape of Climate Finance**: CPI's biennial report (most recent: 2023, covering 2021/2022 data) found that total climate finance reached approximately $1.3 trillion annually in 2021/2022. This includes public and private, domestic and international flows. Key findings:
  - Mitigation dominates: ~95% of tracked climate finance goes to mitigation (clean energy, transport). Adaptation receives only ~5-7%.
  - Private sector provides the majority (~49% in 2021/22), but largely in developed markets with mature policy and financial frameworks.
  - Debt instruments dominate (~61%), followed by equity (~33%).
  - Developing countries receive a fraction of what is needed — CPI estimates a fivefold increase in climate finance flows to developing countries is necessary.
- **The composition gap**: Not all clean energy investment is equal. Mature technologies (solar PV, onshore wind, EVs) attract the vast majority of capital. Harder-to-abate sectors (heavy industry, shipping, aviation, agriculture) and earlier-stage technologies (long-duration energy storage, green hydrogen at scale, direct air capture, sustainable aviation fuel) remain drastically underfunded relative to need.
- **The emerging markets gap**: Explain why capital does not flow to where it is most needed. Currency risk, political/regulatory risk, lack of bankable project pipelines, higher cost of capital, insufficient concessional finance to de-risk private investment. IEA estimates that the cost of capital for renewable energy projects in developing countries is 2-3 times higher than in developed economies, making identical projects significantly more expensive.
- **The adaptation gap**: UNEP Adaptation Gap Report (2023) estimates adaptation costs in developing countries at $194-366 billion per year by 2030. Actual adaptation finance flows: approximately $21 billion per year. The gap is widening as climate impacts accelerate.

### Key Data Points

- **$4.5 trillion/year**: Clean energy investment needed by 2030 for net-zero pathway (source: IEA, Net Zero Roadmap 2023 update)
- **$1.8 trillion**: Global clean energy investment in 2023 (source: IEA, World Energy Investment 2023)
- **$1.3 trillion**: Total tracked climate finance in 2021/2022 (source: Climate Policy Initiative, Global Landscape of Climate Finance 2023)
- **~15%**: Share of global clean energy investment going to emerging/developing economies outside China (source: IEA 2023)
- **$194-366 billion/year**: Estimated adaptation costs in developing countries by 2030 (source: UNEP Adaptation Gap Report 2023)
- **~$21 billion/year**: Actual adaptation finance delivered to developing countries (source: UNEP 2023)
- **2-3x**: Higher cost of capital for renewables in developing vs. developed countries (source: IEA, various)

### Profiles

- **Fatih Birol** — IEA Executive Director. Under his leadership, the IEA shifted from an organization associated with fossil fuel supply security to one actively advocating for clean energy transition, producing landmark analyses (the Net Zero Roadmap, annual investment tracking) that reframed the energy investment narrative.
- **Barbara Buchner** — Global Managing Director, Climate Policy Initiative. CPI's Global Landscape of Climate Finance report is the most comprehensive tracking of climate-related financial flows globally.

## Section 2: The VC Landscape

**Header: Venture Capital and the Climate Tech Boom**

### Content Notes

- **The climate tech investment wave**: After the "cleantech 1.0" bust of 2008-2012 (where VCs lost billions on capital-intensive clean energy hardware companies — Solyndra being the most infamous example), climate tech investing went through a fallow period. It re-emerged around 2019-2020, rebranded from "cleantech" to "climate tech," with a broader scope (software, food, agriculture, built environment, carbon removal, circular economy) and more sophisticated investment theses.
- **Peak and correction**: PwC State of Climate Tech report and CTVC (Climate Tech VC) data tracked the boom. Climate tech VC peaked at approximately $40 billion in 2022 (some sources put the 2021-2022 peak slightly differently depending on methodology). The 2023 correction was significant — investment fell to approximately $30 billion or below, driven by rising interest rates, broader VC market downturn, and the burst of SPAC-driven public market speculation.
- **The "valley of death" problem**: Climate tech faces a distinctive challenge compared to software startups. Many climate solutions — batteries, green hydrogen, carbon capture, alternative proteins, new building materials — require physical infrastructure, manufacturing scale-up, and pilot deployments that cost tens to hundreds of millions of dollars. VC funding can support early R&D, but the gap between successful prototype and commercially viable first-of-a-kind facility is where many promising technologies die. This "missing middle" or "valley of death" is too large for VCs, too risky for project finance, and too early for infrastructure funds.
- **Sectoral distribution**: Where climate tech VC dollars actually go. Transport/mobility (especially EVs) captured the largest share. Energy generation and storage attracted significant funding. Carbon removal, food/agriculture, and the built environment received smaller shares despite enormous potential impact.
- **The role of corporate venture capital**: Oil and gas companies (Chevron Technology Ventures, bp ventures, Equinor Ventures), automakers, and utilities increasingly invested in climate tech, though often at levels dwarfed by their core business capital expenditures.
- **SPAC aftermath**: The 2020-2021 wave of climate tech SPACs (Quantumscape, Lucid, Nikola, and dozens of others) brought many companies public prematurely. Subsequent performance was poor — many fell 70-90% from their peaks. This dampened public market appetite for climate tech and complicated later-stage fundraising.

### Key Data Points

- **~$40 billion**: Approximate peak climate tech VC investment (2022) (source: PwC State of Climate Tech 2023; CTVC)
- **~$30 billion**: Climate tech VC in 2023, reflecting market correction (source: CTVC / PwC; exact figures vary by data provider)
- **2008-2012**: "Cleantech 1.0" bust — VCs lost an estimated $25+ billion (source: MIT Energy Initiative study; various)
- **Solyndra**: $535 million DOE loan guarantee; company filed for bankruptcy in 2011 — became a political symbol that set back clean energy investment for years
- **70-90%**: Peak-to-trough decline in many climate tech SPAC stocks (2021-2023)

### Profiles

- **Breakthrough Energy Ventures (Bill Gates, Carmichael Roberts)** — BEV I ($1B, 2016) and BEV II ($1B+, 2022). Gates' thesis: focus on technologies with potential to eliminate at least 500 million tons of CO2 equivalent per year. The patient capital model — 20-year fund life vs. typical 10-year VC fund. BEV portfolio includes QuantumScape (solid-state batteries), Form Energy (iron-air long-duration storage), Carbon Engineering (direct air capture, acquired by Occidental Petroleum in 2023), and others.
- **Lowercarbon Capital (Chris Sacca, Crystal Sacca)** — Founded 2020. Deployed over $800 million across 100+ climate tech investments. Known for an aggressive, high-volume approach — investing in carbon removal, alt-proteins, grid optimization, wildfire tech, and other categories. Sacca's public advocacy and media presence raised the profile of climate tech investing.
- **CTVC (Climate Tech VC)** — Research and media platform (founded by Sophie Purdom and others) tracking climate tech investment data. Their weekly newsletter and annual reports became standard references for the sector.

## Section 3: Public Finance and Development Banks

**Header: Concessional Capital and Green Bonds**

### Content Notes

- **The role of public finance in de-risking**: Private capital alone cannot close the climate investment gap, especially in developing countries and for early-stage or adaptation-related projects. Public finance — grants, concessional loans (below-market interest rates), guarantees, and equity investments from governments, development finance institutions (DFIs), and multilateral development banks (MDBs) — plays a critical role in de-risking investments and mobilizing private capital.
- **Multilateral development banks**: World Bank Group (IBRD, IFC, MIGA), regional development banks (Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, European Investment Bank, Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank). These institutions are under intense pressure to scale climate finance:
  - World Bank committed to align 100% of new financing with Paris Agreement goals by July 2023 and to increase climate finance to 35% of total lending.
  - The "MDB reform" agenda: the Bridgetown Initiative (PM Mia Mottley of Barbados) and G20 processes calling for MDBs to use their balance sheets more aggressively, expand lending capacity, and reform governance to better serve climate and development needs. The independent review of MDBs' Capital Adequacy Frameworks (CAF review, 2022) found that MDBs could sustainably increase lending by hundreds of billions without compromising their credit ratings.
  - European Investment Bank declared itself the "EU Climate Bank" and committed to end financing of fossil fuel energy projects after 2021.
- **Green bonds and sustainable debt**:
  - Green bond issuance grew from $37 billion in 2014 to $575 billion in 2023 (source: Climate Bonds Initiative). Cumulative green bond issuance surpassed $2.5 trillion by end of 2023.
  - Sustainability-linked bonds (SLBs) and social bonds add to the broader sustainable debt market, which exceeded $900 billion in annual issuance in 2023.
  - Sovereign green bonds: major issuances from Germany, France, UK, Italy, Japan. The EU itself issued green bonds to fund the NextGenerationEU recovery program.
  - Integrity concerns: the green bond market has faced questions about "greenwashing" — bonds financing projects with marginal environmental benefit. The EU Green Bond Standard (voluntary, finalized 2023) and Climate Bonds Initiative certification aim to strengthen credibility.
- **US Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund**: The IRA established a $27 billion fund (the "Green Bank") to mobilize private clean energy investment, particularly in disadvantaged communities. The EPA awarded funds in 2024 to a national green bank network.
- **Challenge**: Even with expansion, public finance alone is far from sufficient. The key question is whether public finance can effectively catalyze multiples of private capital — the "mobilization ratio."

### Key Data Points

- **$575 billion**: Green bond issuance in 2023 (source: Climate Bonds Initiative)
- **$2.5+ trillion**: Cumulative green bond issuance through 2023 (source: Climate Bonds Initiative)
- **$27 billion**: US Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund under the IRA (source: IRA text, EPA announcements)
- **35%**: World Bank's climate finance target as share of total lending
- **$900+ billion**: Total sustainable debt issuance in 2023 (green, social, sustainability, sustainability-linked bonds combined) (source: BloombergNEF)
- **Hundreds of billions**: Potential additional MDB lending capacity identified by the Capital Adequacy Framework review (2022)

### Profiles

- **Ajay Banga** — World Bank President (appointed June 2023, succeeding David Malpass). Former Mastercard CEO. Tasked with reforming the World Bank to better address climate change alongside poverty reduction — the "evolution roadmap." His appointment signaled a shift after Malpass faced criticism for initially declining to affirm climate science.
- **Mia Mottley (Bridgetown Initiative context)** — Her proposals for MDB reform, SDR rechanneling, and new financing mechanisms reframed the conversation about international climate finance architecture.
- **Climate Bonds Initiative (Sean Kidney)** — The organization that has defined green bond standards, tracked market growth, and advocated for integrity in sustainable debt markets.

## Section 4: The ESG Reckoning

**Header: From Hype to Substance**

### Content Notes

- **The ESG arc**: Environmental, Social, and Governance investing grew from a niche approach to a dominant narrative in finance between roughly 2018-2022. ESG-labeled funds attracted massive inflows, and virtually every major asset manager launched ESG products. Global sustainable fund assets reached approximately $2.8 trillion by end of 2021 (source: Morningstar).
- **The backlash**: Beginning in 2022-2023, a powerful political and financial backlash emerged:
  - **Political anti-ESG movement in the US**: Republican state attorneys general and legislators targeted ESG investing as politically motivated. Texas, Florida, and other states passed laws restricting state pension funds from considering ESG factors or doing business with firms that "boycott" fossil fuels. The "anti-woke capitalism" framing positioned ESG as ideological overreach.
  - **Financial skepticism**: Tariq Fancy, former BlackRock sustainable investing CIO, published a high-profile critique (2021) arguing that ESG investing as practiced was largely a marketing exercise that did not drive real-world emissions reductions — a "dangerous placebo." Academic research found limited evidence that ESG fund strategies outperformed or drove measurable environmental impact.
  - **DWS/Deutsche Bank case** (see Chapter 14): The first major regulatory enforcement action against ESG overstatement.
  - **Greenwashing concerns**: Many ESG funds held significant fossil fuel positions. The lack of standardized definitions meant "ESG" could mean almost anything.
- **The maturation**: Despite the backlash, serious climate-focused investing evolved:
  - Shift from broad ESG ratings (which aggregate environmental, social, and governance factors into a single score) toward specific climate metrics: Scope 1/2/3 emissions, transition plans, capital expenditure alignment, temperature scores.
  - The Glasgow Financial Alliance for Net Zero (GFANZ), launched at COP26 (November 2021), brought together financial institutions with over $130 trillion in assets committing to net-zero portfolios by 2050. By 2023, some members had departed and commitments were widely questioned, but the framework pushed disclosure and target-setting.
  - SEC climate disclosure rule (finalized March 2024, stayed pending litigation): Would require public companies to disclose Scope 1 and 2 emissions and climate-related risks. The EU's CSRD already mandated more comprehensive disclosure.
  - ISSB (International Sustainability Standards Board) issued its first global sustainability disclosure standards (IFRS S1 and S2) in June 2023, creating a baseline for climate-related financial disclosures.
- **What serious climate investors look for**: Real engagement vs. divestment debate. Transition finance — investing in high-emitting companies that have credible transition plans, rather than simply excluding them. The "financed emissions" framework for measuring portfolio climate impact.

### Key Data Points

- **$2.8 trillion**: Global sustainable fund assets at peak (end of 2021) (source: Morningstar)
- **$130+ trillion**: Assets represented by GFANZ members at launch (November 2021) (source: GFANZ; figure later disputed as overlapping and aspirational)
- **19 US states**: Had passed or proposed anti-ESG legislation by end of 2023 (source: various trackers)
- **June 2023**: ISSB issued IFRS S1 and S2, the first global baseline for sustainability disclosure
- **March 2024**: SEC finalized (then stayed) US climate disclosure rule

### Profiles

- **Larry Fink / BlackRock** — As CEO of the world's largest asset manager ($10+ trillion AUM), Fink's annual letters drove and then moderated the ESG narrative. His 2020 letter declared climate change a defining factor in companies' long-term prospects. By 2023, Fink said he had stopped using the term "ESG" because it had become "weaponized." BlackRock's actual voting record on shareholder climate resolutions declined sharply in 2023.
- **Tariq Fancy** — Former BlackRock CIO of Sustainable Investing. His 2021 public critique ("The Secret Diary of a Sustainable Investor") argued that ESG was a feel-good distraction from necessary regulation, sparking a significant debate about whether voluntary finance-sector commitments drive real climate outcomes.
- **GFANZ (Glasgow Financial Alliance for Net Zero)** — Chaired by Mark Carney and Michael Bloomberg. The alliance's struggles — departures, watered-down requirements, tension between ambition and implementation — illustrate the difficulty of translating financial sector pledges into real-economy emissions reductions.

## Section 5: Where Capital Is Most Needed

**Header: The Underfunded Frontiers**

### Content Notes

- **Adaptation finance**: The most critical and most neglected category. Climate impacts are already causing devastating losses in the most vulnerable countries. UNEP's Adaptation Gap Report (2023): adaptation finance needs in developing countries are $194-366 billion per year by 2030, but actual flows are approximately $21 billion per year — a gap that has widened over time. Only about 7% of total tracked climate finance goes to adaptation (source: CPI).
  - Types of adaptation investment needed: climate-resilient infrastructure, early warning systems, drought-resistant agriculture, coastal protection, water management, climate-resilient housing.
  - The challenge: adaptation investments often don't generate direct financial returns in the way that renewable energy projects do, making them harder to finance with private capital. Public and concessional finance must play a larger role.
- **Emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs)**: Home to the majority of the world's population, the fastest-growing emissions (in many cases from industrialization that is essential for development), and the most severe climate impacts. Yet they receive a fraction of global climate investment. Barriers:
  - High cost of capital (see Section 1)
  - Currency risk for international investors
  - Lack of standardized project development pipelines
  - Regulatory and political uncertainty
  - Insufficient technical capacity for project preparation
  - Debt distress limiting fiscal space for public climate investment (many vulnerable countries are already in or near debt distress)
- **Technology categories still underfunded**:
  - **Long-duration energy storage** (beyond lithium-ion's 4-hour sweet spot): iron-air (Form Energy), compressed air, gravitational, thermal storage. Essential for grids with very high renewable penetration. Still in pilot/first-commercial-project phase.
  - **Carbon dioxide removal (CDR)**: Direct air capture (Climeworks, Carbon Engineering/1PointFive), enhanced weathering, biochar, ocean-based approaches. Current removal capacity is a fraction of what is needed — IPCC scenarios consistent with 1.5-2C require billions of tons of CDR per year by mid-century. The market for carbon removal credits is nascent (Frontier, the advance market commitment led by Stripe, Alphabet, Shopify, and others, committed $925 million to permanent carbon removal purchases).
  - **Sustainable agriculture and food systems**: Agriculture accounts for ~25% of global emissions (including land use change) but receives a small fraction of climate tech investment. Alternatives to conventional animal agriculture (plant-based, cultivated meat, precision fermentation), soil carbon management, reduced-emission rice cultivation, and other solutions remain underfunded.
  - **Industrial decarbonization**: Cement, steel, chemicals, and other heavy industries are responsible for ~30% of global CO2 emissions. Solutions — green hydrogen for steel (H2 Green Steel, Hybrit), electrification of heat, carbon capture for process emissions, alternative chemistries for cement — require enormous capital expenditure for first-of-a-kind facilities.
  - **Climate-resilient infrastructure in developing countries**: Combines the adaptation and EMDE challenges. Essential for climate justice but the least attractive category for private capital.

### Key Data Points

- **7%**: Share of total tracked climate finance going to adaptation (source: CPI, Global Landscape of Climate Finance 2023)
- **$194-366 billion/year**: Adaptation finance needs in developing countries by 2030 (source: UNEP Adaptation Gap Report 2023)
- **~$21 billion/year**: Actual adaptation finance flows to developing countries (source: UNEP 2023)
- **$925 million**: Frontier advance market commitment for permanent carbon removal (source: Frontier, launched 2022)
- **~25%**: Agriculture's share of global GHG emissions including land use change (source: IPCC AR6)
- **~30%**: Heavy industry's share of global CO2 emissions (source: IEA)
- **Billions of tons/year**: CDR needed by mid-century under IPCC 1.5-2C scenarios (source: IPCC AR6 WGIII)

### Profiles

- **Frontier (Stripe, Alphabet, Shopify, Meta, McKinsey)** — The advance market commitment model for carbon removal. By guaranteeing purchases of permanent CDR at scale, Frontier aims to send demand signals that pull technologies down the cost curve. Portfolio includes Climeworks (DAC), Charm Industrial (bio-oil sequestration), CarbonCure (mineralization in concrete), and others.
- **Carmichael Roberts** — Vice Chair, Breakthrough Energy Ventures. Former entrepreneur with deep expertise in scaling hardware-intensive businesses. His investment thesis focuses on the specific challenge of bridging the valley of death for climate technologies that require physical infrastructure.
- **Climate Policy Initiative** — Their data and analysis on the climate finance gap, particularly the adaptation and EMDE shortfalls, have been central to international negotiations on the NCQG and MDB reform.
