## Opening Scene

**Held v. Montana — August 14, 2023**

Rikki Held was sixteen when she joined the lawsuit. By the time Judge Kathy Seeley delivered her ruling in the First Judicial District Court in Helena, Montana, Held was twenty-two and the lead plaintiff in the first constitutional climate trial in United States history. Sixteen young Montanans, ages five to twenty-two, had argued that Montana's policy of promoting fossil fuel development without considering climate impacts violated their constitutional right to a "clean and healthful environment" — a right explicitly guaranteed by Montana's 1972 constitution.

The state's defense was revealing: Montana's attorneys argued that the state's emissions were too small to matter in the context of global climate change. Judge Seeley rejected this reasoning. Her 103-page ruling found that Montana's greenhouse gas emissions are "a substantial factor" in climate change, that the state's MEPA exemption for fossil fuel permits was unconstitutional, and that the young plaintiffs had standing because they were already experiencing concrete harms — worsening wildfires, drought, and smoke that affected their health and livelihoods.

The ruling did not order Montana to reduce emissions directly. But it struck down a provision that had barred the state from considering climate impacts in energy project reviews. The state appealed, and the case moved to the Montana Supreme Court.

*Content note: Open with Rikki Held's personal story — ranching family, direct experience with wildfire and drought. Contrast her testimony with the state's expert witnesses, several of whom had documented ties to fossil fuel industry funding. This case crystallizes the chapter's central tension: the gap between what the industry knew, what it told the public, and what the courts are now beginning to adjudicate.*

**Alternate/supplementary opening:** The January 2024 filing by the European Court of Human Rights in KlimaSeniorinnen v. Switzerland, the first climate case decided by an international human rights court (ruling issued April 2024, finding Switzerland violated the European Convention on Human Rights).

## Section 1: The Documented Record

**Header: What They Knew and When They Knew It**

### Content Notes

- Begin with Exxon's internal climate research program, launched in 1977 under scientist James Black, who told Exxon's Management Committee that CO2 from fossil fuels would warm the planet. Black's 1978 internal presentation: "present thinking holds that man has a time window of five to ten years before the need for hard decisions regarding changes in energy strategies might become critical."
- Exxon funded sophisticated internal climate modeling through the early 1980s. Company scientists published peer-reviewed papers. The Esso tanker fleet was equipped with CO2 monitoring instruments. Internal documents show the company's models were remarkably accurate — a 2019 study by Supran, Rahmstorf, and Oreskes in *Science* (January 2023) found that Exxon's internal projections from the late 1970s and early 1980s predicted global warming with startling precision: 63-83% of their projections accurately forecast observed warming trends.
- The American Petroleum Institute's own research: The 1968 Robinson Report commissioned by API warned that CO2 levels could cause "significant temperature changes" by 2000. API's CO2 and Climate Task Force operated through the 1980s, producing internal reports on climate risk.
- Shell's internal documents: A 1988 confidential Shell report ("The Greenhouse Effect") acknowledged climate change as a serious risk and projected impacts consistent with current observations. A 1991 educational film produced by Shell, "Climate of Concern," accurately described climate risks decades before the company's public messaging shifted.
- The pivot: In the late 1980s and 1990s, these same companies transitioned from internal acknowledgment to public doubt. The formation of the Global Climate Coalition (1989-2002), an industry lobbying group that fought the Kyoto Protocol and publicly questioned climate science while its own scientific advisors acknowledged the IPCC's conclusions were well-established.

### Key Data Points

- **1977**: James Black's internal Exxon presentation on CO2 and climate (source: Inside Climate News, 2015; original documents released via litigation)
- **1982**: Exxon internal memo projecting CO2 doubling by ~2060 and ~3C warming (source: Exxon documents archived by Climate Investigations Center)
- **1988**: Shell's confidential "Greenhouse Effect" report (source: Dutch investigative journalists at De Correspondent, 2018)
- **2023**: Supran, Rahmstorf, and Oreskes, "Assessing ExxonMobil's Global Warming Projections," *Science* 379, no. 6628 (January 13, 2023) — quantitative analysis showing Exxon's internal models were 63-83% accurate
- **Global Climate Coalition**: Internal primer (1995) acknowledged the "icheld scientific basis" for climate concern while publicly disputing the science (source: Union of Concerned Scientists document archive)

### Profiles

- **Inside Climate News investigative team** — Neela Banerjee, Lisa Song, David Hasemyer. Their 2015 series "Exxon: The Road Not Taken" won the Pulitzer Prize for Public Service (as a finalist, with the prize going to the Associated Press; ICN won the Pulitzer in 2024 for their continued climate accountability reporting). The series was built on hundreds of internal Exxon documents.
- **Geoffrey Supran** — University of Miami (formerly Harvard). Lead researcher on quantifying the fossil fuel industry's climate disinformation. Published systematic analyses of ExxonMobil's public communications vs. internal knowledge.
- **Kert Davies** — Director, Climate Investigations Center. Has spent decades archiving fossil fuel industry documents and making them publicly accessible.

## Section 2: The Disinformation Machine

**Header: Manufacturing Doubt at Scale**

### Content Notes

- The playbook: draw direct line to tobacco industry's disinformation strategy. Oreskes and Conway's *Merchants of Doubt* (2010) documented how some of the same individuals and institutions moved from defending tobacco to defending fossil fuels. Frederick Seitz, former president of the National Academy of Sciences, worked for both R.J. Reynolds and ExxonMobil-funded groups.
- Lobbying expenditures: Use OpenSecrets (Center for Responsive Politics) data to quantify fossil fuel industry political spending. Oil, gas, and coal companies spent $124.4 million on federal lobbying in 2022 alone. Over the 2000-2023 period, cumulative lobbying expenditures exceeded $2 billion.
- The "personal carbon footprint" narrative: Trace to BP's 2004 "Beyond Petroleum" rebranding campaign, which introduced one of the first individual carbon footprint calculators. The framing shifted responsibility from systemic/corporate emissions to individual consumer choices. Mark Kaufman's *Mashable* investigation (2021) documented the campaign's origins and strategic intent.
- Front groups and think tanks: Document the network — the Heartland Institute, the Competitive Enterprise Institute, the George C. Marshall Institute, the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC). InfluenceMap's analysis of industry-funded climate obstruction.
- Social media disinformation: Center for Countering Digital Hate (CCDH) research showing fossil fuel interests amplifying climate denial and delay narratives on social platforms. The evolution from outright denial to "discourse of delay" — acknowledging climate change while arguing against specific solutions (Lamb et al., "Discourses of climate delay," *Global Sustainability*, 2020).
- Dark money: Drexel University sociologist Robert Brulle's research tracking $900+ million in dark money funding to climate denial organizations between 2003-2018.

### Key Data Points

- **$124.4 million**: Oil and gas federal lobbying spending in 2022 (source: OpenSecrets.org)
- **$2+ billion**: Cumulative fossil fuel federal lobbying 2000-2023 (source: OpenSecrets.org)
- **$900+ million**: Dark money to climate contrarian organizations, 2003-2018 (source: Robert Brulle, Drexel University, published in *Climatic Change*, 2014; updated estimates)
- **2004**: BP launches "carbon footprint" calculator as part of "Beyond Petroleum" campaign (source: Mark Kaufman, *Mashable*, 2021; BP corporate archives)
- **Heartland Institute**: Exposed internal documents (2012, "Denialgate") revealed strategies to undermine climate education in schools

### Profiles

- **Naomi Oreskes** — Harvard historian of science, co-author of *Merchants of Doubt*. Her work established the intellectual framework for understanding industry disinformation as a deliberate strategy.
- **Robert Brulle** — Drexel University sociologist (now at Brown University). Pioneered the quantitative study of climate denial funding networks.
- **InfluenceMap** — London-based think tank that tracks corporate climate lobbying. Their A-to-F grading system has become a standard reference for evaluating corporate climate claims against lobbying behavior.

## Section 3: The Divestment Movement

**Header: Follow the Money Out**

### Content Notes

- Origins: Bill McKibben's 2012 *Rolling Stone* article "Global Warming's Terrifying New Math" catalyzed the modern fossil fuel divestment movement. McKibben translated the carbon budget concept into a financial argument: fossil fuel reserves on company balance sheets exceeded the carbon budget by a factor of five, meaning most reserves were "unburnable" — creating a "carbon bubble."
- 350.org's campus campaigns modeled on the South African apartheid divestment movement of the 1980s. Early victories at small colleges (Unity College, Hampshire College), then escalation.
- Harvard University announced in September 2021 that its $42 billion endowment would divest from fossil fuels — a symbolic turning point given Harvard's status and the years of student/faculty activism that preceded the decision.
- Norway's Government Pension Fund Global (the world's largest sovereign wealth fund, ~$1.4 trillion AUM): systematic exclusion of companies based on coal production thresholds, upstream oil and gas exploration thresholds, and specific corporate conduct. The fund's Ethical Council process as a model.
- Total divestment commitments: Global Fossil Fuel Divestment Commitments Database (Stand.earth/350.org) tracked over 1,593 institutions with $40.5 trillion in total AUM that had made some form of divestment commitment as of 2023. Important nuance: "divestment" ranges from full exclusion to partial screening, and AUM figures represent total assets of committing institutions, not the amount actually divested.
- The financial argument matured: the concept of "stranded assets" (Carbon Tracker Initiative, Mark Carney's 2015 "Tragedy of the Horizon" speech at Lloyd's of London). The Bank of England and other central banks began incorporating climate risk into financial stability assessments.

### Key Data Points

- **$40.5 trillion**: Total AUM of institutions with some fossil fuel divestment commitment (source: Global Fossil Fuel Divestment Commitments Database, 2023)
- **1,593+**: Institutions with divestment commitments (source: Stand.earth / 350.org database)
- **$42 billion**: Harvard endowment at time of divestment announcement, September 2021
- **$1.4 trillion**: Norway's Government Pension Fund Global AUM (as of 2023)
- **2015**: Mark Carney's "Tragedy of the Horizon" speech introducing systemic climate financial risk framework

### Profiles

- **Bill McKibben** — Author, activist, co-founder of 350.org. His 2012 "Do the Math" tour translated carbon budget science into a financial divestment argument that reshaped the debate.
- **Mark Carney** — Former Governor of the Bank of England and Bank of Canada. His 2015 speech on climate financial risk brought the stranded assets concept into mainstream central banking discourse. Later served as UN Special Envoy on Climate Action and Finance.
- **Carbon Tracker Initiative** — Founded by Mark Campanale. Pioneered the "unburnable carbon" and "stranded assets" analytical frameworks that gave the divestment movement its financial logic.

## Section 4: Greenwashing Exposed

**Header: When the Green Paint Peels**

### Content Notes

- Define greenwashing in the corporate climate context: the gap between public sustainability claims and actual business practices, capital allocation, and emissions trajectories.
- **Shell/Milieudefensie case**: In May 2021, the Hague District Court ordered Royal Dutch Shell to cut its global CO2 emissions by 45% by 2030 (relative to 2019 levels), including Scope 3 emissions. The court found Shell's climate policy was insufficiently concrete. Shell appealed; the appeal was heard in 2024. This was the first time a court held a private company to the Paris Agreement.
- **TotalEnergies "carbon neutral" LNG**: TotalEnergies marketed liquefied natural gas cargoes as "carbon neutral" based on offsetting with forestry credits of questionable integrity. Investigations by Greenpeace and journalists challenged the claims. France's advertising regulatory authority (ARPP) and consumer protection groups pushed back on the marketing.
- **DWS/Deutsche Bank ESG fraud**: In 2022, German police raided Deutsche Bank and its asset management arm DWS over allegations of greenwashing — overstating the ESG credentials of investment products. Former DWS head of sustainability Desiree Fixler had blown the whistle in 2021, alleging that DWS's annual report overstated its ESG-managed assets. DWS settled with the SEC for $19 million in September 2023.
- **Broader pattern**: Advertising Standards Authority (UK) rulings against HSBC, Shell, and others for misleading green advertising. EU's proposed Green Claims Directive (2023) to standardize environmental marketing claims. The FTC's updated Green Guides process in the US.
- The offset problem: Investigations by *The Guardian*, *Die Zeit*, and SourceMaterial (January 2023) into Verra, the world's largest carbon credit certifier, found that over 90% of Verra's rainforest offset credits did not represent genuine carbon reductions. This undermined the offset-based net-zero claims of hundreds of companies.

### Key Data Points

- **May 2021**: Hague District Court orders Shell to cut emissions 45% by 2030 (source: Milieudefensie v. Shell, Rechtbank Den Haag, Case No. C/09/571932)
- **$19 million**: DWS SEC settlement for ESG misrepresentation, September 2023 (source: SEC enforcement action)
- **90%+**: Proportion of Verra rainforest offset credits found to not represent real reductions (source: *The Guardian* / *Die Zeit* / SourceMaterial investigation, January 2023)
- **2023**: EU proposes Green Claims Directive to combat corporate greenwashing
- **ClientEarth v. Shell**: In 2023, ClientEarth (a shareholder) sued Shell's board for failing to manage climate risk; dismissed by UK High Court but established new legal theory

### Profiles

- **Milieudefensie (Friends of the Earth Netherlands)** — Led the landmark Shell climate case with attorney Roger Cox. The legal strategy treated Shell's climate commitments as binding obligations.
- **Desiree Fixler** — Former DWS Head of Sustainability. Her whistleblowing on ESG overstatement triggered the first major regulatory enforcement action against greenwashing in asset management.
- **ClientEarth** — Environmental law organization that pioneered the use of corporate and securities law for climate accountability. Their shareholder derivative action against Shell directors broke new legal ground.

## Section 5: Corporate Transitions Underway

**Header: Pivots, Real and Imagined**

### Content Notes

- The distinction between genuine strategic transformation and incremental rebranding. Criteria for evaluating corporate climate transitions: capital allocation shifts, revenue mix changes, absolute emissions trajectories, executive compensation tied to climate targets, lobbying alignment.
- **Orsted (formerly DONG Energy)**: The most frequently cited case of genuine fossil-to-renewable transformation. Danish Oil and Natural Gas (DONG) was founded in 1972 as a state oil and gas company. In 2017, it sold its upstream oil and gas business, rebranded as Orsted, and became the world's largest offshore wind developer. By 2023, renewables generated over 90% of its energy. The company set a target of carbon neutrality by 2025 for its own operations and a 98% reduction in Scope 3 emissions by 2040. Important nuance: Orsted faced financial headwinds in 2023-2024 due to rising interest rates, supply chain costs, and the cancellation of US offshore wind projects.
- **Iberdrola**: Spanish utility that began shifting to renewables under CEO Ignacio Galan in the early 2000s. By 2023, renewables made up ~40% of installed capacity, with plans for 52 GW by 2025. Invested over EUR 40 billion in renewables over two decades. Exited coal entirely.
- **Contrast with companies where transition is primarily rhetorical**: BP's "Beyond Petroleum" rebranding in 2000, followed by continued expansion of oil and gas. In February 2023, BP scaled back its climate targets under CEO Bernard Looney (who later resigned over separate misconduct issues). Shell similarly softened targets after strong oil and gas profits.
- **The "energy transition" framing debate**: Some companies (e.g., Saudi Aramco, large IOCs) frame continued fossil fuel production as essential during the transition. The IEA's 2021 Net Zero by 2050 roadmap stated that no new oil and gas fields were needed — a finding contested by industry.
- The role of shareholder activism: Engine No. 1's successful proxy fight at ExxonMobil in May 2021, placing three climate-focused directors on the board with just 0.02% of shares. Follow-On Climate Action 100+ engagements.

### Key Data Points

- **Orsted**: Oil and gas revenues went from ~75% of total in 2006 to ~0% by 2023. Offshore wind capacity: 15.6 GW installed/under construction (2023). Stock performance since IPO in 2016.
- **Iberdrola**: EUR 47 billion invested in renewables and grids (2001-2023). Coal exit completed. 42 GW renewables installed capacity (2023).
- **Engine No. 1 proxy fight**: Three directors elected to ExxonMobil board, May 2021, with support from BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street (source: SEC proxy filings, corporate press releases)
- **IEA Net Zero by 2050**: No new oil and gas development needed beyond projects already committed in 2021 (source: IEA, May 2021)
- **BP backtrack**: February 2023, BP reduced its 2030 oil and gas production cut target from 40% to 25% (source: BP annual strategy update, corporate filings)

### Profiles

- **Orsted leadership** — CEO Mads Nipper (succeeded Henrik Poulsen, who oversaw the transformation). The institutional story of how a state-owned fossil fuel company reinvented itself, including the political dynamics in Denmark.
- **Engine No. 1** — The small activist hedge fund (Charlie Penner, Chris James) that won the ExxonMobil proxy fight. Demonstrated that climate-focused shareholder activism could succeed at the largest fossil fuel companies.
- **Richard Heede / Climate Accountability Institute** — Heede's "Carbon Majors" research (2014, updated) traced 71% of global industrial greenhouse gas emissions to just 100 companies. This research reframed the climate debate from diffuse responsibility to corporate accountability.
