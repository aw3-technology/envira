# Interlude: The Greenwashing Trial -- Shell and the Limits of Corporate Promises

On the morning of May 26, 2021, in a wood-paneled courtroom at the Palace of Justice in The Hague, a Dutch environmental attorney named Roger Cox rose to hear a ruling that would reverberate through corporate boardrooms on every continent. Seated behind him were representatives of Milieudefensie -- Friends of the Earth Netherlands -- and 17,379 individual co-plaintiffs who had joined the case, each one a Dutch citizen or resident who had lent their name to an argument that no court had previously accepted: that a private corporation could be legally compelled to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions in line with climate science.

The defendant was Royal Dutch Shell, at the time one of the five largest companies on Earth by revenue, an oil and gas major whose annual carbon dioxide emissions -- across the extraction, refining, and burning of its products -- exceeded those of many nation-states. Shell's headquarters sat barely three kilometers from the courtroom. The proximity was appropriate. The Netherlands is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries in Europe, with roughly a quarter of its land below sea level, and Dutch courts had already demonstrated a willingness to treat climate change as a justiciable question. In 2015, in a case brought by the Urgenda Foundation, a Dutch court had ordered the national government to cut emissions faster -- the first time any court had compelled a government to act on climate grounds. The Dutch Supreme Court upheld that ruling in 2019. Milieudefensie v. Shell was the logical next step: if a government could be held to account, why not the companies whose products drove the crisis?

Cox had spent years building the legal theory. He was the author of *Revolution Justified*, a book arguing that courts had both the authority and the obligation to hold private actors accountable for climate harm when legislatures failed to act. His argument in the Shell case rested not on environmental regulation -- Shell was not accused of breaking any existing statute -- but on the Dutch Civil Code's unwritten standard of care, a provision establishing a general duty to act with reasonable care toward others. Cox contended that the scientific evidence -- principally the reports of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, the remaining global carbon budget, and the temperature targets enshrined in the Paris Agreement -- established what reductions were necessary to prevent dangerous climate change. Shell's own internal documents, he argued, acknowledged the risks. A 1988 internal report, later made public, had described with notable precision the warming trajectory that greenhouse gas emissions would produce. The company's climate plans, however, were not commensurate with those risks. Shell had pledged to reduce the carbon intensity of its products -- the emissions per unit of energy sold -- but had not committed to reducing absolute emissions at the pace the science demanded. The distinction mattered enormously: a company could reduce its carbon intensity while increasing total production and total emissions. Cox asked the court to order Shell to reduce its net carbon dioxide emissions by 45 percent by 2030 relative to 2019 levels, across its entire value chain -- not merely the emissions from its own operations and purchased energy, but the far larger volume of emissions generated when customers burned the oil and gas Shell sold.

Shell's defense was vigorous. The company argued that a private corporation could not be held responsible for a problem as diffuse and collective as global climate change -- a crisis created by billions of individual and institutional actors over more than a century. Shell maintained that it was already investing in renewables, hydrogen, and low-carbon technologies, and that the energy transition must be driven by market forces and government regulation, not by judicial orders directed at individual companies. Shell's lawyers raised a practical objection that carried real weight: if Shell reduced its production, global demand for oil and gas would not diminish. Competitors -- many of them state-owned enterprises in countries with no comparable legal frameworks -- would simply fill the gap. The net effect on global emissions would be zero. The ruling, Shell contended, would punish a company for a problem it could not solve alone, while doing nothing to address the underlying demand. The company also challenged the legal basis itself, arguing that the duty of care had never been applied to require a private company to reduce its global greenhouse gas emissions, and that doing so would amount to judicial legislation -- courts making policy decisions that properly belonged to elected lawmakers.

Judge Larisa Alwin was not persuaded. Reading the ruling to the courtroom that morning, she found that Shell's climate policy was insufficiently concrete -- riddled with conditions and qualifications that rendered it insufficiently binding, even on Shell itself. The court held that Shell had an obligation under the unwritten standard of care in the Dutch Civil Code, informed by the scientific consensus, the Paris Agreement, and the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, to reduce its net carbon dioxide emissions by 45 percent by 2030 compared to 2019 levels. The obligation applied to the company's entire value chain: Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3. It applied globally, not merely to Shell's Dutch operations. It was, the court stated, a best-efforts obligation -- not a guarantee of a specific outcome, but a requirement that Shell adopt and execute a credible plan to achieve the reduction. The ruling was immediately enforceable.

It was the first time any court in the world had ordered a private company to align its emissions with the Paris Agreement.

Shell announced its intention to appeal within hours. The company subsequently moved its corporate headquarters from The Hague to London, a decision formally attributed to simplifying the dual share structure but widely interpreted as an effort to distance the company from the jurisdiction of Dutch courts. On November 12, 2024, the Hague Court of Appeal overturned the district court's ruling. The appeals court acknowledged that Shell owed a duty of care to help mitigate dangerous climate change, but held that a court could not impose specific emission reduction percentages on a single company, and that ordering Shell to reduce production would not lower global emissions because other suppliers would fill the demand. Milieudefensie announced in February 2025 that it would appeal to the Dutch Supreme Court. The reversal was a setback for corporate climate litigation, but it did not erase what the district court's decision had already set in motion.

The broader wave was in motion. According to the Grantham Research Institute on Climate Change and the Environment at the London School of Economics, the total number of climate-related court cases globally exceeded 2,600 by 2024, more than double the count from five years earlier.^1 Cases had been filed on every inhabited continent -- against governments, corporations, and financial regulators. In Montana, a group of young plaintiffs won the first constitutional climate trial in the United States in *Held v. Montana*, with a state court ruling in August 2023 that the government's failure to consider climate impacts in energy permitting decisions violated the state constitution's guarantee of a clean and healthful environment.^2 In Australia, a federal court found in 2021 that the country's environment minister owed a duty of care to young people when approving projects that would contribute to climate change.^3 In February 2026, the United States Supreme Court agreed to hear *City of Boulder v. Suncor Energy* and a consolidated ExxonMobil challenge, a case that will determine whether municipalities can sue fossil fuel companies for climate damages in state courts. The legal theories varied by jurisdiction. The direction did not.

What the Hague courtroom revealed, in the starkest possible terms, was the inadequacy of corporate promises. Shell had published sustainability reports. It had set carbon intensity targets. It had used the language of transition. The court examined those documents and found them insufficient -- not because the language was dishonest, but because the plans behind the language did not add up to emissions reductions consistent with the scientific consensus. The court read the fine print of a corporate climate strategy and held the company accountable for what the numbers actually showed.

The Arsenal chapters of this book surveyed the technologies fighting climate change -- clean energy, carbon removal, industrial decarbonization, ecosystem restoration, agricultural transformation. Those technologies are real, and many of them are advancing faster than even their advocates predicted a decade ago. This courtroom showed something different: the moment when the language of corporate sustainability met the language of law, and law demanded that sustainability mean something measurable. But neither technology nor law operates in a vacuum. The energy transition will be built by workers whose livelihoods are tied to the old economy. Its benefits and burdens will fall unevenly across communities separated by wealth, geography, and history. Its pace will be shaped not only by engineering curves and judicial rulings but by the choices of billions of people whose daily decisions -- what to buy, how to vote, what to demand of the institutions that govern their lives -- collectively determine the trajectory. Part IV turns to the human equation.
