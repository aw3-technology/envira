# Chapter 6: The Energy Renaissance
## The Sun, the Wind, and the Battery

**Part III: The Arsenal: Technologies Fighting Back**

---

There was no ribbon-cutting, no ceremony, no moment of public drama. The most consequential economic fact of the twenty-first century arrived as a line on a graph crossing another line on a graph.

In 2020, the International Renewable Energy Agency published its annual assessment of global electricity generation costs. The data showed that the weighted-average levelized cost of electricity from newly built utility-scale solar photovoltaic installations had fallen to $0.057 per kilowatt-hour, making solar the cheapest source of new electricity generation on Earth. It was cheaper than new coal. It was cheaper than new natural gas. In most of the world, it was cheaper than any alternative available, fossil or otherwise. A decade earlier, in 2010, that same figure had been $0.460 per kilowatt-hour. The decline was approximately 90 percent in just over ten years.

By 2023, updated IRENA data showed the number had fallen further still, to $0.044 per kilowatt-hour. In the sunniest regions of the world -- the Middle East, parts of North Africa, the American Southwest, Australia, and India -- solar auction bids had dropped below $0.02 per kilowatt-hour: electricity so cheap that the marginal cost approaches zero.

For decades, the central argument against renewable energy had been cost. Solar panels were beautiful in theory but prohibitively expensive in practice, a luxury for wealthy environmentalists, a technology that could never compete with the entrenched economics of coal, gas, and oil. That argument is now inverted. In roughly two-thirds of the world, building new solar or wind capacity is cheaper than building new fossil fuel generation. In a growing number of regions, it is cheaper to construct a new solar farm than to continue operating an existing coal plant. The economic logic that sustained the fossil fuel era has reversed.

This is not a projection. It is not a scenario dependent on optimistic assumptions or favorable policy conditions. It is a documented reality, measured in prices paid at auction, costs recorded in utility filings, and kilowatt-hours delivered to grids on every continent. The energy transition, once framed as a sacrifice, is now an investment with superior returns.

But if the economics have flipped, the deployment has not kept pace. If solar and wind are already the cheapest sources of new electricity, why does the world still burn fossil fuels for roughly 60 percent of its power? Because cost competitiveness alone does not automatically translate into deployment at the speed the climate crisis demands. Fossil fuel subsidies -- totaling approximately $7 trillion per year when externalities are included, according to a 2023 analysis by the International Monetary Fund -- continue to tilt the playing field. Grid infrastructure designed around centralized generation resists distributed renewables. Permitting processes delay projects for years. In the United States alone, the interconnection queue held more than 2,000 gigawatts of capacity waiting for grid connection as of 2023, according to Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory -- nearly double the country's total installed generation capacity. The energy transition is no longer primarily a technology problem or a cost problem. It is an infrastructure problem, a storage problem, and above all a deployment speed problem.

This chapter traces the trajectory of that transition -- from the solar cost curve that launched it, through the wind revolution expanding it, the storage technologies enabling it, the grid modernization constraining it, and the nuclear technologies supplementing it. The economics have already been decided. The question now is whether the infrastructure can be built fast enough.

## Solar's Exponential Curve

The solar industry's cost trajectory is not merely impressive. It is historically unprecedented -- the fastest sustained cost decline of any energy source in the history of industrialization, and it has followed a pattern so consistent that it has earned its own name.

In 1976, Richard Swanson, an electrical engineering professor at Stanford who would later found SunPower Corporation, observed that every time cumulative solar photovoltaic manufacturing capacity doubled, the cost per watt fell by approximately 20 percent. This observation, which became known as Swanson's law, has held with remarkable consistency for more than four decades. It is not a physical law. It is an empirical regularity driven by the iterative improvements that come with manufacturing scale: better cell architectures, thinner silicon wafers, improved metallization, automated production lines, larger factories amortizing fixed costs across higher volumes. Each doubling of installed capacity triggers the next round of cost reductions, which drives further deployment, which triggers the next doubling. The result is an exponential curve that has defied skeptics for nearly half a century.

The learning rate -- approximately 20 to 24 percent cost decline per doubling of cumulative capacity, as documented by IRENA and multiple academic studies -- has transformed solar from a niche technology for spacecraft and remote telecommunications into the dominant source of new electricity generation worldwide. The IEA estimated that roughly 380 gigawatts of solar photovoltaic capacity were added globally in 2023 alone, out of a record total of approximately 510 gigawatts of renewable additions. To put 380 gigawatts in perspective: it is roughly equivalent to the entire installed electricity generation capacity of Germany and France combined, built in a single year.

The manufacturing engine behind this expansion is overwhelmingly Chinese. China produces more than 80 percent of the world's solar panels across the entire value chain -- polysilicon, wafers, cells, and finished modules. The scale of Chinese manufacturing dominance is difficult to overstate: companies like LONGi Green Energy, founded by Li Zhenguo in 2000, made strategic bets on monocrystalline silicon technology when the industry still favored cheaper multicrystalline alternatives. That bet proved prescient as efficiency gains made monocrystalline dominant, and LONGi's relentless cost reduction helped drive global solar prices to historic lows. LONGi is now the world's largest solar wafer and module manufacturer by shipments.

The concentration of manufacturing carries geopolitical implications. Western governments, recognizing the supply chain vulnerability, have launched efforts to build domestic production capacity. In the United States, this effort centers on the Inflation Reduction Act's production tax credits for domestically manufactured clean energy components. In Europe, the EU Solar Manufacturing Alliance pursues similar goals. But these efforts will take years to reach scale, and the world's near-term deployment depends on Chinese factories.

**First Solar** represents the most significant counterweight to Chinese dominance. Based in Tempe, Arizona, First Solar is the largest American solar panel manufacturer and one of the few non-Chinese companies with substantial manufacturing scale. Under CEO Mark Widmar, who has led the company since 2016, First Solar has navigated intense competition from Chinese manufacturers, tariff disputes, and the IRA-driven manufacturing boom. The company uses cadmium telluride thin-film technology rather than conventional crystalline silicon -- a fundamentally different approach with advantages in hot climates and low-light conditions, where crystalline silicon panels lose efficiency. First Solar has invested heavily in expanding its American manufacturing footprint, benefiting from IRA incentives that reward domestic production. The company's trajectory illustrates both the challenge and the strategic importance of maintaining manufacturing capability outside a single dominant supplier.

On the frontier of solar technology, perovskite tandem cells -- which layer a perovskite material on top of conventional silicon -- have achieved laboratory efficiencies exceeding 33 percent, according to the National Renewable Energy Laboratory's Best Research-Cell Efficiency Chart. Standard commercial silicon panels operate at 20 to 23 percent efficiency. Oxford PV, a UK-based company, has begun commercial production of perovskite-on-silicon tandem cells, promising a step-change in panel performance. If tandem cells can be manufactured reliably at scale -- and the durability and scaling challenges remain significant -- they could drive the next leg of the cost curve, wringing more electricity from every square meter of panel surface.

## Wind at Scale

Wind power's cost trajectory has been less dramatic than solar's but no less consequential. Onshore wind has matured into one of the cheapest electricity sources available, with levelized costs of $0.033 to $0.050 per kilowatt-hour in favorable locations, according to IRENA's 2023 cost data. The technology is proven, the economics are well understood, and deployment continues to accelerate across every continent.

The real revolution in wind is happening offshore, and it is happening at a scale that defies easy comprehension. The latest offshore wind turbines -- Vestas's V236-15.0 MW, Siemens Gamesa's SG 14-236 DD -- have rotor diameters exceeding 230 meters and capacities of 14 to 15 megawatts per turbine. Each one of these machines can power more than 10,000 homes. They are among the largest rotating structures ever built, and they are being deployed in arrays that stretch across kilometers of ocean.

No company embodies the offshore wind revolution more completely than **Orsted**, and no corporate transformation in the energy industry is more remarkable. In the mid-2000s, the company was called DONG Energy -- Danish Oil and Natural Gas -- and 85 percent of its energy production came from coal and gas. Under CEO Henrik Poulsen, who led the company from 2012 to 2020, the company divested its entire oil and gas business, sold its coal plants, and reoriented entirely around offshore wind. It renamed itself Orsted in 2017, after Hans Christian Orsted, the Danish physicist who discovered electromagnetism. Today, Orsted is the world's largest developer of offshore wind energy, with installed capacity exceeding 15 gigawatts globally. The transformation -- from a fossil fuel company to the global leader in a clean energy technology in approximately fifteen years -- stands as proof that even legacy energy companies can pivot, though it requires leadership willing to abandon the core business entirely.

Global offshore wind capacity reached approximately 75 gigawatts by the end of 2023, according to the Global Wind Energy Council. The buildout is concentrated in Europe -- the North Sea, particularly around the United Kingdom, Denmark, Germany, and the Netherlands -- but is expanding. Vineyard Wind, the first utility-scale offshore wind farm in the United States, an 806-megawatt installation off the coast of Massachusetts, began generating power in early 2024.

The next frontier is floating offshore wind. Conventional offshore turbines use fixed-bottom foundations limited to water depths of roughly 60 meters. Floating platforms -- spar buoys, semi-submersibles, tension-leg configurations -- can access much deeper waters where wind resources are stronger and more consistent. This opens vast new areas for development: the U.S. West Coast, deep waters off East Asia, the Mediterranean. Equinor's Hywind Scotland, the world's first commercial floating wind farm, has been operational since 2017, demonstrating the concept at 30 megawatts. Multiple larger floating projects are in development globally.

The offshore wind sector has also demonstrated vulnerability. Rising steel and component costs, supply chain bottlenecks, and interest rate increases in 2022 and 2023 led to cost inflation that forced several high-profile project cancellations and contract renegotiations, particularly in the U.S. Northeast. These setbacks are real and should not be minimized. They reflect the difficulty of building complex industrial infrastructure in a volatile economic environment. But they do not alter the fundamental trajectory: offshore wind is a proven, scalable technology with enormous resource potential, and the global pipeline of projects continues to grow.

## The Storage Revolution

Solar produces power when the sun shines. Wind generates electricity when the wind blows. The gap between when renewable energy is available and when it is needed is the central technical challenge of a grid powered by variable sources. Energy storage is the linchpin.

The cost curve for lithium-ion batteries has followed a trajectory nearly as dramatic as solar's. Battery pack costs have fallen from more than $1,100 per kilowatt-hour in 2010 to approximately $115 per kilowatt-hour as of 2024, according to BloombergNEF. Since the first commercial lithium-ion batteries appeared in 1991, costs have declined by roughly 97 percent. The decline has been driven by manufacturing scale -- concentrated heavily in China, South Korea, and Japan -- improved cell chemistries, and larger production formats. Lithium iron phosphate batteries, which are cheaper, longer-lasting, and free of cobalt and nickel, have gained significant market share, driven largely by Chinese manufacturers CATL and BYD.

Grid-scale battery storage deployments have accelerated dramatically. California alone has deployed multiple gigawatts of utility-scale lithium-ion storage, transforming the state's evening energy profile. Australia's Hornsdale Power Reserve -- originally dubbed the "Tesla Big Battery" -- was an early landmark that demonstrated how fast battery storage could respond to grid disturbances. China leads globally, with more than 30 gigawatts of new grid-scale storage installed in 2023 alone. The cumulative global total reached approximately 45 gigawatts by the end of that year, according to the IEA.

But lithium-ion batteries, for all their success, are optimized for short-duration storage -- typically four to eight hours. A fully renewable grid needs to handle longer gaps: multiday weather events where clouds linger and winds stall, the phenomenon Germans call *Dunkelflaute* -- the dark doldrums. This requires technologies that can store energy for days or weeks at costs far below what lithium-ion can achieve.

**Form Energy**, co-founded by MIT professor Yet-Ming Chiang, is developing what may be the most promising candidate: iron-air batteries. The chemistry is elegantly simple. The battery stores energy by converting iron to iron oxide -- rusting -- and releases it by reversing the process. Iron is one of the most abundant and cheapest materials on Earth. Form Energy's target is 100-hour duration storage at a system cost below $20 per kilowatt-hour -- an order of magnitude cheaper than lithium-ion at comparable durations. If achieved, iron-air storage could make multiday backup economically viable, enabling a grid that can ride through extended periods without sun or wind. According to Form Energy, the company is constructing its first commercial-scale manufacturing facility and has signed agreements with multiple utilities, though the technology remains in the early stages of commercial deployment.

Beyond iron-air, a diverse ecosystem of long-duration storage technologies is emerging: compressed air energy storage, liquid air systems, gravity-based storage using suspended weights, flow batteries using vanadium or zinc-bromine chemistry, and green hydrogen for seasonal storage. Pumped hydroelectric storage -- the oldest form of grid-scale storage, using reservoirs at different elevations -- still accounts for more than 90 percent of installed storage capacity worldwide and remains viable where geography permits. The storage revolution is not a single technology story. It is a portfolio, with different chemistries and approaches suited to different durations, scales, and applications.

## Grid Modernization

The electricity grid is the binding constraint on the energy transition. In country after country, the grid -- not the generation technology -- is the bottleneck that determines how fast clean energy can be deployed.

Most electricity grids were designed for a fundamentally different energy system: a small number of large, centralized, dispatchable power plants feeding electricity outward through a hierarchical transmission and distribution network. The renewable energy system is structurally different. It features millions of distributed generators -- rooftop solar panels, wind farms, battery systems -- producing variable output that must be balanced in real time across a network that was never engineered for bidirectional power flows.

The scale of the mismatch is starkest in the United States. As of 2023, more than 2,000 gigawatts of generation and storage capacity -- mostly solar, wind, and batteries -- sat waiting in interconnection queues, according to Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. That backlog is roughly double the country's entire existing generation fleet. The average wait time for a project to move through the queue exceeds five years. Building a utility-scale solar farm takes months. Connecting it to the grid can take years. The mismatch between how fast clean energy can be built and how slowly it can be connected is arguably the single biggest bottleneck in the American energy transition.

Solutions are emerging but deploying slowly. High-voltage direct current transmission lines, which lose less energy over long distances than conventional alternating current systems, are critical for connecting remote renewable generation to population centers. The SunZia project, according to developer Pattern Energy the largest clean energy infrastructure project in American history, is a 550-mile HVDC line connecting wind resources in New Mexico to markets in Arizona and California. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission has issued interconnection reform orders aimed at streamlining the queue, shifting from a serial review process to cluster studies that evaluate projects in batches.

Virtual power plants offer a complementary approach. By aggregating distributed energy resources -- rooftop solar, home batteries, electric vehicles, smart thermostats -- into coordinated fleets that can provide grid services, virtual power plants create flexibility without new transmission. Tesla's virtual power plant programs in South Australia, which connect more than 50,000 participating homes, demonstrate the concept at meaningful scale, dispatching stored solar energy during evening demand peaks.

## Nuclear's Role

Any honest assessment of deep decarbonization must reckon with nuclear power. The world's existing fleet of roughly 440 nuclear reactors supplies approximately 10 percent of global electricity and remains the largest single source of zero-carbon power in many countries, including the United States and France. Retiring these plants prematurely, as Germany did with its remaining reactors in 2023, often results in increased fossil fuel generation to fill the gap -- the opposite of the intended effect.

The challenge with nuclear is not the physics. It is the economics and the timelines. Large conventional reactor projects have been plagued by extraordinary cost overruns and construction delays. The Vogtle Units 3 and 4 in Georgia, the only new nuclear reactors completed in the United States in decades, came in years late and billions over budget. Hinkley Point C in the United Kingdom and the Olkiluoto 3 reactor in Finland followed similar trajectories. These experiences have made utilities and investors wary of committing capital to nuclear projects.

Small modular reactors represent the industry's attempt to break this pattern. By shrinking reactor designs to factory-manufacturable sizes, SMR developers aim to shift construction from one-off megaprojects to standardized, repeatable units. Kairos Power, based in Alameda, California, is developing a fluoride salt-cooled reactor and is among the companies furthest along in the U.S. regulatory process. NuScale Power received design certification from the Nuclear Regulatory Commission for its small modular design, though its first commercial project, planned for Idaho, was cancelled in late 2023 due to rising cost estimates -- a reminder that the economic challenges facing nuclear are not automatically solved by smaller designs.

On the far frontier sits fusion -- the process that powers the sun, promising virtually limitless energy from abundant fuels with no long-lived radioactive waste. On December 5, 2022, the National Ignition Facility at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory achieved a genuine milestone: for the first time, a fusion reaction produced more energy from the fusion process than the laser energy delivered to the fuel target. The result -- 3.15 megajoules out from 2.05 megajoules in -- was a moment of scientific triumph. It was also, in practical terms, vastly net-negative: the facility's lasers consumed roughly 300 megajoules of electricity to produce that 2.05-megajoule shot. The milestone confirmed the physics. It did not demonstrate a path to practical energy generation.

Private fusion investment has exceeded $7.1 billion cumulatively as of 2024, according to the Fusion Industry Association. Commonwealth Fusion Systems, an MIT spinout led by CEO Bob Mumgaard, is building the SPARC tokamak in Devens, Massachusetts, using high-temperature superconducting magnets that generate fields exceeding 20 Tesla -- potentially enabling compact, practical fusion devices. SPARC is expected to be completed in the late 2020s. Helion Energy, TAE Technologies, and General Fusion are pursuing alternative approaches. The influx of private capital and the maturation of high-temperature superconducting technology represent genuine reasons for renewed optimism. But honest framing demands acknowledging that commercial fusion electricity remains likely a decade or more away. Fusion will not contribute meaningfully to the 2030 climate targets and probably not to 2040 targets either. It is essential for the long-term future. It is not a near-term climate solution.

## Nervous System Connection

The energy transition cannot succeed without a nervous system. At its core, renewable energy's intermittency challenge is an information problem, and the grid of the future is as much a data network as an electrical one.

A grid powered by millions of distributed solar panels, wind turbines, and battery systems must balance supply and demand continuously across a network of staggering complexity. This requires the same kind of sensing, processing, and response infrastructure that Part II of this book described at planetary scale -- applied here to the electrical grid itself.

Artificial intelligence is already transforming grid management. DeepMind's collaboration with Google to optimize wind farm output, using machine learning to predict wind power production 36 hours in advance, increased the value of that wind energy by roughly 20 percent, according to Google's published results. Similar AI-driven forecasting systems are being deployed at grid scale, using the weather prediction models discussed in Chapter 4 -- systems like GraphCast and Pangu-Weather -- to produce more accurate renewable generation forecasts. Better weather forecasts mean better renewable energy forecasts, which reduce the need for fossil fuel backup generation held in reserve against uncertainty.

Smart meters, grid sensors, and networked devices create a real-time map of energy flows across the system, enabling rapid response to demand spikes, equipment failures, and shifts in renewable output. Virtual power plants aggregate thousands of distributed batteries and solar systems into coordinated fleets that respond to grid signals in milliseconds. The smart grid is, in the most literal sense, a nervous system for the energy transition: sensing conditions continuously, processing information in real time, and coordinating responses across millions of nodes. Without it, high penetrations of variable renewable energy would be unmanageable. With it, grids can operate reliably at renewable shares that would have been considered impossible a decade ago.

## Tensions and Headwinds

The technologies described in this chapter work. Their costs have fallen to levels that make them economically superior to fossil fuels in most applications. But between the technology that exists and the deployment the climate demands lies a landscape of obstacles that are structural, political, and material.

**Grid bottlenecks** are the most immediate constraint. The 2,000-plus gigawatts sitting in American interconnection queues represent projects that are financed, permitted, and ready to build -- but cannot connect to the grid. Building new high-voltage transmission lines in the United States takes an average of more than ten years, a timeline driven by permitting requirements, environmental reviews, and disputes over rights-of-way that cross multiple jurisdictions. Renewable projects that can be constructed in months wait years for a grid connection. Until transmission planning and permitting are reformed, the bottleneck will persist regardless of how cheap solar panels become.

**Supply chain concentration** creates a different category of risk. China's dominance across the solar, battery, and critical mineral supply chains -- more than 80 percent of solar manufacturing, dominant positions in lithium processing and battery production -- means that the world's energy transition currently depends heavily on a single country's industrial capacity. Trade tensions between the United States and China, including tariffs on Chinese solar panels and electric vehicles and EU anti-subsidy investigations, create a tension between two imperatives that are difficult to reconcile: deploying the cheapest clean energy as fast as possible to address the climate crisis, and reducing dependence on a concentrated supply chain that creates geopolitical vulnerability.

**Mining and material costs** present an environmental challenge within the environmental solution. The energy transition requires massive increases in extraction of lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earth elements. Mining carries its own ecological footprint: deforestation, water contamination, community displacement, and habitat destruction. Cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo has been linked to child labor and unsafe working conditions. Lithium extraction in South America's "lithium triangle" consumes vast quantities of water in arid regions. The transition from fossil fuels to clean energy must not solve one environmental crisis by creating another. Recycling, material substitution, and responsible sourcing standards are essential but remain underdeveloped relative to the scale of demand.

**Local opposition** -- often dismissed as NIMBYism but frequently rooted in legitimate concerns about landscape impacts, property values, and community sovereignty -- constrains wind, solar, and transmission projects in communities across the world. Offshore wind projects along the U.S. East Coast have faced opposition from fishing communities, coastal residents, and tourism-dependent businesses. Transmission lines crossing rural areas provoke resistance from landowners. Community benefit-sharing models, in which project revenues flow directly to host communities, have shown promise in Europe but remain poorly implemented in many jurisdictions.

**Fossil fuel subsidies** remain the largest structural distortion in global energy markets. The IMF's 2023 analysis, which included both direct fiscal subsidies and the unpriced costs of air pollution, climate damage, and other externalities, estimated total fossil fuel subsidies at approximately $7 trillion per year -- roughly 7 percent of global GDP. These subsidies make fossil fuels appear cheaper than they are and renewable energy appear more expensive by comparison. They are, in effect, a massive ongoing public investment in the perpetuation of the system that clean energy is trying to replace.

**Intermittency** remains a real engineering challenge, though it is often overstated by critics and understood poorly by the public. Solar and wind are variable, not unreliable -- their output fluctuates predictably with weather and season. The solutions are known: energy storage, grid interconnection across wide geographic areas to smooth local variations, demand flexibility, and dispatchable zero-carbon generation including nuclear and geothermal. These solutions require investment on a massive scale, but they are technically proven. No fundamental scientific breakthrough is needed. What is needed is the political will to build.

## A Foundation, Not a Finish Line

Return, for a moment, to that line on a graph. In 2010, solar electricity cost $0.460 per kilowatt-hour. By 2023, it cost $0.044. The decline is 90 percent, and the curve shows no sign of flattening. Wind power has followed a parallel path. Battery storage is on a similar trajectory, roughly a decade behind solar. The economic foundations of the fossil fuel era -- the assumption that burning carbon was the cheapest way to power civilization -- have been dismantled by the learning curves of manufactured energy technologies.

This matters not only for electricity. Cheap, abundant clean energy is the foundation for nearly everything else in the climate toolkit. It powers the direct air capture plants that pull carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, the subject of the next chapter. It enables the green hydrogen that can decarbonize steel and shipping. It runs the desalination systems that provide fresh water in drought-stricken regions. It charges the electric vehicles replacing internal combustion engines on roads worldwide. The energy renaissance is not an isolated victory. It is the enabling condition for the broader transformation.

But energy is only part of the problem. Even if the world electrified everything and powered it all with renewables tomorrow, the carbon dioxide already in the atmosphere would continue warming the planet for decades. The concentration stands above 424 parts per million and rising. To stabilize the climate, it is not enough to stop adding carbon. The world must also begin removing it. Chapter 7 turns to the technologies and approaches being developed to do exactly that -- pulling CO2 from thin air and locking it away -- and to the daunting gap between what those technologies can achieve today and what the atmosphere demands.
