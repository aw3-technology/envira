# Interlude: Dispatch from the Fire Line

**Format**: Dispatch
**Target word count**: ~1,500 words
**Placement**: After Part I

---

The smoke arrives before the fire does. That is the first thing you learn in the boreal. In Quebec's Jamessie region, in the first days of June 2023, the smoke came low and thick through stands of black spruce, blotting out the sun at midday and turning the air the color of weak tea. The people of Lebel-sur-Quevillon -- a mining and forestry town of roughly two thousand, eight hundred kilometers north of Montreal, reachable by a single highway -- knew what wildfire smoke meant. They had lived alongside fire their whole lives. But by the evening of June 2, the smoke was different. It was not drifting through from some distant burn. It was closing in.

The evacuation alert arrived at 9:56 p.m., hours after a first warning had been posted at five in the afternoon. By eleven that night, the town was nearly deserted. A long line of headlights crept south along Highway 113 toward Senneterre, ninety kilometers away, families loading what they could into trucks and sedans, dogs in back seats, children asleep against windows. The fire that prompted the evacuation was one of dozens burning across Quebec that night. Within days, it would be joined by a hundred more.

The 2023 Canadian wildfire season did not announce itself in a single dramatic conflagration. It arrived as an accumulation -- a slow-motion catastrophe that began in the western provinces in May and swept eastward through June and July, growing in scale until it exceeded every record in the country's history. By the time it was over, more than fifteen million hectares had burned, more than double the previous national record, and nearly seven times the annual average of the preceding four decades. The fires produced an estimated 647 teragrams of carbon -- comparable to the annual fossil fuel emissions of large industrialized nations, with only China, the United States, and India releasing more carbon per year. The season was not an anomaly. It was a consequence.

The conditions had been set months earlier. Canada experienced its warmest May-to-July period in over eighty years, breaking the previous national temperature record for that span by nearly a full degree Celsius. In northern regions, May temperatures ran ten to fifteen degrees above normal. The snowpack melted early. The boreal forest -- the vast northern belt of spruce, pine, and larch that stretches from Alaska to Newfoundland, the largest intact forest ecosystem on Earth -- dried out. The organic soils beneath the canopy, normally saturated with meltwater well into June, baked and cracked. Lightning found fuel everywhere it struck. And in late May and early June, it struck often.

By the first of June, Quebec's wildfire management agency, known as SOPFEU, declared that it had reached preparedness level five -- the highest designation, meaning that the situation had created extreme demands on provincial firefighting resources. SOPFEU was built to manage two to three dozen active fires at a time. On some days in early June, more than a hundred and fifty fires burned simultaneously across the province, more than a hundred of them classified as out of control. The agency was forced to triage. Fires threatening human lives and essential infrastructure received resources. The rest simply burned.

On June 6, Chibougamau -- the largest town in northern Quebec, home to roughly seventy-five hundred people -- received its evacuation order. Mayor Manon Cyr directed residents to drive to Roberval, two hundred and fifty kilometers to the south. The nearby Cree community of Ouje-Bougoumou evacuated as well, along with the Cree Nation of Mistissini and the town of Chapais, where a thousand residents left in a matter of hours. Across the province, by June 4, fourteen thousand people had already been displaced. By the end of the month, the number would swell far higher, and the evacuations would extend well beyond Quebec -- in August, the entire capital of the Northwest Territories, Yellowknife, would be emptied, twenty thousand people ordered out in what became one of the largest evacuations in Canadian history.

In the fire camps and along the lines, the work was grueling and unrelenting. Photographer Charles-Frederick Ouellet embedded with a SOPFEU crew in mid-June near Girardville, in the Saguenay-Lac-Saint-Jean region, and documented the conditions that firefighters endured: the heat, the exhaustion, the blackened moonscapes where forest had stood days before. His photographs -- stark, black-and-white images of figures moving through landscapes stripped of life -- would later win the North and Central American single photograph award at the World Press Photo contest. The images captured something that statistics could not: the physical reality of what it means when an ecosystem built to burn periodically begins to burn without precedent or pause.

The fires' reach extended far beyond the forest. On the morning of June 7, residents of New York City woke to a sky that had turned a deep, unsettling orange. Canadian wildfire smoke, steered south by a stationary low-pressure system centered near Prince Edward Island, had descended on the most densely populated corridor in North America. The Air Quality Index in parts of Manhattan reached 484 by late afternoon -- just short of the scale's maximum of 500, and anything above 301 classified as hazardous for all people, not just the vulnerable. The daily average concentration of PM2.5 -- the fine particulate matter that penetrates deep into human lungs -- hit 117 micrograms per cubic meter, more than eight times the World Health Organization's guideline limit. Visibility in Central Park dropped below one mile. Schools closed. Flights were grounded. Asthma-related emergency room visits in New York City hospitals surged 81 percent between June 6 and June 7. For a few hours, the city had the worst air quality of any major city on the planet.

The orange light that filtered through apartment windows on June 7 carried an ancient message in an unfamiliar package. Fire has shaped the boreal forest for millennia. Indigenous peoples across northern Canada managed landscapes with fire for thousands of years. But the 2023 season was not a natural rhythm. Research published in Nature Communications found that anthropogenic climate change made the extreme intensity of the season at least twice as likely as it would have been under preindustrial conditions, and the persistence of those conditions at least seven times more likely. The boreal zone is warming two to three times faster than the global average, compressing the intervals between fire seasons, extending the conditions under which fires ignite and spread, and threatening to flip the northern forests from carbon sink to carbon source. In 2023, that flip happened. Canada's boreal forest, which had long absorbed more carbon than it released, became a net emitter -- the fires releasing more carbon in a single season than the forest could absorb.

The international response to the crisis underscored its scale. By the end of the season, more than five thousand firefighting personnel from ten countries had traveled to Canada. South Africa sent teams from its Working on Fire programme. France, Spain, and Portugal dispatched crews through the European Union's civil protection mechanism. Firefighters arrived from Australia, New Zealand, Costa Rica, Chile, Mexico, and South Korea. The United States alone sent 2,774 wildland firefighters across multiple provinces. It was the largest international firefighting mobilization in Canadian history. And it was not enough. Many fires simply burned until the weather changed or the fuel ran out.

In Lebel-sur-Quevillon, the residents returned home after the first evacuation, only to be evacuated again on June 22 when the fires advanced a second time. The town's two thousand inhabitants loaded their vehicles and drove south once more, along the same highway, past the same burned shoulders of road, toward the same shelters in Senneterre. Some would do this drill a third time before the season was done.

And yet, even in the smoke, something was happening that would have been unimaginable a generation earlier. As the fires raged and the evacuations unfolded, data was flowing. NASA's Fire Information for Resource Management System tracked thermal anomalies from orbit in near real-time, plotting active fire detections from the MODIS and VIIRS instruments aboard the Terra, Aqua, and Suomi NPP satellites. Air quality sensors across the eastern seaboard transmitted PM2.5 readings block by block. The European Space Agency's Sentinel constellation and private imaging companies like Planet Labs captured the fire perimeters at resolutions fine enough to trace individual burn fronts. The Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service tracked the smoke plume's trajectory, predicted its dispersion hours before it arrived, and quantified the season's total carbon emissions with a precision that would have been fantasy twenty years prior. Atmospheric scientists at NASA's Goddard Institute used satellite observations of carbon monoxide to trace the chemistry of the plume as it crossed international borders.

None of this monitoring stopped the fires. None of it cooled the air in Manhattan or shortened the evacuation line on Highway 113. But it did something that mattered: it made the invisible visible. The connections between a warming boreal forest in Quebec and a public health emergency in New York, between a local drought and a global carbon pulse, between a thousand individual fires and a planetary system under stress -- these connections were not inferred or theorized. They were measured. Tracked. Mapped in real time by an infrastructure of satellites, sensors, and algorithms that is still, even now, being built.

That infrastructure -- Earth's emerging nervous system, the planetary web of observation and computation that is learning to see the crisis as it unfolds -- is the subject of Part II.
